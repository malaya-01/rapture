## I — Cassian

He reached the interchange at noon, which was when the light through the fractures was at its highest — not bright, not the kind of bright that required sunglasses, but the best light available in the new world's palette, and the best light was when you moved if you had to move through open ground.

The interchange had been the junction of three major routes before the Rapture. Now it was something else: a topography of human decision-making, legible if you knew how to read it. He had been reading it for twenty minutes from the elevated access ramp that the collapse had left intact, and what he read was this:

Many people had stopped here. Not all at once — in waves, over days. The evidence was layered: first the debris of the first day's panic, cars still locked and abandoned with personal items visible through windows, the geometry of vehicles that had reached this point and found it blocked and had been left rather than turned around, which meant their drivers had continued on foot. Second layer: the evidence of shelter-use, the temporary occupation of the understructure — a fire circle, careful with the ventilation, someone who knew not to suffocate in an enclosed space; blanket-weight depressions in the dust; food packaging organized into a pile rather than scattered. Third layer: the most recent, only days old, the residue of a group that had moved through faster than it had stopped, the evidence of a decision made collectively rather than individually.

He wrote in his notebook: *Interchange — Junction of 3 routes. High traffic, multi-day occupation. Most recent: 3-4 days ago, group transit, eastward direction (litter distribution). Note: useful waypoint. Water source nearby (cistern under east pylon — wet concrete, access hatch).*

He crouched at the fire circle and held his hand above the ash.

Cold.

Whoever had been here last had been here at least two nights ago.

He moved to the east pylon and found the cistern. He filled both water bottles and purified them with the tablets he had taken from the warehouse pharmacy, because the discipline of clean water was the discipline that kept people functional when everything else cost more than they had.

He sat in the shade of the pylon with his water and his notebook and the city map, and he noted the interchange on it with a square rather than a circle — the square meant *waypoint, water, shelter*, different from the circle which meant *defensible, long-term potential*.

He was on the eastern edge of Ashford now, and the map ended here in the sense that the territory east of the interchange was territory he had not yet been in, marked only with the roads that existed and the landmarks he had inferred from his building's rooftop view. He had been planning this transit for four days, waiting until he had the supplies to extend his range, waiting until he understood enough of the city behind him to leave it with the specificity of intention rather than the vagueness of flight.

He left the interchange from the eastern approach.

Where the concrete ended, a tire track crossed his path — two parallel lines in the dust, recent, leading north. Someone with a vehicle, moving north from the interchange within the past day.

He crouched and studied the tracks. Four-wheel vehicle, not heavy. Moving at a pace that left controlled tread marks rather than the churned evidence of speed or stress. Someone who knew what they were doing.

He stood and looked north.

Nobody visible. The track disappeared around a vegetation break three hundred meters away.

Someone alive, with a vehicle, going north.

He made a note: *tire tracks, interchange exit, northward, recent.* He turned east.

He would not know, for weeks, whose tracks they were.

## II — Adrian

He came through the interchange in the early afternoon, two hours after Cassian had left it by the eastern approach.

He knew it was a waypoint — had known it from the road, from the way the road's damage pattern pointed toward it as a location where decisions had been made. He had been making similar assessments since day one, reading infrastructure damage the way he read gym floors before a class: where had people been, what had they done there, what did the space tell him about the choices that had been made in it.

What the interchange told him: occupied recently, organized briefly, departed east. The fire circle was careful enough to be someone's work rather than accident. The cistern access hatch was closed from the outside, which meant the most recent user had closed it behind them — the habit of someone who expected others to come after and wanted to leave the resource intact. He approved of this.

He noted the tire tracks at the eastern exit. Northward. He did not take the northern direction; his evidence pointed east and slightly south, toward the larger congregations. He moved east.

He was alone at the interchange.

He was almost never alone anymore, which he was learning to experience as a resource. The eighteen hours since leaving Dav at the gymnasium had been the first extended solo since day three, when Selene had been at his side and he at hers, and the solitude had the quality it had had in the early days: clarifying. He was better at thinking about where to go when he did not have to think about the people coming with him.

He thought: *East. Larger congregations. Selene is organized and she will make herself visible where the largest number of people can see her. Largest congregation east of here: approximately twelve kilometers.*

He moved.

At the eastern exit of the interchange he found something Cassian had not noted in the margins of any record — because it had arrived after Cassian left and before Adrian arrived — a scrap of paper wedged into a crack in the concrete pylon at shoulder height.

He read it.

A name — *M. Chen*, which meant nothing to him. A date two days ago. A direction: east, then south at the water tower, ask for the school with the green doors.

Someone had left directions for someone who was looking for them.

He took the paper. Filed it under *evidence of communication networks forming* rather than *useful to me specifically*. Kept moving east.

He was twelve minutes behind Cassian.

He would not know this for months.

## III — Rowan

He passed through the interchange in the vehicle at mid-afternoon, which was the wrong time for open road transit and the only available time given when the scouting run had started.

Denis was driving. Rowan was in the passenger seat with the radio handset and the emergency channel running at low volume, because they were three kilometers from the barn and the barn's signal strength could still be reached from here if something went wrong. In the back, covered with a tarp that provided security theater rather than actual concealment, were the pharmaceutical supplies Felicity had confirmed were at the clinic northeast of the interchange — confirmed because Felicity had been there and had evacuated all she could carry on day one and had left the rest behind, and the rest was worth the vehicle run.

The interchange was empty when they drove through.

Rowan scanned it from the passenger window with the habit of a man who had driven into emergency scenes for nineteen years: note exits, note occupancy, note hazard, note resource. He saw: evidence of recent occupation, eastern exit recently transited on foot (disturbance pattern in the loose material), access hatch to the cistern on the east pylon standing closed.

"Stop," he said.

Denis stopped.

Rowan got out and went to the cistern. Opened the hatch. Checked the water level. Tasted a small amount — clean, within acceptable range. He closed the hatch and stood for a moment looking east.

The road east was better than the roads he had been patrolling for sixteen days. Less debris, the fracture-related ground disturbance lighter here than it had been closer to the harbor. Someone had cleared part of the debris on the near section, the way people cleared debris when they were moving through regularly and wanted the path kept open.

He thought about Adrian, who was somewhere east of him and about whom he had been thinking with the specific managed quality he applied to worries he could not action. Adrian was east. The scouting runs were clearing the route incrementally. The route would be complete within a week if the patrols held and the creature activity on the north side of the road did not increase.

He got back in the vehicle.

"Clinic's another kilometer," Denis said.

"Go."

Denis drove. Rowan held the radio handset and did not transmit, because there was nothing to transmit yet. He looked at the eastern road through the windshield and thought about the people who had been at the interchange before him — the careful fire circle, the closed cistern hatch, the footsteps going east.

People who were trying to do things right, in the specific way of people who had thought about what *right* meant and had decided it included leaving resources intact for the people who came after.

He filed this as evidence that the barn was not alone in its approach.

That, he thought, was the best news he had had in a week.

## IV — Nora

She saw the interchange from the ridge.

Not directly — it was too far south and too low in elevation for direct sightline. But she had Ximena's map with the updated corrections, and the compass deviation she had been tracking since the ridge, and the knowledge of the route patterns she had been reading from elevation for sixteen days. The interchange was visible as a concept from the ridge: the place where three routes converged, and the convergence meant people, and people meant waypoints, and waypoints were the infrastructure she was beginning to map as systematically as she mapped the fractures.

Whisper had been south this morning.

Not south of her position — south of the ridge, lower altitude, flying the grassland margins at a height that suggested scouting rather than hunting. The hawk had been doing this for three days, ranging further from her position each day, returning with the kind of information that Nora was learning to read in its behavior: type of terrain covered (long flights meant open ground, short flights meant complex terrain), presence of hazard (posture on return, tight feathers versus loose), presence of large concentrations of life (the specific attention that large groups of anything produced in a predator's cognition, even a bonded one).

Today Whisper came back and sat on the tent apex and faced south with an attention that meant *large group in that direction, not a threat, but significant*.

She estimated: the interchange, probably. Movement through it.

She noted: *Whisper south-facing 15 min, attention posture, no alarm. Estimated: survivor group activity, interchange vicinity.*

She did not go south. Her route was northeast, toward the second fracture, toward the source the compass had been pointing at. She had a direction and a reason and two days of approach left if the ridge stayed passable.

But she sat for a moment and looked south, toward where the interchange was, and thought about the people she could not see from her elevation doing the things people did at waypoints — stopping, drinking water, reading the evidence of who had been there before them, deciding which direction to go next.

She thought about the file she had been building on the fracture geometry. If there was something coming from the northeastern source, if the geometric architecture in the fracture frames meant what it seemed to mean — a structured passage, a designed penetration rather than a random tear — then the people at the interchange were going to need to know about it.

But she did not yet know enough to tell them.

She needed the second fracture first.

She wrote: *Whisper south — survivor activity, interchange probably. Not investigated. Priority: NE source. Note: develop communication back to known groups. Find radio when possible.*

She turned northeast.

The ridge held her weight.

Below her, in the invisible space of four hours and twelve minutes, four people had passed through the same collapsed junction — had drunk from the same cistern, had read the same evidence of each other's passage, had moved in four different directions — without once sharing the same air.

The fractures above the city pulsed.

The ridge held.

Whisper circled once, high, reading the land below with eyes older than the relationship between hawks and people who followed them, and then turned northeast to follow the woman on the ridge, and the interchange was empty again, holding only the accumulated record of the decisions made in it, which would weather away in weeks and be gone before anyone arrived who could have read it.

But the tire tracks pointing north would last another day, and the water in the cistern would be there tomorrow, and the habit of closing the hatch behind you would travel east with Cassian and north with Rowan and onward with Adrian and would surface in the communities they built — small practice of consideration, the kind that could not be legislated, only inherited from the example of people who had done it when no one was watching.

Adrian, hours earlier, had left a strip of reflective tape on the guardrail where the off-ramp split—red on the left fork, nothing on the right—because someone in his group had asked how they would know which way he had gone if they fell behind. He had not explained that he expected them not to fall behind. He had simply done the thing that made the path legible.

Cassian, later still, would drink from the cistern and notice the hatch and close it without knowing why it mattered, only that leaving things open felt like inviting the wrong kind of attention.

Under the same sky.

Not together.

Not yet.

The interchange would fill again before nightfall with someone else's choices—another cistern drawn down, another set of tracks laid over the ones already fading—and the sky above would keep its pulse, indifferent to whether the people beneath it ever learned each other's names.
