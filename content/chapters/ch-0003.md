The bakery below had not opened in two days.

Adrian Hale noticed this because he had spent the last forty-eight hours learning which sounds meant the building was still holding: the creak of the third stair, the hiccup of the refrigerator in the apartment above, the particular complaint of the window frame that flexed whenever temperature changed. The silence where the morning bread-mixer should have been was not a sound he had counted on missing. He missed it.

Selene had finally fallen asleep around three in the morning, face pressed against his shoulder, one hand curled around the collar of his shirt. He had not woken her. He sat in the dark and watched the eastern sky.

It had been fracturing since Monday.

Not cleanly. Not like breaking glass. More the way ice cracked on a deep lake when you walked out too far—slow and exploratory, then all at once. The first line had appeared above the harbor, and since then the cracks had multiplied. At night they glowed. The color was wrong for electricity, too cold for fire. It looked like the sky had been drawn on by something that used light as ink.

He had called Rowan twice and gotten through once. The call had lasted forty seconds. In the years since their father had died, they had reduced the language of fraternal love to a compression of the practical: codes for *I'm okay* and *I'm scared* and *the family is what matters*, all transmitted through a brevity they had inherited from a man who said more with a hand on your shoulder than with a paragraph.

*Family first*, Adrian had typed, when the signal came back briefly at midnight. *Moving south. Find cover. Call when you're safe.*

No reply had come through. The network had died four hours later.

---

Selene stirred at six.

He watched her wake. He had been watching her wake for three years and it remained one of the things about her he could not adequately describe to himself—the particular inventory of her returning consciousness, the moment her eyes opened and found him, the second after that when she remembered what was outside and the softness in her face revised itself into something braver.

"Still there," she said.

"Still there."

She sat up. Her dark hair was sleep-tangled and she didn't touch it. She reached past him for the water bottle on the nightstand, drank, set it back with the precision of someone conserving everything, and then looked at the window.

"You should have woken me," she said.

"You needed sleep."

"We both needed sleep." She looked at the fractured light on the ceiling. "I'm making coffee."

"There's no power."

"I know. I'm making it on the camp stove."

He had found the camp stove the first night and staged it in the hallway closet—old habit from the years he'd spent on field assessments, the automatic preparedness that had once been professional and was now something else. Selene moved to the kitchen with the efficiency of someone who had accepted the situation and was choosing not to theatrize it. He heard the lighter catch, the small triumph of the burner ring, the complaint of the old kettle.

She came back with two mugs and sat beside him on the edge of the bed.

The coffee was imprecise—the camp stove ran hot and she'd miscounted the grounds—but she'd made it with the focused care she brought to tasks she couldn't afford to fail. He drank it. She leaned her shoulder against his and they both looked at the window.

"Your brother," she said. Not a question.

"I don't know."

"Elena would have gotten them out."

"Yes."

He believed this. Elena Hale was competent in the way that redefined what the word meant—not efficient, not merely trained, but fundamentally oriented toward what needed to happen next. If anyone could move Rowan when Rowan's instinct was to stand in the doorway and hold the line, it was Elena.

He believed it because he needed to. He let himself have that.

"We have to talk about today," Selene said.

"I know."

"You think we should move."

"I think the fractures are connecting." He had been watching them do exactly that, a slow-motion process like watching a windshield's crack travel: each line finding the next, the web growing. "I don't know what happens when the full perimeter closes. But I don't want to be on the seventh floor when it does."

Selene was quiet. She turned the mug in her hands. She was a translator by profession—three languages fluent, two more serviceable—and she had a scholar's instinct for precision, for knowing what a word actually meant before she used it. She did not say things she didn't mean and she didn't mean things she was still working out.

"If we go now," she said, "we might meet them in the street."

*Them.* The things on the street on Day 1 had not been people—not entirely. He had seen them from the window. Had watched the way they moved, recognized the shape of what had been human and the wrongness of what it had become. He had not named them. Naming them would have made them the story, and right now the story was Selene finishing her coffee and them both walking out of this building.

"We go fast and we go together," he said. "South route, the way Rowan said."

She nodded once. She didn't say *good* or *okay* or any of the words that would have sounded like agreement pretending to be resolution. She just nodded and set the mug on the nightstand and stood up and went to pack.

This was one of the things he loved about her, though love was not the word he would have chosen—not because it was wrong but because it was insufficient. She made decisions. When she made them she did not apologize for them. She had argued with him for two days about whether to wait—good arguments, careful arguments, the arguments of someone who understood that movement carried its own risk—and now she was packing.

He started packing too.

---

They worked in the pattern they had developed over three years of shared space: not by division of labor so much as by instinct, each of them having learned the other's architecture well enough to move without collision. She knew where he kept the first aid kit. He knew which of her documents she would not leave without, and had taken them from the desk drawer the night before and added them to his bag without telling her.

She found them when she opened the inner zipper.

She looked at him across the bed.

"You didn't sleep at all," she said.

"I slept some."

"You packed my documents at three in the morning and then decided that counted as sleeping."

"I also closed my eyes."

She looked at him for a long moment—that particular look, the one that was not quite warmth and not quite reproach, the one that had been coming at him since the first month they were together whenever he did something that was simultaneously the right thing and evidence that he was incapable of asking for help. Then she crossed the distance between them and pressed her forehead against his chest, just for a moment. His arms went around her. She was warm and solid and smelled like the same shampoo she'd always used, and he thought about how many ordinary mornings he had taken for granted and did not linger there.

"Okay," she said into his shirt. "South."

"South," he confirmed.

---

The window turned white.

Not sunlight. Not lightning. A brightness so clean it had edges—every fracture in the sky widened at once, and the light behind them, whatever existed on the other side of air, came through all at once. He was across the room with his arm around Selene's shoulders before the sound arrived.

The sound was a pressure, not a volume. He felt it in his sternum and in the bones behind his teeth. The coffee mug cracked on the counter—not shattered, just cracked, as if something had passed through the ceramic at a frequency it couldn't process. The window frame buckled inward and held.

Outside, Ashford screamed. Not one voice—the collective exhalation of a city that had been waiting two days to exhale and had just found the reason. It passed through the street like a second pressure wave, lower and longer than the first.

Selene was already at the glass.

He came to stand beside her because there was no useful alternative.

The sky above the harbor had fractured entirely. Not cracked—fractured, the way a mirror went when you hit its center: from one point of impact, a web of lines spreading to every edge. The light through those lines was not daylight. It had the color of something hotter and older, amber-gold-white, and where it fell on the buildings below it made their shadows point the wrong direction.

Then the dragon came through.

Adrian had been, in his better moments, a man who believed that language was adequate—that if you had words for enough things, you could survive them. He had built his career on the ability to describe, precisely, what had failed in a structure and what remained. He had been in collapsed buildings. He had calculated load tolerances for things that no longer had tolerance left.

He did not have words for the thing descending through the fractured sky.

He had *dragon*, and he had its inadequacy.

It was the color of heated iron—deeper red than rust, each scale slightly out of sync with its neighbors, so that the whole body seemed to breathe light the way banked coals breathed heat. Its wingspan was enough that when it turned it blocked the entire harbor view, replacing the water and skyline with a moving wall of red. When it opened its mouth—not to attack, simply to breathe, as naturally as any living thing breathed—a plume of furnace air preceded the fire by three full seconds. Adrian felt the heat increase inside the apartment even through the glass.

Its eyes were gold. He was too far to see them clearly, but there was a quality in the way the creature oriented—head tilting toward movement, toward sound, toward the small shapes on the street below—that read as intelligence. Not animal intelligence. Something older. Something that had looked at things like Ashford and considered them.

It passed over the harbor district.

The buildings it passed over caught fire. Not immediately. There was a delay, a beat in which the world seemed to need to accept what had happened, and then the glass in those buildings shattered outward and smoke rose in columns tinted amber by the fracture light. The sound the fire made at that scale was not a roar—it was deeper, more patient, the sound of something large consuming something small.

"Oh," Selene said.

Only that. Only the single syllable.

Adrian put his hand over her hand on the windowsill.

---

They were moving within four minutes.

He had made a list, the first night, of what leaving would require. Selene had made the same list separately, and they had compared them at midnight with the battery lantern between them, two versions of the same calculation shaped by different minds. Her list was better organized. His included the fire escape route and the service stairs he'd mapped on the first day without telling her because telling her would have made her afraid before she needed to be.

They went down the back stairs. The bakery at the base was unlocked—the owners had gone somewhere, he didn't know where, and he couldn't help them now. He took the bread from the counter that hadn't gone stale, left a note on the remaining money he had in his wallet, and knew the gesture was pointless. He did it anyway. It seemed important to behave as if the world still had manners, for as long as behavior remained a choice.

The alley behind the building was empty. Beyond it, the street was not.

He looked before he moved Selene forward. Habit, not heroism—the structural engineer's reflex of checking load-bearing walls before entry. What he saw: people running. Not in panic exactly—panic implied direction. This was disbursement, fluid moving through fractured material, finding every available gap simultaneously. A man in a business shirt carrying a child under each arm, moving with the concentrated calm of someone beyond adrenaline. A woman sitting on the curb with her hands in her lap and her eyes on the smoke column like she was waiting for it to resolve itself into an answer. A car that had rolled slowly into a bus shelter and stopped, door open, engine still running, driver gone.

A fire hydrant on the corner had been sheared at the base—sheared, not broken, cleanly, by something that had passed through the intersection at height. The water running skyward from the cut pipe had nowhere to go and fell back on itself, catching the fracture light as it descended.

Adrian took the south route. Away from the harbor, away from the smoke, toward the secondary roads that led to the outskirts where Rowan had told him to go. He kept Selene to the inside lane, near the buildings—not because the walls would help, but because the buildings gave her something to look at other than the sky.

They were four blocks out when they heard the second set of screams.

Not the same register as the first—lower, more organized, the sound of people who had already processed one impossible thing and were now being asked to process a worse one. Adrian stopped.

He should not have looked up.

He looked up.

The second shadow crossed the sky like a continent moving.

He could not measure it against anything in his catalog of knowable scale. The first dragon had been the size of the harbor district—an area he could walk in twenty minutes, a space his city contained. This shadow was larger. Not by degrees. The first dragon was to this thing what a dog was to a barn—same general category, different physics entirely. When it moved, it moved all the sky with it. The wingbeats were not audible as wingbeats; he felt them as pressure changes, two bass notes too low for the ear, resonating in his ribcage, in the walls of the buildings around them.

The fracture above opened wider to receive it.

Selene pressed her back against the wall beside him. Her shoulder found his chest, her breathing too fast, the measured breath of someone running the same calculation he was and arriving at the same answer.

He pressed his lips against the top of her hair. She did not pull away.

"Bigger," she said.

"Yes."

"How much bigger."

He watched the shadow complete its crossing and tried to find any answer that would help either of them.

"We keep moving," he said.

She pushed off the wall. He followed.

Behind them, through the sound of the city coming apart, through the fire and the water still falling from the severed hydrant in a spray the wind kept carrying east, the second descent continued—slow and enormous and unhurried, the way a continent moved toward a coast: so large it required no urgency, so certain of its arrival that the sky made room.

He took Selene's hand.

They ran.
