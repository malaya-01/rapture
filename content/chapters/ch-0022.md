He had promised himself he would not collect people.

Not collect—that was a bad word, not what he meant. He meant he would not acquire responsibilities he could not discharge. That was the distinction he had drawn in his mind somewhere around Day Eight when the third straggler had fallen into step behind him on the rubble street outside what had been a dental clinic, and he had understood, with a particular kind of exhausted clarity, that the decision was already made. People followed people who kept moving and didn't look panicked. He was one of those people, for reasons he had never asked for and didn't entirely understand.

Seven of them now.

Seven was too many. Seven was also too few to divide into watch rotations, too many to feed from what he had cached and supplemented by scavenging, too much of a signature—noise, smell, footprint—for a man trying to move quietly through blocks that had been claimed by things he could not fight alone.

He was aware he could have moved faster without them. He was aware that he hadn't tried.

The two boys were brothers, nine and twelve, separated from a convoy that had turned north on Day Fourteen and taken their parents with it. Whether the convoy had made it somewhere safe or had simply stopped moving, he didn't know. The boys were named Finn and Owen. Finn was the younger one, the one who asked questions without end, who had discovered that asking Adrian questions was a reliable way to stay close to the front of the group where it felt safest. Owen was older and had decided that the way to make himself useful was to be very quiet and watch what Adrian watched and imitate it as closely as possible. Adrian had noticed. He hadn't said anything. There was something respectful about it.

The woman was named Dara. She had been an ER nurse. Her hands shook now—not from injury but from the cumulative expenditure of the last three weeks, the kind of tremor that lived in muscle that had been asked too much for too long. She was still the most useful person in the group aside from Adrian himself, and she knew it, and it was keeping her together. He had seen this in rescue work: competence as anchor. As long as she had something to do she would not stop.

The other four were less distinct to him yet—an older couple from the far side of the interchange, a young woman who had not spoken more than a dozen words since he'd found her crouched in a doorway watching something invisible, and a man about Adrian's own age who had introduced himself as a plumber and then spent two days proving he was more useful than that title suggested. The plumber's name was Hector, and of all of them Hector was the one Adrian trusted most not to do something panicked in a crisis, because he had already tested that and Hector had been solid.

That was the word for it. Solid.

He was collecting solid people, apparently.

Selene was still ahead of him somewhere. Or behind him. That was the particular torment of the search: no communications, no landmarks that hadn't shifted or been destroyed, no network of information. He had a direction—the last route she had spoken of, the camp to the northeast where she had been taking shelter, the rumor from a woman at a barricade on Day Twelve who said she had seen a blonde woman matching Selene's description moving east on the highway spur. East. That narrowed it to half a continent.

Rowan. He thought of his brother with a frequency that had become its own kind of pain—a dull, persistent ache, the kind you stopped noticing until you breathed too deeply and it sharpened. Rowan had the kids. Elena had the medical supplies. They had moved before Adrian could backtrack, taken the family group north toward the shelter at the outskirts where the building codes meant thicker walls and a diesel generator that might still have fuel. Might. He had been telling himself might for three weeks and it remained the most useful word he had.

You moved on might. Certainty was not available in the current market.

The block they were approaching had been claimed.

He knew the signs now—had learned them in the brutal empirical way of someone who had made two or three nearly fatal mistakes and survived with the lesson driven in. The claiming signs varied by creature type, which was itself a piece of information he kept adding to, building a rough taxonomy in the back of his mind: scent marks in certain species, physical modifications to the environment in others, the strange silence that descended when an alpha predator was nearby versus the very different silence that meant a group predator had established territory and was waiting.

This block had the physical modification kind of claim. He stopped the group with a raised fist—Finn almost walked into Owen before catching the signal, but the older boy had already halted—and crouched to look.

The doors of three consecutive storefronts had been forced inward, not broken outward. Something had been building. The sills of the upper windows were scratched in parallel lines at consistent intervals. The street had debris cleared from a central path, not by human effort but by the wide, purposeful sweep of something large that had passed repeatedly. The debris edges were too clean for accident. A territory line.

"Alternate route," he said quietly.

He did not explain. He had found it was better not to explain when the explanation would require him to describe exactly how dangerous the alternative to his decision was. Finn would ask more questions. Owen would absorb the fear and carry it, and he was already carrying enough. He led them back around and through the cross-street, angling south of the claimed block, and they came out three blocks further west, which added twenty minutes to their route.

Twenty minutes was worth it.

They had been moving east for three days—east and slightly north, following the bearing he had decided on after consulting the one map he had and the memory of every route out of Ashford he had ever driven. The ruin belt thinned as you moved away from the city center, which was counterintuitive until you understood that the city center had been the densest concentration of people, and the densest concentration of people had been the loudest, most active attractor for newly emerged predators. The margins were quieter. Not safe—the quietness had its own dangers, the kind that came from a single large territory-holder rather than multiple competing ones—but navigable.

At the edges of the belt there were blocks that looked almost normal. That was another kind of dangerous: the human mind kept trying to pattern-match to the pre-Rapture world, kept insisting that a block with intact windows and an intact road was a block where normal things still applied. He had made his first nearly fatal mistake that way, on Day Nine, when a block that looked intact had turned out to be intact because something had moved through it and claimed the ground-floor spaces and simply not bothered to break the windows because it didn't need to.

He was more careful now about intact windows.

---

He stopped them for the night in a parking structure—second level, access to the ramp for multiple exit routes, no single point of failure. He had learned to think in those terms too: exit routes and chokepoints, what could be blocked and by what.

Dara checked Finn's foot, which had been bothering him since they'd crossed the collapsed overpass two days ago. Owen sat nearby, watching the medical procedure with the careful neutrality of a boy who had decided that watching injuries was something he needed to be able to do without flinching, and Adrian did not tell him to look away.

"Sprain, not break," Dara said, which was what she had said the previous two times she'd checked it.

"Still hurts," Finn said.

"Yes," Dara said. "Sprains do that."

Hector had found a fuel station on their approach and carried the container Adrian had designated for waste oil, which burned dirty but burned, and they had a small heat source in the corner of the second level's covered section, sheltered on three sides and monitored. Small fires. Low fires. Enough for warmth, not enough to announce them from three blocks out.

Adrian ate quickly and then went to the parapet and looked at the city.

This much of the ruin belt: the buildings still stood, mostly. Fire had taken some, collapse had taken others, but the city's bones were still there—that particular Ashford geometry of older brick and newer glass, the mix of eras that he had never found beautiful and now found nearly unbearable in its familiarity. He knew this city. He had run its streets for years, had driven its routes, had known which intersections got complicated at rush hour and which back alleys were useful shortcuts. It was a working knowledge, the kind that lived in the body.

He looked at what was left of it and tried to feel whatever he was supposed to feel, and mostly what he felt was the blunt arithmetic of the situation: there was somewhere ahead of him, probably, where Selene was still alive. There was somewhere else, probably north, where Rowan was still alive and managing whatever crisis had presented itself today. There was something wrong with the eastern sky that he had been watching for three days—not the fracture glow of the first week, which had dimmed somewhat, but something newer, something that sat lower on the horizon, steadier and stranger.

He was going toward it anyway. East was where Selene had gone. East was where the rumor had pointed. East it was.

You couldn't search effectively if you were afraid of the direction.

---

In the morning they moved again.

He had established a pattern: he scouted thirty minutes ahead of the group alone, came back, moved the group, stopped, scouted again. It was slower than moving continuously, but it meant he was the first person to encounter whatever was going to be encountered, which was the only arrangement he could morally defend. The others watched his returns for information—not what he said, but how he was moving when he came back. Fast was bad. Careful was neutral. Relaxed was something he hadn't managed yet, but he approximated it when the route was clear.

On the scouting pass of the third crossing he found the rift mark.

He had learned to identify rift marks too—places where mana pressure had been concentrated enough to leave a physical signature on the environment. They looked different from monster damage: the edges were sharp rather than torn, the surfaces smooth in the wrong way, as if matter had been displaced rather than broken. This one was on a wall—a section of brick that had been preserved in a gap between two collapsed facades, and on it, running from ground level to about shoulder height, a vertical line of scorched perfection, as if someone had laid a burning blade flat against the surface and drawn it upward.

No char. No heat scar. Just that line, and the faint metallic smell he had come to associate with the fractures themselves—copper and something older, something that had no name in his vocabulary yet.

He stood and looked at it for a moment and then kept moving.

There were things in this world now that he was not equipped to understand. He had made his peace with that. His job was not to understand them. His job was to navigate around them, keep the people behind him alive, and find the ones he had lost.

Understanding was for people with more time.

---

At the edge of the ruin belt, where the last suburban blocks dissolved into the wide flat land of the outer margin, the group stopped to rest and Finn asked where they were going and Adrian said east and Finn said why east and Adrian said because that was where the people he was looking for had gone.

"My parents went north," Finn said. Not accusing. Just stating.

"North is also a direction," Adrian said.

"But you're going east."

"I am."

Finn thought about this with the particular seriousness that nine-year-olds applied to things they had decided mattered. "If we go east and then north later, do we get to where they went?"

"Maybe," Adrian said. "Probably. Depending on how far north they traveled and how much time we have."

"And if we don't find them?"

He looked at the boy. Finn's face was dirty and his foot was wrapped and he had not cried since the first day Adrian had encountered him, at least not where anyone could see. The calculation behind his eyes was the calculation of a child who had understood that adults also did not know things, and was weighing how honest he wanted the answer to be.

"Then we find somewhere safe," Adrian said, "and we send word back the way we came, and we look for them from there."

Finn processed this. "That's not the same as finding them."

"No. It isn't."

"Okay," Finn said, and that was the end of it—the particular okay of a child who had decided that partial honesty was better than comfortable lies and was adjusting his understanding accordingly.

Owen was watching from slightly to the side with his arms folded and his face arranged into the expression he had borrowed from Adrian without knowing it.

Adrian felt something in his chest that he didn't have a name for. He stood up and looked at the eastern margin.

---

The child was the one who saw it first.

Not Finn—the girl who hadn't spoken much, whose name he had finally learned was Petra, fourteen, who had been walking in the middle of the group with her head down for three days. She had stopped walking, and when he turned to look she was standing at the edge of the road with her head up and her eyes on the eastern horizon.

He walked back to her. "What?"

She pointed.

He looked where she was pointing, beyond the last of the suburban tree line to where the land flattened out and the sky met the earth in a line that was too clear today, too visible after the morning haze had burned off.

He did not see it at first. Then he did.

"Something big," Petra said, voice low and unused-sounding. "Like a mountain. But new."

He saw it now—or the suggestion of it: a shape on the horizon that was too regular. Not the jagged silhouette of actual mountains, which existed nowhere within two hundred miles of Ashford. Not the artificial angularity of tower buildings, which he knew from Ashford's skyline and which didn't sit right at this distance or this angle. Something else. Something that sat on the flat eastern horizon with the solidity of geology but hadn't been there the day before yesterday when he had last had a clear sightline in that direction.

The others had stopped and gathered behind him. He could hear them breathing.

"What is it?" Hector asked.

"Don't know," Adrian said, which was true.

He looked at it for a long moment. It did not move. It did not pulse or glow—at least not from this distance, at least not in the daylight. It simply sat there on the far line of the world with the patience of something that had not yet decided whether it had noticed them.

He said, "We keep moving."

"Toward it?" Finn asked.

"East."

He turned back toward the direction he had been going. The shape was east. Selene might be east. He could not afford to be afraid of the only bearing he had.

Behind him, the child named Petra took one last look at the horizon and then fell back into line without a word.

The shape sat on the far edge of the world, patient and impossible and growing, by fractions too small to measure, with every step they took toward it.

Adrian did not look back again. Looking back was its own kind of danger.

He walked east, and the group walked behind him, and the morning opened ahead of them like a wound that hadn't finished happening.
