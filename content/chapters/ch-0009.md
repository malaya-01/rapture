On the eighth morning, Rowan stopped waiting for the radio to answer and started listening to what it was actually saying.

He had been at it since before the others woke. The generator burned diesel they could not afford to waste, and the radios it fed had become the closest thing the barn held to ritual — morning and evening, Rowan on the stool beside the charging rack, headset on, cycling through every frequency he knew. Fire channels. EMS. Military bands. Ham operators on the secondary shortwave bands his mentor Captain Duchamp had drilled into him two decades ago, because Duchamp had believed in redundancy the way some men believe in God. *The system will fail*, Duchamp used to say, pouring terrible coffee in the station kitchen. *Plan for that.*

Rowan had planned for it. He just hadn't believed it.

The shortwave tonight gave him static, static, a burst of something that might have been a carrier tone, more static, and then a voice — distant, fragmentary, cycling a recorded message in a loop so old the audio had degraded to something barely above noise:

*—civilian authorities advise—shelter in—do not approach—*

The voice dissolved. Behind it, layered like sediment, were other voices: wrong syllables, wrong cadence, a language that made his back teeth ache when he focused on it. Then nothing. He turned the dial past the gap where AM news stations had once lived and found only the hiss of an uninhabited spectrum.

He pulled the headset off and sat with it in his hands.

Eight days.

The thought arrived not like a fact but like a weight. Eight days since Atlas had found him in the kitchen and pointed east toward a sound in the bones of the house. Eight days since the Ashford Harbor footage on the television — the fracture, the impossible wings, the scale of something so large the news anchor's smile had simply stopped working. Eight days since he had driven his family down Miller's Cutoff through creatures that should not have existed, through a road that had heaved upward like a spine deciding to express itself. Eight days since he had arrived at this barn with a spiderweb windshield and four people he loved and a generator that still, by some grace, had not run dry.

In eight days, no rescue had materialized. No military column. No FEMA trucks. No helicopter circling with a megaphone. The fire department—his department, Engine Four, twenty-seven years of his life—had gone silent on day two. He had sent a radio query to the mutual aid frequencies on day three: nothing. Day five he had tried the state emergency broadcast system and received in return a recorded announcement in a voice he did not recognize, speaking instructions that made no geographic sense, referencing evacuation centers that might have been in their county or might have been in a country he could not identify.

Day six the voice had stopped.

Day seven he had tried the amateur frequencies and raised, briefly, another human—a man named Perkins, scared and south of the river, who said he'd heard there were camps but didn't know where, who said the roads were bad, who said stay where you are if you have water. The signal dropped. Rowan could not raise Perkins again on day eight, try as he might.

He sat on the stool and looked at the barn around him.

What had been a volunteer rescue barn—cinderblock walls, metal roof, red paint fading at the eaves—was now a place where eleven people slept on cots and folded blankets, shared a propane stove that stood between them and eating cold canned goods, and kept their voices low after dark because loud was an invitation to the things that moved along the tree line once the sun set.

Elena had organized the sleeping arrangements on day three, dividing the barn into a medical alcove near the cabinets and a living area near the stove, with the generator room kept separate and the doors reinforced with crossbars Rowan had cut from salvage lumber. She had done this without being asked, the way she had always done necessary things—quietly, efficiently, without ceremony, because ceremony was for situations that had a margin for it.

He loved her more thoroughly than he had words for, and that love had become a specific ache over the past eight days as he watched what the work was costing her.

---

He heard her before he saw her. The sound of a cabinet latch—careful, not to wake anyone—and then the precise series of sounds that meant Elena was checking supplies: the soft displacement of glass vials, the rustle of the gauze roll replaced in its position, a pause, a small intake of breath that meant she had found something she didn't want to find.

Rowan crossed the barn.

She stood at the medical cabinet with her back to him, shoulders set in the line he had learned to read the way she read fevers—not by measure but by posture. She had been awake half the night again. He could see it in the way she held the back of her neck with one hand while she catalogued with the other.

"How bad?" he asked.

She turned. Elena never flinched at being caught; she simply incorporated his presence and continued the assessment. "Antibiotics three days at current rate. The saline is gone—I've been using clean water and salt, which works until it doesn't." A beat. "The surgical sutures are fine. We won't need them until we need them and then we'll need them badly."

"Garrison?"

"Stable. The infection hasn't tracked further up the arm in twelve hours, which is better than yesterday." She replaced a bottle and closed the cabinet with the same careful attention she'd used to open it. "If I had a pharmacy, I'd be cautious. If I had what I have, I'll be inventive."

He wanted to tell her to sleep. He had told her to sleep twice in the past forty-eight hours and both times she had looked at him with an expression that was not quite a refusal—more like a patient acknowledgment that he was still, after all this time, failing to understand the math of what she did. Sleep was not a resource Elena allocated to herself until everyone else was stable. He had stopped arguing about it and started putting food near her when she worked.

"The radio," she said. It was not quite a question.

"Nothing useful."

She nodded once, the small precise movement of a woman who had already accounted for this possibility and was simply having the account confirmed. She did not say *I thought so* or *what do we do* because Elena did not spend words on questions she was already answering. She said: "Then we plan for what we have."

Rowan looked at his hands. The scrapes from the first night had healed into dark lines at the knuckle. There was a bruise along his forearm he had stopped noticing. He thought about Duchamp. *The system will fail. Plan for that.*

"I need to tell them," he said.

"The whole barn?"

"Yeah."

Elena looked at him for a long moment. Outside, something moved in the early morning beyond the tree line—the soft thud of something heavier than a deer—and they both went still until it passed. The barn held its breath with them. Then the sound diminished, and the morning reasserted its thin normalcy, and Elena touched his arm once with a hand that smelled of antiseptic and sleep deprivation.

"After breakfast," she said. "Let them eat first."

---

Atlas was up before the other children, which was not a surprise. Atlas had always treated dawn as a personal challenge, something to be met and beaten rather than endured, and the apocalypse had not changed this. It had only given him more to watch for.

He had taken, in the past week, to sitting on the cot nearest the barn door with his chin on his knees and his father's old field binoculars hanging around his neck, scanning the visible strip of road through the gap where the main door didn't quite meet the frame. He said nothing during these vigils. He ate what Elena handed him without being asked twice. He helped carry water and did not complain about the cold.

He was nine years old and trying, Rowan understood, very hard to be useful instead of afraid. Rowan recognized the posture from his own early years on the department, from the probie version of himself who had stood at every fire scene with his jaw set because a set jaw looked like confidence.

He sat beside his son.

Atlas moved the binoculars an inch to make room without being asked.

"Anything?" Rowan said.

"There was a group on the road yesterday. Before dark. Three adults, maybe more."

Rowan had seen them too. They had passed at a distance—moving east with the particular gait of people carrying everything they owned, pausing at the driveway entrance to the rescue barn's access track before moving on. He had let them move on. He did not feel entirely good about that decision and had not found a way to feel worse about it that would change the math.

"Didn't stop," Atlas said.

"No."

"Should we have—"

"I don't know yet." Rowan put his hand briefly on the back of Atlas's neck, the way he had done since the boy was small enough to carry. "Sometimes I don't know until later."

Atlas considered this with the gravity of a child who had been told honest things by adults he trusted and had decided, somewhere in the architecture of who he was becoming, to accept honest things as better than comfortable ones. "Okay," he said.

Leon appeared from the back of the barn wrapped in a blanket, eyes still blurred with sleep, dark hair pushed sideways. He was seven and still operating on the assumption that morning existed for the purpose of sitting near people he loved and being warm, an assumption Rowan did not want to correct. Leon pressed himself against Rowan's side without ceremony or comment and watched the gap in the door alongside his brother, and Rowan put his arm around the smaller boy and sat there for the three minutes before Elena called them to breakfast and the barn's daily work began.

*This*, he thought. *This is the thing worth holding.*

---

He told them after breakfast.

There were eleven people in the barn: his family of four, Garrison with the infected arm who had arrived on day four half-delirious and been dragged back from that edge by Elena's stubborn attention, a couple named Theo and Priya who had come on day five with their teenage daughter Petra who had barely spoken since arrival, a retired teacher named Weaver who wore the same cardigan every day and had somehow kept it clean, and two young men—brothers, early twenties, who had introduced themselves as Kai and Denis and had spent most of the first days sitting in the corner like animals trying to decide whether a new space was safe.

He stood near the stove. He didn't make it formal—no gathering them together, no announcement. He just said it, conversationally, while they were finishing their coffee or what passed for it: "I've been on the radios since day one. I want you to know what I've found."

Everyone stopped.

"The emergency services channels are dead. Not intermittent—dead. The military frequencies I know are either empty or running recorded messages that don't reference current events. I raised one amateur operator on day seven, south of the river, who had no information except to say stay put. I've had no contact since." He paused. He had rehearsed this, trying to find the framing that was honest without removing people's ability to function. He had concluded there was no such framing. "I don't believe a rescue operation is coming. I believe whoever was responsible for organizing rescue operations is either occupied with problems larger than our location or no longer operating."

The silence that followed was not shock. It was recognition—the sound of a thing already suspected being confirmed, which was different from surprise and sometimes worse. Theo put his hand over Priya's. Kai and Denis looked at each other. Weaver smoothed the cardigan over his knees and looked at the middle distance.

Garrison, the man with the infected arm, who had arrived incoherent and was now functional and lucid and slightly ashamed of the drama of his arrival, said: "So it's us."

"Yes," Rowan said.

"What does that mean for us specifically?"

"It means we operate as if help is something we build, not something we wait for."

He watched them process it. He had seen this before—not the apocalypse, but the aftermath of major incidents, the moment when firefighters emerging from a collapse or a mass casualty event had to reorganize their understanding of what was possible. There was always a moment of paralysis. The trick was not to eliminate it but to move people through it with enough momentum that the paralysis became decision.

"The barn is defensible," he continued, "but it's not permanent. We need to assess what we have, how long it lasts, and what comes after that. I want to go through the supply inventory today. All of it. Openly, with everyone who wants to understand the numbers."

"What about the things outside?" Priya asked. Her voice was steadier than her hands.

"We set watches. Day and night. Two-person rotations. I'll make the schedule this afternoon." He looked at each of them in turn—Kai and Denis, who had straightened; Petra, who had looked up from the floor for the first time since arriving; Weaver, who was nodding slowly. "You don't have to trust me," Rowan added. "You're here because you found shelter. What we build from here is a choice."

Kai said, "I drove trucks. Long haul. I know roads."

Denis said, "I know engines. Diesel mostly."

Priya said her husband was a civil engineer. Theo looked at his hands as if examining them for evidence of this.

Weaver said he had been a teacher for thirty-one years and didn't know what use that was, and Rowan told him honestly that he would find out.

---

The afternoon brought work and the work was clarifying.

Inventory: diesel, three-to-four weeks at current generator use—less if they were not disciplined. Water, adequate from the cistern if rain continued and they maintained the collection. Food, six weeks if they stretched, less if the group grew. Medical, as Elena had said, variable and concerning. Weapons were the wrong word but Rowan wrote it down anyway, under the heading *security resources*: the fire axe, two crowbars, Theo's hunting rifle with forty-three rounds, a kitchen knife Priya had been carrying since the first day.

He went through the numbers with whoever wanted to see them. Kai sat across the makeshift table for an hour, asking questions about diesel ratios and load calculations with a precision that came from years of understanding that precision was what kept trucks on the road and their drivers alive. Denis rebuilt the generator's air filter from components in the barn's maintenance cabinet. Petra helped Elena reorganize the medical alcove, not speaking much but working with care.

Atlas watched everything and asked questions when he judged them appropriate, and Rowan let him because Atlas at nine with information was safer than Atlas at nine without it.

Leon sat with Weaver near the stove. Rowan caught snatches of their conversation—the old man patient and unhurried, the boy asking about what happened to fire before people knew what fire was, about whether the sky had ever broken before. Weaver answered everything he knew and said honestly when he didn't know, which was most of it. Leon seemed to find this satisfying. There were children who needed certainty and children who needed honesty, and Leon, at seven, had always been the second kind.

The afternoon wore into evening without crisis, which was itself a kind of progress.

---

Dusk came in the particular way it had come every evening since the fractures—not clean, not gradual, but in layers of wrong color. The silver lines were fainter now than on day one, absorbed into the new normal of a sky that no longer behaved like sky, but they persisted along the horizon above the tree line, threading the clouds with light that should not have been there.

Rowan stood in the doorway at the end of the watch rotation and looked at it.

He thought about Adrian. He thought about him the same way he thought about the radio: methodically, cataloguing what he knew, trying to separate fact from fear. Adrian had sent a text on day zero—*You seeing this?*—and Rowan had answered, and that was the last contact. Adrian had been in the city with Selene. He was thirty years old and a very capable man who had spent his adult life moving toward crisis. He was also Rowan's younger brother, which made capability a partial comfort at best.

He would be moving. That was the fact. Adrian moved. He did not sit and wait for things to stop; he oriented toward whatever needed doing and started walking. He would be somewhere in this landscape, covering ground, doing what he had always done. That had to be enough.

He thought about Elena's hands on the medical cabinet that morning. He thought about Atlas at the door with binoculars too large for his face. He thought about Leon asking Weaver about fire.

He thought about Duchamp, who had retired four years ago and lived three counties south, and about whom Rowan could not think for too long without the grief arriving ahead of the facts.

He was still in the doorway when headlights appeared at the far end of the access track.

Two vehicles—one truck, one sedan, moving slowly, no way to know if that was caution or damage. They turned toward the barn with deliberate intention and stopped at the edge of the clearing where the track opened up. Doors opened. Shapes emerged: four adults, possibly five. Someone helped someone else from the back of the sedan—assisted, not carrying, but needed.

Rowan counted bodies. Counted supplies in his head. Counted the rate at which eleven people were consuming diesel, water, food, antibiotics.

The shapes at the edge of the clearing raised their hands—not a gesture of greeting, exactly, but of visibility. *See us. We are not armed, or if we are, we are showing you our open hands first.* It was the correct gesture. Someone in that group had thought about this.

He called back into the barn: "Elena. We have incoming."

She appeared beside him. She looked at the vehicles and the figures and she did the same arithmetic he had done, he could see it in the small tightening at the corner of her eyes. Then she stepped out past him into the failing light.

"Come into the clearing," she called. "Slowly. Keep your hands where we can see them."

They came.

Rowan counted: six adults, one child. A woman with a bandaged forearm. A man who moved like something in him was broken and not quite set. A girl roughly Atlas's age who looked at the barn the way people looked at things they'd stopped believing in and then found anyway.

Six more mouths. Six more bodies between them and whatever moved in the dark.

Six more people who needed somewhere to stand.

He stepped back from the doorway and let Elena do what Elena did, and he went back to the radios to try again, one more time, all frequencies, because the habit of trying had become the practice of being the kind of man who did not stop—even when he knew, as he knew now with the clean settled certainty of a firefighter who had read all the signs, that no answer was coming.

The static held its shape.

The barn held its warmth.

Outside, Ashford burned at the edge of the horizon, smaller than it had been yesterday, which meant less was left to burn.

Rowan turned the dial. Listened. Noted the frequency, the time, the failure.

Wrote it in the log.

Started again.
