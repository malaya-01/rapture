On the twelfth day, he was going the right direction for the wrong reasons.

He knew this. He had catalogued his reasoning on the second morning after sheltering in the maintenance shed, the way his brother catalogued incident reports: what he knew, what he believed, what he was afraid of. The column labeled *what he believed* was longer than either of the others, which meant he was operating on inference rather than evidence, which was the kind of condition that got firefighters killed and gym instructors ineffective and brothers lost.

What he knew: Selene had been at the refugee congregation point he had identified on the south side of the rail line three days ago, according to Ximena, who had been at the same congregation point two days earlier and described a silver-haired woman organizing intake lists with the particular combination of competence and terror that Ximena associated with people who were good at their work and were watching the work fail around them.

What he knew: Rowan's likely route from his house on the outskirts would have taken him south or southwest, toward the secondary roads that connected to the county infrastructure Rowan knew from his department's operational area. East was not Rowan's direction from his house.

What he believed: east was the direction where the congregation points were, where the survivors were moving, where Selene would go when the south side rail camp became untenable. East was where both of the people he was looking for might converge if they were still moving and still making the same kind of decisions people made when they were trying to find each other in a broken city.

What he was afraid of was not a column he had written down. He knew what was in it.

He kept moving east.

---

The ruin belt was what the eastern margin of Ashford's industrial district had become: a zone of partial and complete structural failures extending from the harbor to the overpass network two kilometers inland, where the ground had shifted and the buildings had followed. Not all at once — the failures had been occurring over eleven days, the kind of slow structural response to fracture-event ground disturbance that civil engineers would later explain with models Adrian would never read. What he understood was simpler: blocks that had been standing on day one were down on day twelve, and blocks he had memorized as navigational landmarks were now landmarks of a different kind.

He had four people with him.

Dav, who had spoken for the first time on the broadcast night and had not stopped since — not compulsively, but in the way of someone working through ten days of accumulated observations, testing language against what he had seen. He was seventeen and had been walking since day two with the kind of endurance that adolescent bodies sometimes produced in crisis, the endurance of a person who had not yet learned to understand their own limits as limits rather than parameters.

Preet, the delivery driver with the splinted radius. The splint was holding. The fracture was not healing — it could not heal properly in motion and they were always in motion — but it was not getting worse, which Adrian counted as success under the conditions.

Preet's neighbor Ximena, who was quiet in the particular way of competence: she spoke when she had information and did not speak when she didn't, which meant her words could be trusted. She had a map of the eastern margin from before the Rapture that she had been updating in pencil as the current reality diverged from the printed one.

The fourth was a woman named Bree who had joined them yesterday on the rail line, emerging from a drainpipe that she had been using as a sleeping position for three days. She was shaking when they found her and had stopped shaking after two hours of movement and a protein bar and the particular effect that other people's presence had on bodies that had been alone too long. She hadn't said much about what she'd seen in the drainpipe but Adrian did not ask.

He led. Not from authority — from the practical reality that he moved at a pace and with a pattern that could be followed, that he assessed threat before the group reached it, that the others had decided without discussion that this was how they were organized. He kept Preet and Bree toward the inside of routes, Dav and Ximena on the outside, because Dav's endurance and Ximena's pattern-reading compensated for the fact that both Preet's arm and Bree's recent stillness made them slower to respond to hazards.

It was not a perfectly efficient arrangement. It was the one he had, and he would work with the one he had.

---

The problem on day twelve was the overpass network.

He had been routing around the eastern overpass collapse since day three — the failure was too extensive for safe foot transit, the approaches too exposed to the territory of a particular large creature that had claimed the under-structure as den or patrol area; he had seen it twice at distance and identified it as not-dragon, not-walker, a third category his mind filed as *territorial and large* and treated accordingly. The detour added forty minutes to every eastward run but removed the category of risk he could not manage.

Today the detour route was compromised.

Something had moved through the tertiary alley that was his standard detour approach — not recently, based on the smell and the arrangement of debris, but within the past twelve hours. The movement had been large and had left the kind of damage that large things left: walls pushed rather than walked around, a parked car moved from its position by what must have been blunt force from the side, a door pulled off its hinges toward the inside of the building behind it, which meant the thing had been reaching rather than moving through.

Reaching was new. Reaching meant appendages and interest and an awareness of interior space that his *territorial and large* category had not included.

He stopped the group at the alley entrance.

"This is the detour route," Ximena said, reading his posture.

"Was," he said.

"The direct approach—"

"Has the territorial one under the overpass."

Preet said, quietly: "There's a third way."

Adrian looked at him.

"I ran deliveries in this district for eight years. There's a drainage channel that runs under the manufacturing block north of the overpass — it connects to the storm basin on the east side. It's deep enough to walk through if you crouch, long enough that you're through the overpass footprint in about four minutes."

Adrian thought about the under-overpass on day three, where he had crawled through with Selene in the dark, stone grinding above them as the seam shifted. He thought about Selene's voice counting through the breathing exercise until they were clear.

"Can Preet make it with the arm?" he asked Ximena, not Preet, because Preet would say yes regardless.

Ximena assessed. "If we keep the pace slow and I go first with the light."

"Dav?"

"I'm good," Dav said, with the certainty of a seventeen-year-old.

"Bree?"

Bree had been tracking the conversation with eyes that moved between speakers without settling on any of them. She was processing at a half-second delay, which Adrian had identified on day eleven as a stress response rather than an injury response — the kind of processing delay that happened when the nervous system was still working out which things were safe to react to. She nodded when he said her name.

They went through the drainage channel.

It was not as bad as the overpass underpass. It was bad in different ways: lower clearance than the overpass had been, the smell of standing water and something biological that he did not investigate, and the specific quality of darkness underground since the fractures had changed the way light distributed itself — not darker than before, but *differently* dark, the dark of a space that had something moving through it at a frequency too low for hearing. Mana, his mind supplied without evidence, using the word the broadcasts had introduced.

He counted steps. Ninety-four. Four minutes, roughly, at the pace they were holding.

They came out on the east side of the overpass footprint, in a yard between a closed manufacturing plant and a chain-link fence that had fallen inward. The overpass structure loomed to the south, close enough to see the seam where the fracture-light had made new geometry in the concrete.

---

He found the scarf on the wrong side.

He had been looking for it — not the scarf specifically, not with the specific intention of finding it, but looking for evidence of Selene the way he looked for all evidence in damaged environments: systematically, noting discrepancies, checking his assumptions against what the landscape actually said.

What the landscape said at the base of the overpass eastern approach was this: something had been here recently. Not *things* — the evidence was too selective, too human in its arrangement. A food wrapper placed deliberately against a wall rather than dropped in movement. A smudge on the concrete at shoulder height that was consistent with someone pressing against a surface in the way people pressed when they were hiding rather than moving. The faint compression in a sheet of damp cardboard that suggested someone had sat here for longer than a stop.

He had trained himself, over eleven days, to read this kind of evidence the way he read a room for exits: fast, without attachment, noting what was there.

The scarf was attached to a piece of chain-link at the south end of the yard, at a height Selene would reach if she were tying it there at five feet four inches. Silver-grey wool, the color she had chosen not because it was fashionable but because she found grey calm in the way other people found blue or green calm. He had bought her a grey scarf for her birthday and she had worn it until the wool felted and he had bought another.

This was not the second scarf. This was one he did not know, similar enough in color that he was certain.

He stood in front of it for a moment.

There was blood on it.

Not a lot. Not catastrophic blood, not the blood of a grievous wound — the blood of a cut or an abrasion, the kind of injury you acquired in transit through broken architecture, the kind you taped or pressed and kept moving. But blood.

He removed it from the chain-link carefully. Held it.

Ximena appeared at his shoulder. She read the scene without being asked: the evidence of occupation, the height of the scarf, the blood on the wool.

"She was here," she said.

"Yes."

"The blood—"

"Minor. She was moving when she tied this." He looked at the scarf. Selene had always left marks when she was uncertain of her route — a habit she had started in university, leaving notes in library margins and on doors, the instinct of someone who had been told her whole life that she took up too much space and had decided to take up exactly enough space to be found. "She was here and she went east. The scarf is facing east."

"We're going east."

"Yes."

He folded the scarf and put it in his jacket pocket, in the interior pocket against his chest where he kept the things that required protection rather than the things that were useful. He did not think about what it meant that she had been here and was not here now. He thought about *east*, which was a direction, and directions could be followed.

---

They reached a secondary congregation point — a school gymnasium three buildings east of the overpass — at mid-afternoon.

Sixty people. No radio. Food for four more days at the current count, which the woman managing the distribution — a teacher named Holt — had already extended by implementing a ration system that she had built from first principles with a piece of chalk on the gymnasium floor. Adrian assessed the room in the first five minutes: no immediate threat, adequate shelter for the conditions, organization that was holding but needed more experienced hands than the ones it had.

He delivered Preet, Ximena, Bree, and Dav to the gymnasium resources — food, water, the medical supply chain Holt had improvised — and went to find the community radio, which turned out to be a hand-crank unit in the office off the main hall, operated by a man named Jemison who had been a logistics manager before the Rapture and had transferred that skill set to the management of what the gymnasium had.

"Looking for someone," Adrian said. "Silver hair, omega, mid-twenties, organized, probably coordinating intake if she's here."

Jemison considered. "There was a woman here. Two days ago. Silver hair, yes. Organized, definitely. She stayed one night and left east."

"Why?"

"Someone came in with a rumor of a larger congregation point. Twelve kilometers east, better resources. Most of the survivors who could move were cycling through toward it. She left with a group of four."

East. Still east.

He asked what supplies the gymnasium needed and did what he did — organized a supplementary scavenge run with two of the gymnasium's more mobile survivors, identified a secondary water source three blocks north that Ximena's updated map suggested was still accessible, helped Dav's endurance and energy into something the gymnasium could use for the afternoon watch rotation.

He stayed for dinner — the word was generous, the reality was calorie allocation at a table that had been a lunch bench two weeks ago — and considered the four people he had carried east.

Preet would be here for weeks. The arm needed immobility it was not going to get in transit, and this gymnasium had the space and the resources to provide something close. Ximena had already inserted herself into the distribution logistics with the efficiency of someone who had been doing this kind of work in different forms for years. Bree was in the corner with two of the younger survivors, not talking much but present in the way that mattered.

Dav was watching him.

"You're leaving," Dav said.

"In the morning."

"Still looking."

"Still looking."

Dav was quiet. "She's east?"

"Best evidence says east."

"You could stay. They need people."

"They do," Adrian said. "And they have people. In the morning they have you, and Ximena, and Preet knows roads east of here better than anyone in this gym." He met the boy's eyes. "I'm not leaving because you can manage without me. I'm leaving because the person I'm looking for can't manage without someone looking for her, and right now I'm the someone."

Dav turned this over with the care he applied to arguments. He had the particular seventeen-year-old quality of taking things seriously because he had not yet learned to protect himself from taking things seriously, and Adrian found that quality, in this world, more valuable than its opposite.

"Okay," Dav said.

"Keep moving east if you leave here," Adrian said. "The roads north of the rail line are better than the south routes. Avoid the overpass footprint — go through the drainage channel on the manufacturing block north side."

Dav nodded. Remembering. He was a boy who remembered.

"And if you find a silver-haired woman organizing things," Adrian said, "tell her east is where I'm going and I won't stop."

---

He slept in the gymnasium with the others and woke an hour before dawn.

He picked up his bag. Checked his flashlight, his water, the dented flashlight that had been dented since he'd swung it at a grave-walker on day one and that he kept for that reason and also because it was heavy and weight was a tool.

The interior pocket of his jacket held the grey scarf.

He went through the gymnasium to the door and stepped out into the predawn dark, the fractured sky's silver-wound glow making the street navigable, the city east of him still holding its wrong geometry.

He thought about Selene's face the morning before the sky broke — coffee steam, laptop open, statistics as personal enemies, the private smile she gave when they were alone and did not need to perform stability for anyone.

He thought: *She is east. I am going east. These facts are enough.*

He walked.

Behind him, the gymnasium held its people. Below him, Ashford held its dead and its changed and its twelve days of evidence that the world had been replaced entirely. Above him, the fractures held their silver wounds, which had not healed and had not grown, as if the sky were waiting to see what the people below intended to do with what it had shown them.

He intended to find her.

He walked toward the light that was not sunrise.
