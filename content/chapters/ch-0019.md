He started the list on the inside wall of the barn at seven in the morning, in the space between the door frame and the first support strut where the cinderblock was smooth enough to write on.

He used the chalk Petra had found in the supply cabinet — the same chalk he had used for the rules on the door, the same chalk Leon used to draw with when the drawing helped him think. He wrote in block letters because block letters were legible and legibility was the point.

*Day 0 — Garrison*

He stopped.

He did not know Garrison's first name.

He turned to the barn, which was beginning to produce its morning sounds — the generator not yet on, the stove warming, the shuffle of people adjusting to wakefulness on cots and folded blankets. He raised his voice to a level above the ambient noise but not above the calm threshold he had established in himself as the maximum for indoor communication.

"Garrison. Does anyone know his first name?"

A pause.

Weaver said: "Thomas."

Rowan wrote: *Day 0 — Thomas Garrison*

He wrote the name of every person who had died in the barn's first seventeen days. He had six. Thomas Garrison was the only one with a full name entry because the others had arrived without introduction or had not survived long enough for full names to be known. He wrote what he knew: a woman he called only *Day 4 — woman from the north road, approximate 60s, farm jacket.* A man who had arrived with the group on day nine and died within twelve hours of a head injury whose severity he had not disclosed: *Day 9 — man, dark coat, concussive.* Soren's entry he wrote carefully, with the detail he had from Priya: *Day 17 — Soren Mehta, age 8.*

Six names. Seventeen days. The rate was not sustainable and it was also not as bad as it could have been, and both of those things were true simultaneously and neither one was comfort.

He put the chalk back in his pocket and looked at the list.

---

The argument started at breakfast.

Not with him. With Cole, who had been one of the day-six arrivals and who had spent his previous life driving supply routes and had spent the past eleven days in the barn developing the kind of concern for operational efficiency that tended to emerge in people who were good at logistics when the logistics of survival were bad.

Cole said: "We need to talk about movement."

Rowan looked up from his bowl.

"We have three weeks of food," Cole said. "The diesel runs out in eight days at current consumption. If we're going to move, we should move while we have the vehicle and the fuel, not after."

"Where?" Kai asked.

"East. The radio's been picking up the congregation point broadcasts. The school gymnasium twelve kilometers east has resources, coordination—"

"We have coordination here," Denis said.

"We have seventeen people and a barn. The gymnasium has—"

"Three hundred people with less security and less food per capita than we have here," Rowan said.

Cole looked at him.

"I've been listening to the same broadcasts you have." Rowan set down his bowl. "The congregation points are high-traffic, which means high visibility, which means high creature activity at the perimeter. They're burning resources faster than us because they have more people and less structure. The gymnasium is three weeks ahead of us in food depletion by their own numbers."

"But the people—"

"The people are also here." He looked around the barn. "Our group has structure that took seventeen days to build. We move to the gymnasium and we start over with seventeen people who know each other inside three hundred people who don't know us."

"The numbers—"

"The numbers say we're doing better than most." He paused. "That's not a reason to be comfortable. It's a reason to be thoughtful about what we'd be trading."

Cole was quiet.

Rowan let the quiet sit for a moment, the way he let silences sit in difficult meetings: long enough to show he was thinking, not so long it became stonewalling. "I'm not saying no. I'm saying we do the math together before we decide."

He asked Denis for the diesel count. He asked Felicity for the medical supply projection. He asked Theo, whose structural engineering background he had been drawing on since the barn's first week, about the current shelter assessment — how long the barn could be occupied safely, what maintenance it needed, what its failure modes were.

It took forty minutes. At the end of the forty minutes, Cole was running the same calculation with the same numbers and reaching a different conclusion than he had started with: not that movement was wrong, but that the timing and destination required more information than any of them currently had.

"Reconnaissance," Cole said. "Before we decide."

"Yes," Rowan agreed.

Cole was a man who followed logic rather than preferences, which was why he had ended up in the argument and why he was ending it: the logic had changed. Rowan had a great deal of respect for people who followed logic rather than preferences. The barn needed more of them.

---

After breakfast, the second argument.

This one was not about movement. It was about the list on the wall.

Priya said: "We should have a service. For Soren."

Weaver nodded. "And for the others."

"We don't have time—" Cole started.

"We have thirty minutes," Priya said, with the specific softness of a person who had decided not to raise her voice because she did not need to, because the room's attention was already hers.

Rowan watched the room navigate this.

The barn had eighteen living people and six names on the wall. The six names were not friends to all eighteen; most of the eighteen had arrived after most of the deaths, and attachment was a function of time and proximity that the barn's population had not had time to build uniformly. The argument for a service was about the particular kind of currency that Rowan understood from his department's culture: rituals were not sentiment, they were maintenance, the practice of treating shared loss as shared rather than individual so that the group did not fracture along the seams of uneven grief.

"Thirty minutes," he said. "After the morning watch change."

Cole nodded. Denis nodded. The others followed.

---

Weaver led it.

He stood in the center of the barn with his cardigan and his thirty-one years of finding language for things that resisted language and he spoke, not at length, not with the performance of a eulogy but with the precision of a man who knew that the people in front of him were still deciding whether the world they were living in was worth the effort of continuing to be human in.

He said: *We did not know all of them well. Some of them we did not know at all. We know their names because we wrote them down, and we wrote them down because names are the minimum we owe to people who were here and are no longer here. The minimum is enough. The minimum is more than the world requires of us right now. It is what we choose to require of ourselves.*

He said: *If we stop requiring this of ourselves, we will be efficient but we will not be people, and efficiency without people is only math.*

Then he said the six names.

When he said *Soren Mehta, age 8*, Priya made a sound she had made once before, and Theo put his arm around her, and Atlas looked at the wall with the expression he had been wearing since yesterday morning — not the nine-year-old expression he had brought to the barn on day zero but something older, something he had been building toward since the creek bed and the barn and the moment Rowan had told him that brave is not stupid.

Leon was standing beside the wall with his hand flat on the cinderblock beside Soren's name. He was not touching the name — he was touching beside it, the specific gesture of a child who respected that the name belonged to something larger than his grief but wanted to be physically adjacent to it.

Rowan stood at the back and watched his family and the people who had become, by seventeen days of proximity and shared necessity, something closer to community than collection, and felt the weight of the list not as failure but as accounting.

Six people. In seventeen days. In a world that had no emergency services, no hospital within reach, no pharmaceutical supply chain, no cavalry coming.

Six.

They should have been more.

---

After the service, the barn returned to its work, because work was also a form of remembrance — the continuation of living in a space that the dead had also occupied.

Rowan was at the medical cabinet doing the supply inventory when Leon appeared beside him.

"Dad."

"Yes."

"Someone added to the wall."

Rowan turned. Went to the wall with the names.

Below his block-letter list, in smaller handwriting, someone had added a line that was not a name.

*We are alone.*

He stood in front of it.

The words were not new — he had heard them in the broadcast nine days ago, the sentence that had ended the last signal of whatever remained of the old world's information infrastructure. He had heard them in his own internal accounting on day one when he had understood that no rescue was coming. They were not unfamiliar.

But they were here on the wall beside the names. And they were not in fresh chalk.

He crouched and examined the inscription more carefully.

It was not chalk. It was something else — etched, not drawn, into the surface of the cinderblock itself. Not deep — a surface scoring, the kind a sharp object made. It was not recent. The color of the mark was different from the white chalk of his block letters: darker, settled into the material the way old marks did.

Older than yesterday.

Older than the broadcast.

The wall of the rescue barn, which had been here before the Rapture, in the meadow north of the county roads where the volunteer rescue squad had drilled twice a month, held the words *We are alone* in a scoring that predated the world that had produced the broadcast.

He stood up.

He thought about what that meant for a long time, and reached no conclusion he was comfortable with.

Weaver appeared beside him. The teacher had been at the barn since day eight and had shown no indication of having been there before; but Weaver had been here before, twice that Rowan knew of, for supply runs. He might know.

"The inscription," Rowan said, and pointed.

Weaver looked. He was quiet for a moment. "That was here when I arrived on day eight," he said.

"You didn't mention it."

"There were other things to mention." He paused. "It might be older than that. The rescue squad used this building for forty years."

"Someone in the rescue squad wrote it."

"Or someone who used the building. Before the squad." Weaver looked at the words. "It's interesting, isn't it. The same sentence. Before the broadcast."

Rowan looked at the barn wall and at the names above the inscription and at the sentence that had apparently been here, in the cinderblock of a volunteer rescue building in a meadow on the outskirts of a city that had now ended, for years.

*We are alone.*

He thought: whoever wrote this, wrote it as a statement. Or a warning. Or a conclusion reached after some experience that had brought them to this barn, as he had been brought, and to this wall, as his dead had been brought.

He thought: the sentence was true in the way all generalities were true — imprecisely, partially, containing more than it said.

They were alone in the sense that no one was coming. They were not alone in the barn. The barn was not alone in the meadow. The meadow was not alone in the world.

He picked up his chalk.

Below the inscription, in the same block letters he used for the names and the rules, he wrote:

*Not finished yet.*

He stood back and looked at what he had written.

It was not a refutation. It was a continuation. The sentence on the wall could stay — it was true — and his answer could stay beside it — also true — and the two things could occupy the same wall without either canceling the other.

He put the chalk in his pocket.

Atlas was at his shoulder.

"What does it mean?" Atlas asked.

"That someone else was here and felt what we feel," Rowan said. "And that we're still going."

Atlas looked at the wall for a long time.

"I want to put someone's name on it," he said. "Not someone who died. Someone who was here."

Rowan considered this. "Whose name?"

Atlas looked at the barn — at the cots, the stove, the people doing the barn's daily work with the specific quality of people who had decided, by presence rather than declaration, that this was where they stood. "Everybody's," he said.

"That's a lot of chalk."

"I know." Atlas was not asking permission. He was telling his father what he intended to do.

Rowan handed him the chalk.

Atlas went to the wall.

He wrote carefully, in letters smaller than his father's but more deliberate than a nine-year-old's letters needed to be, the names of seventeen living people below the names of six dead ones.

Weaver's name. Denis and Kai. Petra, who had barely spoken since day five. Felicity, who had arrived two days ago with pharmaceutical stock and competence and the expression of someone who had been doing this alone for too long. Every person in the barn.

When he reached the end of the list he looked at his father.

"You're not on it," Rowan said.

Atlas wrote his own name. Then Leon's. Then Elena's. Then, last, Rowan's own — in letters slightly larger than the others, which Rowan noticed and did not comment on, because sometimes a nine-year-old's handwriting told you what they meant better than their words.

He stood back and looked at the wall.

Twenty-three names. Six dead, seventeen living. *We are alone* scratched in old chalk below them, and *Not finished yet* beside it.

The barn wall held its accounting.

Rowan held his son's shoulder for a moment.

The work was still there. It would always be there. But this, too, was work.

He let go, and they went back to it.
