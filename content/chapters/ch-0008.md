On the second day, the food problem became undeniable.

Not critical — he had two days left at quarter rations if he was careful, which was a mathematical fact rather than a plan. But the apartment he had treated as a shelter was also becoming a cage, and cages had their own failure modes. He had identified three: water (the tap had given him four liters before pressure failed, not enough), food (counted and dwindling), and information (the hand-crank radio cycling between silence, recorded messages, and the ache-inducing language he could not identify). All three pointed the same direction: out.

He had spent the morning thinking about out.

The building's stairwell was a route he had used twice yesterday and knew to present problems at the third-floor landing — where something had paused at his door last night — and at the second-floor window, which faced an alley he had not yet assessed in daylight. The front exit opened on Fourth Street. The back exit was a service door that connected to the alley where the dumpsters served the nail salon and the takeout restaurant that had closed last spring; he had used that alley once a week for eighteen months and knew it as a passage without interest.

Alleys without interest were, in the current taxonomy, assets.

He packed what he could carry without slowing: the utility knife, the hand-crank radio in the bag's outer pocket, the remaining food distributed between bag and jacket pockets to keep the weight low and the access fast, the city map in the inside jacket pocket where it would not fall. He drank the last of one water bottle so he was carrying one full and one empty rather than two half-full, which was a choice his mother would have called typical of him and which was in fact the correct choice.

He looked at the door for thirty seconds before unbarring it.

The chair first. Then the deadbolt in pieces — open slowly, hold the knob against the catch on the return, do not let the mechanism snap.

The hallway smelled of dust and something metallic, the smell that had been present since yesterday and was now either environmental or his own fear, he could not tell.

Empty.

He moved toward the stairs.

---

The stairwell announced the problem before he reached the second floor.

Sound first: the same wet patience he had heard from the landing last night, but plural — two rhythms instead of one, slightly out of phase with each other, which meant two of them and not in coordination. The crossing guard from yesterday had had that quality. So had the two others he'd seen moving in the lane. They did not communicate; they occupied the same space by proximity rather than strategy.

He stopped on the third-floor landing.

From the stairwell below he could smell them — not rot, not yet, but something copper-sweet and wrong, the smell he associated now with the changed ones and would not soon forget.

He had approximately fifteen seconds before one or both sounds became a shape.

He went up.

Not his first choice — up led to the roof, which led nowhere useful, but the service door to the roof was his second-best option and he had filed it that way deliberately since yesterday. He moved on the outer edge of each step where the wood was quieter against the frame, a habit built from years of not wanting to wake a neighbor he had never met, repurposed now against things that were not neighbors.

The fourth floor was empty. The access hatch to the roof stuck on the first push — swollen wood, a problem the building management had reportedly been addressing for the past three winters — and opened on the second with a crack that was too loud in the particular way of sounds that could not be recalled.

Below him, the wet sound changed tempo.

Cassian went through the hatch.

---

The roof smelled like air. Not clean air, not Ashford air, but the absence of the building's sealed-in quality — open sky, the faint char of distant burning, the morning light that was ordinary in color even if ordinary had been revoked as a condition. He pressed against the parapet and waited.

The hatch below him was silent.

He gave it two minutes. Then three.

Nothing came through.

He breathed.

He looked at Ashford. The city lay below in a state that the newspapers, if they were still printing, would have described as *devastated* — damage distributed unevenly, some blocks intact, others showing fire scars or structural failure or the particular stillness of places that had been emptied of living occupants in the space of an afternoon. The smoke columns he had seen yesterday had diminished, not because anything was better but because there was less left to burn. The fracture above the harbor was smaller in angular size from here than it had been yesterday, but he did not believe it had actually closed. He thought he was simply becoming calibrated to its presence the way the eye calibrated to dimness — not by the darkness receding but by the eye adjusting to what was there.

He needed to go north-east.

There was a pharmacy on Calloway Street, four blocks away, that he had passed every week since moving to the neighborhood. It was not a large pharmacy — a neighborhood drug store that smelled of cough syrup and stock photos of smiling families — but it had the thing pharmacies had, which was inventory: food supplements, water purification, electrolyte tablets, things that could extend the utility of what he already had. The question was whether anything was left, and what condition the building was in, and what condition the four blocks were in.

He planned the route from the rooftop. Took his city map out and traced it with one finger, identifying landmarks from their rooftop equivalent — the converted warehouse on Third that he could recognize from its water tower, the chain-link-fenced lot on the corner of Calloway and Western, the pharmacy's distinctive yellow sign which he could almost see from here if the smoke between them were less.

He climbed down the fire escape on the north face of his building.

---

He saw the grave-walker on the second block.

Not the crossing guard — a different one. This one had been a man and was still wearing the evidence of having been a man: work boots, cargo trousers, a jacket that had been zipped before whatever had unmade him changed the geometry of his chest so that the zip sat wrong. He was crouched over something in the middle of the street — Cassian could not tell what, and made a decision not to try to tell — and his attention was fully occupied.

Cassian pressed himself into the recessed entryway of a shuttered electronics shop and watched.

The grave-walker's attention was not human attention. It did not scan; it focused on the thing it was focused on with a concentration that had no edges, no peripheral awareness, the way the attention of something looked when its nervous system had been reorganized around hunger rather than cognition. Its eyes, when it moved its head slightly, caught the fracture-light the way all the changed ones' eyes did: coins, silvered glass, holding light they had no reason to hold.

Cassian catalogued for thirty seconds: speed (still slow, as consistent with yesterday's sample); awareness radius (appears limited, sound and proximity rather than line-of-sight); behavioral focus (preoccupied, preoccupation reduces response time to secondary stimuli). He backed away from the entryway the way he had backed away from Mara's coffee cup on an afternoon that felt like another century, smooth, no sharp edges.

The grave-walker did not look up.

He went a block east, which added distance to his route but cost less than the alternative, and came at Calloway from the longer angle.

---

The pharmacy had been looted, but not comprehensively.

The front window was broken — not in the smash-and-take way of organized opportunism but in the way of someone who had crashed a car into the facade, possibly accidentally, possibly in a vehicle that no longer had a driver. The cash register lay on its side. The greeting card rack had tipped, spilling get-well-soons across the floor in a pattern that Cassian found grimly appropriate.

He went to the back.

The back was where the real inventory lived: medication racks, first aid cabinets, the supply room that community pharmacies kept behind the dispensing counter because customers were not meant to self-serve the things that required assessment. The combination lock on the supply room door was a standard commercial padlock — he could not open it, and he did not try, and he reminded himself that trying to access things he had no training for was the kind of decision that produced injury, and injury was now uncorrectable.

The shelves had been picked over but not picked clean. He found: six water purification tablets in a foil strip that had been left on the shelf, which meant whoever had been here before had not known what they were. A packet of electrolyte powder. A box of protein bars of which eight remained. A lightweight emergency blanket. Athletic tape. Two rolls of gauze. A broad-spectrum antibiotic in a sealed box with someone's prescription label, which he took with the awareness that this was a category of choice he would have to make going forward — *whose name is on the label* would stop being a meaningful criterion when the alternative was infection.

He packed it all, moving efficiently, not rushing because rushing meant things dropped and things dropped meant noise.

He was filling the last gap in his bag when the supply room door opened.

---

The thing that came out of the supply room had not been in the supply room when he arrived.

He was certain of this — certain because he had been listening, because the lock had been on the outside of the door when he checked it, because nothing had moved when he moved. It had come through the back of the supply room through a wall that should not have opened and was now in the narrow dispensing corridor between the shelves and the counter, and Cassian was also in the narrow dispensing corridor, and there was approximately three feet of space between them.

Grave-walker, his mind filed it.

Female, recently changed — recently enough that the clothes were largely intact, a pharmacy coat with a name tag he did not read because reading it would have taken a unit of attention he needed elsewhere. Her jaw worked. Her hands were wrong at the knuckles. Her eyes found him with the coin-bright fixed focus of a thing that had replaced cognition with targeting.

She moved.

Not slowly this time. Whatever the pharmacy coat woman had been in life had left her faster than the man in the cargo trousers on the street, or the changed ones operated at different speeds depending on—

He threw the protein bar box.

Not at her. Past her — at the shelving behind and to her left, a throw designed not to hit but to redirect, the kind of misdirection that cost him twelve inches of space and bought him half a second.

It was enough.

He went over the counter rather than around it, clearing it at an angle that cost him a palm against the corner — pain, catalogued, not structural — and hit the floor on the other side moving. The pharmacy coat woman came over the counter more slowly, the way grave-walkers always moved slower when they had to navigate terrain that required decision rather than direction. He was already at the broken window.

He went through it and ran.

---

He ran for a block before stopping in another entryway, because stopping was information and running without stopping was not.

His hand was bleeding where the counter corner had caught it. He pressed it against his jacket and assessed: not deep, not arterial, would stop on its own. He pressed the athletic tape he had taken — still in his jacket pocket, not in the bag — around the cut with the focused calm of someone performing a task that required attention and not very much courage.

His hands shook a little.

He allowed them to shake. It was the correct response to the situation. He let them shake for sixty seconds and then they stopped.

He looked at his palm. The cut was taped. The tape was neat. He had done it correctly.

He looked at the space where the tape met the cut and there was — for one moment, for less than a moment, for an event so brief his brain categorized it under *possible hallucination* and then immediately under *happened*—

Warmth.

Not heat from the tape. Heat from underneath the tape. From inside his hand, or from the hand itself, in the particular location of the cut. The warmth lasted three seconds and then faded, and when he peeled back the tape corner to look, the cut was — not closed. Not healed. But the edges had stopped bleeding in a way that felt faster than athletic tape alone should accomplish.

He held his hand and looked at it for a long time.

He put the tape back.

He did not write this down. Not yet. He did not have words for it yet, and his rule about the notebook was that he wrote what he could describe accurately, not what he could only describe as *wrong* or *impossible*, because wrong and impossible were not data.

He stood up, adjusted his bag, and continued east.

---

He found the intersection at Calloway and Third by accident — by following the street until the buildings fell back on the east side and opened onto a block-sized area that had been a commercial parking lot and was now, by virtue of the fall of one three-story building to the south, somewhat more defensible than parking lots generally were.

He stopped and looked at it for the reasons Marcus Vale would have stopped and looked at it, though Marcus Vale was somewhere on the other side of the city doing the same thing with better instruments: because interesting terrain was data, and data was what kept you alive when the list of known facts was short.

The parking lot sat at an intersection of three streets, which meant sightlines in three directions. The collapsed building to the south formed a wall of rubble that was climbable but would slow anyone — or anything — crossing it. Two other buildings framed the east and north sides, both intact, both with accessible upper floors if the stairwells held. A fire hydrant stood at the northwest corner, which meant a water supply line below if infrastructure remained. A hardware merchant's van had rolled against the east wall and lodged there — shelter, potentially additional tools, possibly fuel if it had not been looted.

He marked it on his city map with a small circle.

He wrote beside it, in his receipt-paper margin notation: *defensible? water. walls. sightlines. 3 directions. too open still — but if walls were added…*

He did not finish the sentence. It was too early for the sentence. You did not build walls when you were still figuring out whether you would survive the week.

But you marked it.

---

He was two blocks from home when he heard the sound behind him.

Different from the pharmacy coat woman — denser, lower, the sound of something larger moving through the space between buildings with the particular intention of something that had found a trail. He did not look back. Looking back cost forward motion and he had learned from this morning that forward motion was what the cut on his palm had bought him.

He moved at the pace just below running that covered ground without announcing itself. Past the shuttered bakery, the alley with the dumpsters — smell of sweetness and rot and the wrong copper-sweet of something that had been here recently — onto his block, the nail salon still dark at the base of his building, the door he had propped against closing with a wedge he had placed this morning.

The wedge was where he had left it.

He went through, pulled the door, replaced the wedge from inside.

He heard the sound outside pass and then slow and then stand at the door for a long moment — felt it more than heard it, the pressure of attention from outside, the awareness of a thing that was deciding whether the door was worth the door.

A line of silver light ran through the gap at the bottom of the door — not sunlight, not the building's emergency strips, but the fracture-light refracted from the street — and the sound outside stopped.

Waited.

Moved on.

Cassian stood inside the door for five minutes after the sound faded. Then he climbed the stairs to his apartment, ate one protein bar, drank a measured amount of water, and sat on the floor with his palm open on his knee.

The cut was still there. Taped. Slightly cleaner at the edges than it should have been.

He looked at it until the light outside changed.

He did not know what the warmth had been. He did not know what the line of silver light at the door had meant to the thing outside. He did not know what the intersection at Calloway and Third could become, in some version of the future he was not yet willing to believe in.

He knew: four blocks, one encounter, one escape, one filled bag.

He knew: food for four more days at half rations.

He knew: there was a parking lot at Calloway and Third with walls on two sides and sightlines in three directions, and he had put a circle on his map, and the circle meant he had not stopped planning.

He uncapped his pen and wrote on the receipt paper in the smallest letters his ordinary handwriting could produce:

*Day 2. Alive. Pharmacy looted but not empty. Back supply room: ingress unknown — monitor. GW faster than sample 1. Throw misdirection: works. Intersection C/3: note. Hand: tape, cut, warmth — ?? Note for later.*

He folded the paper and put it in the notebook beside the map.

The city held its silver wounds above him.

His hand held its secret.

For now, both were quiet.
