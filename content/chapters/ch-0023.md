The first thing Cassian noticed about high ground was that it cost something.

Not the climb—the climb was just knees and lungs, and he had been walking long enough that both had stopped complaining in any meaningful way. What it cost was attention. From street level, Ashford was a series of immediate problems: the blocked intersection, the smell from the building on the left, the sound that might be wind or might not be. You navigated those problems one at a time, and the narrowness of it was almost a mercy. You did not have to think about the shape of things. You only had to get through them.

From forty feet up, on the cracked observation deck of what had once been a parking garage on the highest point of the Meridian District, Cassian could see the shape of things.

It was not a mercy.

He had his city map out—the one he had carried since before Rapture, the one with soft creases from years of refolding. He had crossed through half the entries in blue pen, the ones that no longer applied. Hospitals: crossed out. Emergency shelters: crossed out. The library branch where he had renewed his overdue books for the fourteenth time: crossed out, not because it was destroyed but because libraries didn't solve the immediate problem of not starving. He had added his own marks in pencil. Symbols he had invented because he needed a faster vocabulary than words: a small circle for a water source he'd verified, a square for a structure that was still load-bearing, an X for a territory claim he had mapped and avoided.

The city below him was a taxonomy of his own fear.

He spread the map against the concrete parapet and held it flat against the wind, which was doing what Ashford wind always did—finding the angles. He took out his pencil and studied what he could see from here and compared it to what the map told him had existed.

The agreement was partial, and worsening.

---

He had come up here for a reason that he had not quite admitted to himself yet.

For twenty-three days he had been surviving in the only way he knew how: by treating the problem in front of him as the only problem. Food. Water. Shelter. Monster territories. The sequence kept him moving and moving kept the other thing at bay—the thing that appeared when he stopped, which was the knowledge that this was not going to end. Not in the week-long way that disasters ended. Not with the military restoring order or the power coming back or the government broadcasting a plan over the emergency channel. He had understood that since Day Ten, since the broadcast said *we are alone* and then the silence after, the silence that had weight.

But understanding it in the abstract was different from facing the particular arithmetic of it from forty feet up.

He looked at what was left of his city and tried to be honest with himself, which was harder than it sounded.

The buildings still standing: more than he would have thought. Ashford had been built in three eras—the old district of brick and stone, the mid-century concrete of the city center, and the newer glass towers that had not survived the first week. The glass was gone or going. The concrete was mostly standing. The brick was standing everywhere except where fire or impact had brought it down, and even some of those gaps were less terrible than the ones that looked intact.

What the map and his eyes together told him:

There was water. Meridian District sat at an elevation that put it above the compromised water table zones he'd been navigating at street level. He had found two spring-fed sources in the last four days—actual springs, not taps, as if the ground had decided to express itself now that the pressure from infrastructure had shifted. He marked them both: small circles, annotated with depth estimates.

There were walls. Not purpose-built walls—he wasn't thinking about walls in that way yet, because thinking about walls required thinking about what the walls were for, and what the walls were for required thinking about people, and thinking about people required thinking about whether he wanted to— He stopped himself. Walls. There were natural chokepoints in the Meridian District's street grid: the old market square that was open enough for visibility but small enough to monitor from two positions, the overpass spur to the northwest that was the only vehicle-width entry from that direction, the embankment that formed a natural slope on the eastern edge. Not a fortress. But the bones of something that could become more defensible than anywhere else he'd seen.

There were sightlines. From this deck you could see every major approach route from the north, west, and south. The east was partially obscured by the old commercial block, but he had counted at least two positions at lower elevation that would cover that gap. Nothing came from this direction without being visible from somewhere in the quarter.

He wrote this in the margins of his map because he did not have a better system and the information would not survive if it only existed in his head.

He was not going to settle here.

He wanted to be clear about that, with himself, because the alternative was dangerous—not physically dangerous but the other kind, the kind where you started making decisions based on what you wanted the world to be instead of what it was. He had seen it in three different groups he had moved past in the last week and a half: the survivors who had found a location that felt secure and had stopped asking whether it was actually secure, who had started investing in the permanence of a place without verifying whether the permanence would hold. Two of those groups had been fine. The third had not been fine when he'd passed through later.

He was not going to make the same mistake.

What he was doing was documenting. For whoever came after. There would be people after him who needed to know this terrain, who would come to the Meridian District without the twenty-three days of observation he'd accumulated and who would make decisions about where to shelter and how to hold it with less information than they needed. If he marked the map carefully—the springs, the chokepoints, the sightlines, the territories to avoid—then the map would do work even when he wasn't here to do it.

That was all this was. Documentation.

He told himself this while marking a third position with his pencil. Small triangle, the symbol he'd been using for anything he categorized as *if anyone survives*—the places and features he thought had meaning that extended beyond his own immediate needs. He'd used the triangle twice before, in different parts of the city. He used it three times today, across three specific locations in the Meridian District, and drew faint connecting lines between them, and then drew a rough circle around the area they described.

He did not write anything inside the circle.

He looked at what he had drawn for a moment, pencil motionless.

Then he folded the map back along its original creases and put it away.

---

The descent took him through the top two stories of the garage, which he moved through quickly and low—the upper levels were open to the sky and that meant exposure. On the first floor, where walls still stood on three sides and the fourth was partially blocked by a concrete pillar, he stopped and drank the last of his morning water and ate from the ration bar he'd been portioning carefully for four days.

The city sounds from here were different than from the street. He had learned to catalog them the way he catalogued everything now: habituated until the categories were automatic. There was the constant background of settling structures—the creaks and distant impacts of buildings losing internal coherence, a sound that had been terrifying on Day Two and was merely ambient by Day Ten. There was wind through gaps, which had its own vocabulary of pitches he'd learned to read. There was the occasional sound of something moving at distance in the uninhabited blocks to the east—too consistent to be random debris, too irregular to be a territory marker. Something there. He had given it a wide berth.

And there was the silence that meant monster proximity, the specific absence of small animal sound that preceded a predator's passage.

He was not hearing that silence now. He was hearing the ordinary silences of a mostly-abandoned city in the middle of the day, which was as close to safe as Ashford got.

He ate the last third of the ration bar and thought about what he'd seen from the top.

The thing about the Meridian District was that it was too good. He had almost not let himself see it because things that were too good were usually wrong in a way you hadn't found yet, and his twenty-three days of survival had been built on finding the wrong part before it found him. But he had looked carefully, from angles he didn't normally use, and the wrongness was not here. The springs were real—he'd verified both. The chokepoints existed in the geography, not just in his imagination. The sightlines were what they were.

It was not trap. It was not claimed. It was just a district that had happened to be built on high ground by people who had thought about views before they thought about anything else, and had inadvertently created something that, in the new arithmetic of the world, was worth more than the glass towers and the harbors and the old market square combined.

He stood up and shouldered his pack.

There were things he could do in the Meridian District if he chose to stay. Clear the debris from the approach to the spring. Test the structural integrity of the building nearest the natural chokepoint—it looked like a former pharmacy, brick, three stories, the kind of construction that took decades to fail. Map the exact extent of the territory to the east, the one he'd been giving a wide berth, so he knew where its boundary ran.

He did not do any of these things.

He walked out of the garage and turned south, deeper into the city, toward the routes he had not yet fully mapped, and he told himself that what he had just done in the Meridian District was documentation only.

The scrap map rustled against his side.

---

The afternoon gave him two more verified water sources—a rain catchment someone had rigged on a fifth-floor fire escape and abandoned, and a basement cistern he accessed through a window well. He marked them both. He noted three more buildings that met his structural criteria and the approaches to each. He catalogued a monster territory boundary on the eastern side of the Meridian District that was thinner than he'd estimated—weaker than the western approaches, which matched the pattern he'd been building of how certain species established range.

He was thinking about whether that thinner boundary represented an opportunity or a symptom when he climbed back to high ground.

Not the garage this time. The building next to it—the former pharmacy, three stories, the one he had looked at from the garage deck and noted as structurally likely. He had not planned to check its upper floors. He had simply walked past it and stopped, because from outside it looked intact in the particular way that meant its roof would give him a view.

He climbed internal stairs, one hand on the rail, testing each step before committing. Second floor was clear—pharmacy shelves stripped clean, broken glass underfoot, the smell of disinfectant faded to ghost and under it the ozone of rift proximity. Third floor the same, cleaner because no one had been through it recently. The roof access was locked until he convinced it with the crowbar he kept at the top of his pack.

He stepped out into the afternoon.

The view from here was different from the garage. Lower, but the angle was slightly south of the earlier position, which gave him a look at the eastern commercial block that the garage had obscured.

He looked east.

He looked east for a long time.

The commercial block was there—derelict, intact, nothing unusual. Beyond it, the flat geometry of outer Ashford, the marginal blocks, the beginning of the wide open ground he'd been edging toward without quite leaving the city. And beyond that, at a distance he could not precisely calculate but estimated at somewhere between five and ten miles, something that he did not have a category for yet.

The horizon east of Ashford was not flat.

It had always been flat. He knew this the way he knew the soft fold-creases on his map—not from study but from lived familiarity, from years of looking east toward nothing in particular. The land east of the city was flat because it had always been flat, because it was the kind of place that geography had not bothered to interrupt with anything vertical.

There was something vertical there now.

He could not see it clearly enough to describe it in any detail—the light was wrong, afternoon haze and distance combining to blur it into something that might have been a building or a tower or a natural feature except that the natural features here had always been flat. It was too regular for ruin. It was too solid for rift-light. It sat at the edge of the visible world with a patience that had nothing to do with weather or time of day.

He looked at it for a long time.

Then he got out his map and marked the location—his best estimate of bearing and distance—with a question mark. He had been using question marks for things he could not categorize. The mile marker near Nora's highway, if he'd known to name it. The geometry of the Meridian District, before he'd committed to understanding it. The silver line on the wall that he'd found three days ago in the south quarter, running from floor to ceiling, a rift scar that the monster avoiding had decided was a territory marker.

A question mark for the thing on the horizon.

He looked at it one more time before climbing back down, trying to determine whether it had moved, whether it had changed while he watched, whether it was the same shape it had been when he'd first noticed it.

It was the same shape.

Still. Patient. Too regular.

*If anyone survives,* he thought, the triangles on his map the answer he was not saying aloud.

He descended the stairs and went to find somewhere to sleep, and he did not let himself think about the eastern horizon again until after he had secured his position for the night and verified that nothing had followed him to the Meridian District.

Then, in the dark, listening to the city settling around him, he thought about it anyway.

The shape on the horizon was there when he closed his eyes.

He did not know what that meant.

He suspected that when he found out, it would change the arithmetic again.

Everything had been changing the arithmetic. He was becoming, against his considerable will, a man who understood arithmetic differently than he had three weeks ago—who read buildings for survivability instead of address, who counted exits before he counted chairs, who noticed everything and recorded it because the recording was the only act that felt like it might survive him.

On the scrap map, three triangles in a circle, no words inside.

He slept.
