Cassian Reed woke to the sound of his neighbor's shower and the distant complaint of a garbage truck reversing down the alley. For three seconds the world was exactly what it had always been: cheap coffee, thin walls, the gray light of early autumn pressing against his blinds like a patient hand.

He lay still and listened. The radiator clicked. Someone on the floor below shouted at a dog. Ordinary. He had learned to treasure ordinary the way other people treasured holidays—brief, unreliable, never quite belonging to him for long.

His phone buzzed on the nightstand. A message from his mother, sent at five in the morning her time: *Call me when you can. Love you.*

Cassian rubbed his face and did not call. He would call tonight, after work, when he could perform the son she deserved—cheerful, employed, not the twenty-two-year-old omega who still lived alone in a studio apartment above a closed nail salon and ate dinner over the sink. She worried enough already.

He showered, dressed in the same rotation of shirts, and made coffee in a mug that had lost its handle years ago. The coffee tasted like burnt paper. He drank it anyway.

On the small table by the window he kept three things: a stack of overdue library books he kept renewing out of guilt, a photograph of himself at sixteen with his arm around a girl whose name he no longer remembered, and a city map folded so many times the creases had gone soft. He did not need the map to navigate his neighborhood. He kept it because maps promised that places connected—that if you knew where you were, you could find a way to somewhere else.

Outside, the air smelled like rain that had not yet decided to fall.

---

The bus was late, which was also ordinary.

Cassian stood at the stop with his headphones around his neck and watched commuters check their phones as if the screens might offer an explanation for the delay. A woman in a nurse's scrubs muttered about staffing shortages. A teenager scrolled through videos of animals behaving strangely—deer standing in highways, birds flocking in perfect spirals, a dog barking at the empty sky.

"Global warming," the nurse said, though no one had asked.

"Maybe," Cassian said, because agreeing was easier than conversation.

He did not mention the dream he had woken from twice this week: standing in an open field while the stars rearranged themselves, as if someone were turning the pages of a book written in light. He did not mention the headache that had lived behind his eyes since Monday, or the way colors seemed slightly too vivid when he looked at them too long, as though the world were being printed at the wrong resolution.

Some things you kept to yourself when you had spent your life learning that being an omega meant people already looked at you as if you were a problem waiting to be managed.

The bus arrived. He paid. He sat near the back and watched the city slide past—brick and glass, billboards for phones and insurance, a mural of a smiling family above a shuttered laundromat. Ashford. His city. Not beautiful, not terrible. Home in the only sense that mattered: he knew which corners to avoid after dark, which bodega sold the cheapest eggs, which bus routes still ran when the trains stalled.

At a red light his phone lit up with a news alert.

**UNUSUAL SOLAR ACTIVITY: SCIENTISTS URGE CALM**

He skimmed the article. Auroras reported at latitudes that should not see them. Minor GPS disruptions. A professor quoted saying the phenomenon was "within expected variance." The comments section called it a hoax, a government test, the end of days. Cassian closed the app and watched his reflection in the dark screen—a tired face, headphones around his neck, the same expression he wore when he pretended not to be afraid.

The end of days was a luxury belief. It required the conviction that your life mattered enough to be witnessed by history. Cassian had never been so fortunate.

---

He worked at a logistics office on the fourth floor of a building that smelled permanently of toner and someone's microwaved fish. His job was to reconcile shipping manifests—numbers in, numbers out, discrepancies flagged for someone more important to fix. It was dull enough that his mind could wander while his hands moved.

Today it wandered to rent.

Rent was due in six days. His supervisor had mentioned "streamlining" at the morning stand-up, which everyone understood to mean layoffs by another name. Cassian had smiled and taken notes and felt the old familiar tightness in his chest, the awareness that he was always one bad month from sliding off the edge of a life that already felt provisional.

At lunch he ate a sandwich at his desk and watched the sky through the window.

The clouds were wrong.

He could not have said how, exactly—not at first. They looked like clouds. Gray, layered, slow. But the light beneath them had a faint silvery edge, as if someone had drawn a fine line along each curve with metal ink. He blinked. Blinked again. The edge remained.

"Cassian."

He turned. Mara from accounting stood in his cubicle doorway holding two cups of coffee.

"You look like you saw a ghost," she said.

"Didn't sleep well."

She set one cup on his desk. Mara was a beta, older than him, kind in the practical way of people who had no time for performance. They had shared lunch twice. He had thought, once or twice, that friendship might be possible here—that he might stop being the quiet omega in the corner and become someone with a name people said with warmth.

"There's a guy on the news talking about birds," Mara said. "Thousands of them over the harbor. No one knows why."

"Migration?"

"Wrong season." She shrugged. "My kid says the sky looks weird. I told her to do her homework."

Cassian looked back at the window. The silvery edge along the clouds had deepened. For a moment he thought he heard something—a pressure in his ears, like the pop before a storm, but deeper, as if the sound were not in the air at all but in the bones of the building.

"Probably nothing," he said.

Mara smiled. "Probably."

She left. Cassian drank the coffee she had brought—better than his own—and returned to the manifests. Numbers. Order. The illusion that the world could still be balanced if you tried hard enough.

By three o'clock the office had begun to empty in the wrong way.

Not the orderly drift toward the elevators at quitting time, but a staccato rush—people standing mid-sentence, phones pressed to their ears, faces going flat with the particular blankness that meant bad news had arrived all at once. Someone shouted from the break room. Cassian's supervisor waved everyone toward the windows without explaining why, as if seeing it mattered more than being told.

Not the usual Friday drift toward weekend but something sharper: people standing in clusters, voices low, phones held up to windows. Cassian walked to the break room and found half the floor gathered around a television mounted above the vending machines.

On the screen, a helicopter circled a coastline he did not recognize at first. Then the caption registered: **ASHFORD HARBOR — LIVE.**

The water was black and flat. Above it, the sky had torn.

Not a metaphor. Not a special effect. A fracture—jagged, luminous, silver-white, hanging in the air like a wound that refused to close. It spanned the horizon. Light poured from it—not sunlight, not lightning, something colder and cleaner, and as Cassian watched, something moved inside the light.

Wing. Scale. A shape too large to be a bird.

Someone in the break room screamed.

The television cut to a studio anchor whose smile had cracked. "We are receiving unconfirmed reports of similar phenomena in—" The feed stuttered. The lights flickered. For one instant every screen in the room—phone, television, the microwave's digital clock—displayed the same image: a symbol like a compass rose made of broken glass.

Then the power died.

---

Darkness.

Emergency strips glowed along the floor. People shouted names. Cassian stood with his back against the wall and felt his heart hammering in a body that suddenly seemed too small for what was happening.

He thought of his mother. Of Mara. Of the library books he would never return and the map on his table that promised connection.

Someone said, "The stairs—everyone take the stairs—"

He moved with the crowd because moving was better than standing still. Down four flights in a press of breath and sweat, his palm skimming the rail, the building trembling once as if something enormous had stepped on the street outside.

The lobby was chaos. Security guards waved people toward the doors. Through the glass façade Cassian saw Ashford—not his Ashford. Cars stopped at unnatural angles. A bus had mounted the curb. And above the street, reflected in every window, the fracture spread like frost across glass.

The sound came then.

He would remember it for the rest of his life, however long or short that life turned out to be. It was not thunder. It was not a siren. It was a note—low, sustained, resonant—as if the sky itself were vibrating at a frequency the human ear was never meant to hear. It passed through the city. Through bone. Through thought.

People covered their ears. Cassian did not. He stood in the shattered lobby light and looked up through the glass and felt, with a clarity that terrified him, that the world he had woken into this morning was already gone.

The fracture widened.

Something descended through it—vast, winged, burning at the edges—and the glass exploded inward.

---

He did not remember deciding to run.

Later he would recall only fragments: elbow through a service door, stairs down to the alley, the smell of ozone and blood, a woman pulling a child by the hand, a man shouting that the phones were dead, that the phones were all dead. Cassian ran toward his apartment because it was the only direction that meant anything, because home was a habit even when home could no longer protect you.

The streets were filling. Sirens wailed and died as if the city were losing its voice. Above the rooftops the silver fractures multiplied—one, then three, then too many to count—connecting in a web that turned the afternoon into a twilight the color of old mirrors.

He saw his first monster on Fourth Street.

It had once been a dog. He was almost sure of that—the shape of the legs, the remnant of collar around a neck that had grown too thick. Now it was something else: muscle and wrong angles and eyes that reflected the fractured sky like coins. It crouched over a torn backpack and looked at Cassian with an intelligence that no animal should possess.

Cassian stopped.

The thing stopped.

For a heartbeat they regarded each other across the width of a world that had ended without asking permission. Then footsteps behind him—running, many—and the creature slid backward into shadow and was gone.

Cassian ran again.

---

His building still stood.

The nail salon below was dark. The stairwell smelled of dust and fear. He climbed to the fourth floor and found his door unlocked—the way he always left it, a habit he would never have again—and stepped into the studio that had been the whole of his adult life.

The mug without a handle sat on the counter. The map lay on the table. The photograph had fallen facedown.

He righted it with hands that shook.

The girl in the photograph had laughed when it was taken—he remembered that more than her face. Sixteen had felt like a door he was supposed to walk through into a life that would finally make sense. Instead he had walked into rent notices and quiet rooms and the careful habit of not wanting too much out loud. He set the frame back on the counter and stood there a moment longer than he needed to, as if the glass might keep the memory from sliding away with everything else.

From the window he could see half of Ashford. Smoke rose in columns. Something winged circled the distant towers. People moved in the streets like ants from a flooded hill, and above them all the sky held its wound open, silver and endless and utterly indifferent.

Cassian Reed stood at the glass and watched the last ordinary morning finish dying.

The note came again—fainter now, but still felt in his teeth, in the floorboards, in the air he breathed.

He thought of calling his mother. The phone showed no signal. He thought of Mara. Of the nurse at the bus stop. Of every person he had ever passed without speaking. He thought, with a grief that surprised him, of how much he had wanted to belong somewhere and how late it was to admit it.

Outside, a second shape fell through the fracture—larger than the first, slow as a descending sun—and the city screamed.

Cassian did not scream.

He pressed his forehead to the cool glass and closed his eyes and listened to the world break—the distant sirens stacking on one another, a car alarm that would not stop, someone on the street below praying in a language he did not know. When he opened them again the sky across the center of his window had cracked in a line so perfect and so bright that it looked like the earth had been folded in half and he was standing at the crease.

The last ordinary morning was over.

Whatever came next had already begun.

He would learn to count it, the way he counted everything else—one breath, one block, one day at a time—until counting became living and living became the only answer the world had left.

For one more second he let himself be the man who had reconciled shipping manifests and drank burnt coffee and believed the professor about expected variance. Then he stepped back from the glass and began to inventory what he had in the apartment that might still matter when morning came.
