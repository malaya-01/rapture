He wrote the rules on the inside of the barn door with a marker Petra had found in the supply cabinet.

Not rules in the legal sense — nothing he would enforce with punishment. Rules in the firefighter sense, which were closer to procedures: the conditions under which a structure could be considered safe for occupancy, the minimum requirements for a space to be called shelter rather than merely cover. He had spent nineteen years making and following this kind of rule, and the Rapture had not, he had found, made the practice less useful. If anything it had stripped the practice down to its reasons, the way a house fire stripped a house down to its reasons: what was necessary, what was not.

**BARN RULES — HALE HOLD**

*Watch rotations: 2 persons, 4-hour shifts, no exceptions. Sleep at other hours.*

*Noise discipline after dark: voices low, no generator past 22:00 except emergencies.*

*Food and water: communal stock, allocated by Elena. No private reserves without disclosure.*

*Exit protocol: pair minimum for any movement outside perimeter. Solo exit requires Rowan or Elena approval.*

*Signal: single candle in east window = all clear. No candle = emergency.*

*Medical: all injuries reported to Elena within 30 minutes regardless of severity.*

He stood back and read what he had written.

Eighteen people were living in the barn now, including his family of four. They had arrived in overlapping waves over eleven days — the six who had driven in from the highway on night eight, three more on day nine who had come from the east on foot with nothing but what they were carrying, Garrison with the infected arm who was still fighting his body's rebellion against whatever had happened to him before he found them. The most recent had arrived yesterday morning: a woman named Felicity who had been a nurse in the hospital three kilometers south and who had walked to the barn with a medical kit bag over each shoulder and the expression of someone who had been operating alone in a situation that required company and had run out of reasons to delay finding it.

Eighteen people, seventeen of whom were living. Garrison's arm had tracked the infection overnight despite Elena's antibiotics and her care and the clean water she had washed it with twice daily and the fever watch she had been conducting through every one of the eleven nights. On the morning of day eleven, before Rowan wrote the rules and before Elena came to stand beside him with her hand on the door frame, Garrison had died.

---

Elena told him at five in the morning, in the voice she used when she delivered information that she had processed alone before bringing to him: clinical in the structure, human at the edges.

"He went quietly," she said. "Around three. His fever broke at midnight and his breathing changed and by three it was done."

Rowan nodded.

He did not ask what he could have done differently because Elena would have told him already if there had been anything, and asking would be asking her to carry the question on top of what she had already been carrying.

He sat on the stool beside the radio rack and looked at the barn floor. Garrison had occupied the cot nearest the medical alcove — Elena's arrangement, so she could monitor him without crossing the barn in the dark. The cot was stripped now. She had done that too, before waking Rowan, because Elena understood that waking people to the visible evidence of loss was harder than waking them to loss itself.

"I told him," Rowan said.

"I know."

"Day four, when I said we'd work on it together."

"I know." She sat on the floor beside his stool, which was an Elena thing — she preferred the floor to chairs in difficult conversations, claimed it kept the conversation from becoming formal. "He knew what you meant. He didn't take it as a guarantee."

"I took it as a guarantee."

She was quiet a moment. "You took it as a direction of effort. That's what you can control."

The barn breathed around them. Seventeen people asleep, except for Kai and Petra on the watch rotation, whose soft footsteps at the perimeter were the rhythm Rowan had been sleeping to for eleven days. The generator was off for the night, the darkness reduced to the blue-white of the fracture light through the roof gaps he had not yet repaired.

He got up and went to write the rules on the barn door.

---

The morning was the morning that all mornings were now: inventory, watch change, the distribution of what Elena called breakfast and what was in fact a calorie count wrapped in the performance of normalcy. She had insisted on the performance of normalcy from day three, and he had argued with her about the resource cost and been wrong, as he was often wrong when Elena insisted on something: the performance was not about pretending the world was not what it was. It was about practicing the habits of people who intended to be here long enough for habits to matter.

They told the barn about Garrison after breakfast.

Rowan stood near the stove, as he had stood nine days ago when he told them no rescue was coming. He said it the same way: conversationally, without ceremony, because ceremony was for situations that had margin.

"Garrison passed last night. Elena was with him. He wasn't alone."

No one spoke immediately.

Then Theo said: "Do we — bury him, or—"

"I want to bury him," Rowan said. "We'll do it today. Before dark."

It was not the efficient choice. Efficient choices in their circumstances would have deferred burial for movement — the barn was not permanent, the diesel was not infinite, and the amount of time it took to inter a human being properly was time that could be spent on other necessities. Rowan knew the efficiency argument. He had been making efficiency arguments since day one and would make them again tomorrow.

Today was not an efficiency day.

He had promised a man effort. He had given it. The man had died anyway. The least the world owed him was a place in the ground that remembered he had been here.

"I'll dig," Denis said, and his brother Kai nodded beside him.

They dug in the meadow north of the barn where the soil was softer, in the tree line's edge where the root network offered some stability. Atlas helped, which Rowan allowed because Atlas needed to be useful and also because children had buried their dead since before written history and there was a kind of teaching in it that no book provided.

Leon watched from the barn doorway with his arms folded in the way he folded them when he was trying to sort something into the categories he was building for the new world. Rowan caught his eye once. Leon nodded, which with Leon meant *I see this. I'm recording it.*

Eighteen people stood in the meadow in the fracture light that was still silver even at midday, not sun-silver but wound-silver, and Weaver said a few sentences about life and worth and the unacceptability of forgetting. He had been a teacher for thirty-one years. He knew how to speak to a group that needed language for something that did not have language.

Rowan stood at the foot of the grave and said nothing because he had nothing to say that wasn't about failure, and failure was private.

Elena stood beside him. Their hands found each other.

---

The afternoon was logistics, because the afternoon was always logistics.

He formalized the watch schedule on paper and posted it beside the rules on the barn door: Kai and Denis, Theo and Priya, Cole and Desta were names he did not know yet but had added since receiving them from the sixth-day arrivals. He assigned everyone a rotation, asked for objections, received two — Priya, who said her shoulder was injured and couldn't carry the crowbar for four hours; Weaver, who said he couldn't stay awake past nine at night — and adjusted both without comment.

Competence-mapping was an exercise he had first learned at the department as a probie and had refined over nineteen years: find out what each person did best, build the rotation around the best rather than the average. You did not put the injured woman on the night watch with a heavy weapon. You did not schedule the retired teacher on the shift that required staying awake at hours his body had already decided it would not.

Atlas followed him through the afternoon at a distance that meant he was watching the process without being obvious about watching it. Rowan let him watch. He had been letting Atlas watch since the barn, since Miller's Cutoff, since the kitchen on the morning the sky broke and Atlas had appeared in the doorway and pointed east.

"What are you looking for?" he asked, without turning.

"How you know what to do," Atlas said.

Rowan thought about this. "I mostly don't know. I know what I've done before and I know what the people I've learned from have done, and I know the outcome of both, so when something new comes up I try to find the closest thing to an old situation I've already solved."

"What if there isn't one?"

"Then you make a new one, and you write it down so you have it the next time."

Atlas was quiet for a moment. "Garrison wasn't in any old situation."

"No."

"So what do you write down?"

Rowan stopped and turned. Atlas was nine years old with grass in his hair and binoculars around his neck and the expression of someone asking a question he had been carrying for days.

"Infection, resource constraints, time," Rowan said. "What we had, what we needed, what the outcome was. So the next person in the same situation has more information than I did."

"Even though it was bad?"

"*Especially* because it was bad." He put his hand briefly on Atlas's shoulder. "Bad outcomes are more information than good ones. Good outcomes tell you something worked. Bad outcomes tell you what to try next time."

Atlas thought about this for the rest of the afternoon. Rowan watched him thinking about it in the way he watched Leon approach arguments: sitting with it, turning it over, not answering until he believed the answer.

---

At dusk, after the watch was set and the generator cut and the barn settled into its night rhythm, Rowan stood at the door and listened for the engines he had been expecting since yesterday.

He had heard them twice — a distant diesel sound, east of their position, consistent with the kind of vehicle that moved slowly through damaged roads. Too regular for monster transit, too purposeful for random movement. Someone with a vehicle and fuel and a direction, moving through the farmland east of the barn's tree line with what Rowan estimated was intention rather than desperation.

They came at twenty minutes after seven.

He heard the engine first and then the lights at the end of the access track — not the bright lights of a vehicle running full power but the directed single-light of a vehicle running on caution, someone who had learned that light was a resource and an advertisement simultaneously. One vehicle, not two. Slowing on the approach.

He stepped into the doorway.

The engine stopped.

Silence from the east. Then footsteps on the track — one person, moving at the deliberate pace of someone who had thought about what pace to use.

A woman appeared at the edge of the clearing. She was alone. She was not in obvious distress, which was its own information. She stopped at the clearing's edge and looked at the barn with the expression of a person assessing what they had found before committing to it.

"Whoever's watching from the door," she said, at the volume that was louder than quiet and quieter than announcement, "I have a vehicle with fuel and first aid stock from a clinic I cleared on day six. I'm alone. I can't get the vehicle much further north without someone who knows these roads."

Rowan studied her for a moment. She was watching him study her with the patience of someone used to being assessed in emergencies.

"Name?" he asked.

"Felicity."

"Medical training?"

"Nurse. Twelve years. The clinic I cleared — I have its full pharmaceutical inventory in the back of the vehicle."

Rowan was quiet.

Outside, in the meadow to the north, the new grave in the tree line held its occupant in the dark. Behind him, Elena's footsteps approached from the medical alcove where she had spent the last hour reorganizing supplies around the absence of the patient she had lost.

"Come into the clearing," he said. "Leave the vehicle where it is for now."

She came.

He stepped back from the door and let Elena come forward, because this was Elena's domain and because they were partners rather than command structure and because sometimes the most correct thing a leader could do was know when to step to the side.

He heard Elena's voice — warm, precise, the voice she used for people who were competent and afraid and needed their competence recognized before their fear addressed — and then the sounds of entry and the quiet that meant someone had been given a cot and a space and the knowledge that tonight they were not alone.

Rowan went back to the radio.

He cycled through the frequencies. The recorded messages were degrading — the voice on the emergency channel had developed an artifact at *do not approach* that made it sound less like a warning and more like a sentence unfinished. The shortwave gave him three stations of noise and one of silence and nothing that counted as information.

He turned it off and wrote in his log: *Day 11. Garrison — buried, north meadow. Watch schedule formalized, posted. Arrival: Felicity, nurse, vehicle, clinic pharmaceutical stock. Group: 18. Rules posted. Status: stable.*

He paused.

*Promise kept: partial. Garrison died. Effort was real. Outcome not in my control.*

He closed the log.

He had learned, in nineteen years, that the difference between a firefighter who kept doing the job and one who stopped was not whether people died. People died. The difference was what you did with the accounting.

He noted it. He moved on.

Tomorrow required him.
