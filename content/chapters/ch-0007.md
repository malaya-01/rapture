By the sixth night Cassian Reed had learned the city's new voice.

It was not a thing that happened quickly or cleanly—the learning. It accumulated: one hour of listening added to the next, each night giving him more data than the one before, so that by now he had a rough taxonomy of the sounds his building made versus the sounds the street made versus the sounds that were neither, the ones that had no precedent in his catalog of ordinary city noise.

He sat at the kitchen table with the battery lantern on low—he had learned to keep it at low, which extended its life and gave his eyes enough time in the dark to stay calibrated—and listened.

Familiar: the building settling. The radiator, which had not produced heat since the power went down but still made its tick-and-sigh when temperature changed. The wind coming off the harbor, which was different since Monday, carrying a salt-and-ozone smell that coated the back of the throat. His own breathing.

Unfamiliar: the shuffling on the street below. The patterns it made: irregular, overlapping, slow. He had developed a theory about the things that shuffled—not knowledge, just hypothesis—that they navigated by sound or smell, that they had a range they patrolled and a radius beyond which they either lost interest or encountered something else's territory. He had not tested the hypothesis. He was not ready to test the hypothesis.

The other unfamiliar: the thing that flew over the building at irregular intervals, low enough that the wingbeats were audible as a deep rhythmic pulse. He had not looked at it. He had decided on the first night that looking at it from the fourth floor would cost him a window and possibly the window frame and he was not prepared to lose either.

He recorded all of this in the back pages of the library book he had never returned—the only one with blank end pages, a history of maritime cartography whose irony was not lost on him—using a mechanical pencil he had found in his desk and recording in the smallest handwriting he could manage, to preserve space.

Observations. Times. Durations.

He did not know what the observations were for. He made them anyway. They were what he had.

---

He had been careful with the food.

Day One: panic eating, which he had recognized as panic eating and corrected. Day Two: a proper inventory, everything laid out on the counter in order of perishability, the list he had made with the precision he'd practiced on shipping manifests. Day Three through Six: the list in action—first the things that would go bad, then the things that kept, with the smaller portions he had known intellectually were the right approach and had to practice emotionally.

He was good at this. This surprised him.

He had not thought of himself as someone with survival competence. He had thought of himself as someone who reconciled shipping manifests and lived alone above a nail salon and ate dinner over the sink. These skills were not obviously applicable to an apocalypse.

But the manifest work had given him something he had undervalued: the ability to look at a collection of quantities and understand the rate at which they were depleting and project the point at which depletion became a problem. He had applied this to food the way he had applied it to the quarterly discrepancy reports, and the result was an accounting that was frightening but not yet desperate.

Water was the constraint. He had three days left at his current rate, which meant he needed to go out tomorrow and he needed to find significantly more.

He thought about the corner store he had cleared on Day Three. The cases he had carried back—heavy, worth it, the back of his thighs still sore. The store might have more in the back room. There were other stores on the block he had not checked.

He wrote: *Tomorrow, water. Second run to the corner store + adjacent block east. Leave by 0800, back before noon. Know the route. Know the alternates.*

He looked at what he had written. He was making plans in the same format he used for the manifests: task, time, route, contingency. He had never expected this to be the thing that kept him organized at the end of the world, but here they were.

He turned the lantern down to minimal and listened to the street.

---

The evening was divided into phases he had learned to read.

Dusk was the loudest. Whatever was in the street—the shufflers, the things with wrong shapes he had glimpsed at the edge of his window's view—was most active in the transition between day and dark, as if the shift in light reorganized them in some way. He had been barricaded with the curtains drawn by four in the afternoon on every day since Day Two, which meant he was never visible in the window when the activity peaked.

He had learned this the hard way. Day Two evening: a mistake of five minutes, the curtain not quite closed, the lantern still on medium because he had not yet understood the relationship between the light inside and the attention outside. Nothing had come through the window. But something had stopped below it and stood very still for almost twenty minutes, and during those twenty minutes Cassian had not moved and had barely breathed, and afterward he had sat against the wall farthest from the window and shaken for an interval he chose not to quantify.

Full dark was quieter.

Not quiet—that was the important distinction. The city at night still had sounds, still had traffic of some kind, still had evidence of things moving through it. But the movement was different. Less territorial, more directional. Things going somewhere rather than patrolling.

He had heard, twice, sounds that were unambiguously human. A voice, distant, once. The sound of a door on the third floor below, once, that he had been unable to explain and had been unable to investigate and had filed in the back of his mind under *information I'm not ready to use yet*.

He was aware that isolation was making his assessments of his own decisions unreliable. This was something he had read about—not survival literature, just general psychology, the things you absorbed through the proximity of education and office conversation. Isolation changed how you thought. It made the options in front of you feel more exhaustive than they were, made the walls feel more permanent.

He tried to counter it by making the lists. The lists required engagement with specific reality: this much water, this many days, this route, this alternate. You could not make a list about anything general. Lists were inherently local and specific and useful.

He made a list of everything he remembered about his neighborhood—not the current neighborhood, the pre-Rapture one. Every business. Every building's profile. Places with high walls, places with water access, places you could see multiple approaches from. He was not sure why he was making this list. He put it in the maritime cartography book with the others.

---

He fell asleep sitting up against the kitchen wall at some point after midnight and woke at three to a sound he had not cataloged.

Scraping.

Not the building-settle creak. Not the wind. Not the shuffling from the street that was two floors down and filtered through concrete. This was at his door—his door, the hollow-core interior door of apartment 4C with the brass knocker that had come with the apartment and the deadbolt he had thrown every night for three years out of habit and had thrown every night this week with a fervor he had not previously had reason for.

He did not move.

The scraping was slow. Regular. The sound of something moving along the surface of the door with deliberate contact—not random, not accidental. Not trying to open the door. Just—touching it. Feeling along it. Learning its dimensions.

His heart did what hearts do when something like this happens: a percussive overreaction, a flood of his own blood sound into his ears. He counted his heartbeats to find the other sounds beneath them. He had read once that you could think through panic if you gave it a task—not to suppress it, just to redirect it. He counted.

One. Two. Three.

The scraping continued.

He was aware of the fire iron from the nail salon, which was propped against the wall near the door. He was aware that getting the fire iron required moving, and moving required sound, and he did not know the hearing range of what was on the other side of the door. He stayed still.

Four. Five. Six.

A pause.

During the pause, the building was absolutely silent. The radiator had stopped its tick. The wind had, briefly, stopped. He was aware of the sound of his own breathing in a way that made it feel impossibly loud, and he breathed slower, pulled each breath from farther down, made himself a smaller sound in the silence.

The scraping resumed.

Lower now—the sound had moved, was coming from roughly knee height on the door. Something exploring the base of the door, the gap between the door and the floor. The gap was small—he had put a rolled towel against it two nights ago, partly for draft and partly because he had had the instinct that the gap was a vulnerability and had not wanted to think about why.

He held his breath.

The scraping stopped.

He did not know how long he held it. Long enough that when he finally let it out his vision grayed briefly at the edges, the way it did when you stood too fast. He breathed again, careful, controlled.

Silence at the door.

He waited.

Thirty minutes. He could not have said this with certainty, but he counted in a different currency—the number of times the wind moved through the building, the cycle of something on the street below, his own heartrate slowing back toward normal by degrees. Thirty minutes in that currency.

Nothing.

He did not go to the door. He did not look through the peephole, because the peephole meant pressing his face against the door, which meant being close to whatever might still be on the other side. He stayed against the wall with his knees drawn up and the lantern off—he had turned it off the moment he woke, some reflex he was grateful for—and waited.

---

Dawn came the way it came now: wrong.

Not ugly—the fracture glow faded with the true light and the morning had a quality he had no name for, cleaner than the days before the Rapture but different, the sky a color between blue and silver that no atmospheric index he knew accounted for. Through the curtain edge he watched the light change until it was unambiguously morning.

He checked the door from a distance.

Nothing visible through the peephole's limited circle. He crouched and looked at the base of the door: the rolled towel was still in place, undisturbed. The door's surface, which he inspected afterward by running his hand along it—three inches of exploration, not full contact—had marks he did not remember being there. Shallow, almost like fingernails on wood, in a horizontal line at about waist height.

He did not know what had been there.

He stood at the kitchen threshold for a long time with the granola bar untouched, listening to the building wake in small sounds—pipes, wind, a door on the floor below that might have been wind or might have been someone he could not afford to hope for. The silver line he had seen in the stairwell on Day Four still lived in his memory: a thread of light across concrete that monsters would not cross. He had no proof it would hold again. He had no proof the thing at his door had been a monster at all. Only the scrape, patient as a fingernail on wood, and the held breath, and the hour between midnight and dawn when he understood that night now belonged to something that did not sleep on human schedules.

He thought of the boy at the market who had said *they ate Mr. Chen*. He thought of Mara. He put both thoughts in the same locked room where he kept grief that did not help with water.

He stood in his kitchen in the morning light and ate half a granola bar and drank his measured cup of water and thought about what he knew and what he needed to do.

He had been alone for six days. He had been quiet and careful and mostly successful. He had a door that still held and food for a week and water for three days and the list he had made last night about tomorrow's route.

He needed to stop thinking of the city as the city and start thinking of it as terrain. He needed to understand it the way a field had to be understood before you crossed it—elevations, cover, sight lines, where the predators fed.

He was the only person in his building. He was possibly the only person on his block. He had not yet spoken to a living human being since the morning Mara from accounting had said *your kid says the sky looks weird* and given him a cup of coffee and gone back to her desk.

He thought about Mara. About whether she had gotten out. About whether he would have gotten out if he'd had someone to go with.

He put it away. He was very good at putting things away when they didn't help with the immediate accounting.

He had water to find. He had a route planned. He had a day with a beginning and a task and a direction.

He had survived the first night that mattered.

He made a new list.

Tomorrow: water first. Then the stairwell silver line—test whether it still held. Then silence, if silence was still a luxury he could afford.

He slept in short intervals, waking to check the door and the window and the silver line in the stairwell that had not yet reached his floor. Each time the building was still empty. Each time that emptiness felt less like luck and more like a condition he would have to learn to carry.
