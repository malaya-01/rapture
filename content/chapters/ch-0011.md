The shelves had been picked clean in the patterns that told you which decisions people made first.

Cassian had been studying the pattern for two days. Not systematically — he lacked Marcus Vale's instruments and Marcus Vale's habit of formal documentation, though he had arrived, by a different route, at the same practice of writing things down as a way of making them real enough to use. His notation was cruder: pencil on folded paper, in the margins of his receipts and the inside covers of the library books he had started carrying because libraries were mostly untouched and the books about history and architecture contained more survival-relevant information than anyone had apparently realized when they were printed.

The pattern he had documented across three scavenging routes:

*Zone 1 (first looted): High-value basics. Water, canned protein, batteries, first aid. Taken fast, taken all. Nothing here.*

*Zone 2 (second wave): Specialty and misunderstood items. Nutrition supplements, water purification, dried goods for diets nobody thought they'd be eating. Partially taken. Work these areas.*

*Zone 3 (still available): Things people didn't identify as useful. Institutional quantities of individual items nobody grabbed because they grabbed in emergency-consumer quantities rather than emergency-sustenance quantities. Condiment packs. Multivitamins mistaken for supplements. Foil pouches of things requiring cooking that people didn't think they'd be cooking.*

He was in Zone 3 today, which meant a grocery distribution warehouse on the edge of the university district — not a public-facing store but a wholesale supplier he had identified from a manifest discrepancy at his old job: eighteen months ago he had reconciled a shipping record for a produce company that delivered to this building, and the address had stayed in his mind the way addresses stayed when you had spent years building a city map from scattered data points.

The warehouse's loading dock door had been rolled partially open — by the last delivery, by refugees, by someone who had known what it held. He went in low and cautious and found, in the back aisles past the stripped fresh-produce shelving and the empty refrigeration units, what the pattern had predicted: commercial quantities of things nobody thought to take.

Industrial pallets of canned tomatoes. Forty-eight-count boxes of rice packages. Vats of oil. Institutional coffee.

He took what he could carry. Made two loads to a point forty meters from the dock, built a small cache behind a collapsed shelving unit he had braced back into place, and covered it with a flattened cardboard box that would look like debris from the dock entrance.

He would come back.

---

The woman was waiting when he came back from the second load.

Not at his cache — she did not know about the cache; she was at the dock entrance, standing with the specific posture of someone who had made the same calculation he had and arrived at the same warehouse through different means. Late twenties, maybe thirty. Dark coat that had been warm three days ago when the coat was clean and dry. A child's backpack slung over one shoulder — the kind with cartoon characters on it, clearly not hers, clearly borrowed or found.

She was looking at the shelving in the Zone 2 section. She had not yet found the back aisles.

She heard him and turned fast, hands coming up — not threatening, the hands-up gesture of someone afraid rather than aggressive, the gesture that meant *I'm not a threat, please don't be one either.*

"I'm not going to hurt you," Cassian said, which was both true and what anyone could say.

She studied him. He watched her study him — taking in the bag, the clothing, the utility knife on his belt that he had put there not from bravado but from the logic that it should be visible rather than surprising. The visible knife said *I have thought about this* rather than *I am armed and panicking*.

"The back aisles," he said. "Commercial stock. Untouched."

She held still.

"Rice. Oil. Canned goods. Industrial quantities." He shifted the bag on his shoulder. "You're welcome to come look."

She came. She kept two meters of space between them, which was correct and which he respected by not closing it. He showed her the aisle without walking ahead, moving to the side so she could see past him. She went forward and her face did the specific thing faces did when the math of survival improved unexpectedly: a relaxation, small and controlled, as if the body were quietly releasing a calculation it had been holding for ten days.

She had a child. She had not said so, but the backpack said so, and the way her eyes moved when she found the rice pallets — not triumph, relief — said it more clearly.

"I have a cache," Cassian said. "Outside. I was building a second load. I wasn't planning to share, I want to be honest about that, but I can't carry all of this and you—"

"I have a boy," she said. "Six. He's with a neighbor."

"I know."

She looked at him.

"The backpack," he said.

She looked down at the cartoon backpack. Then she sat on the warehouse floor and laughed — a single sound, brief and exhausted, the kind of laugh that was not about anything funny but about the particular relief of being seen accurately rather than managed. "Armando," she said. "His name is Armando."

"Cassian."

"Priti."

They divided the cache in thirds: one for her, one for his apartment, one left in the warehouse behind the cardboard under the collapsed shelving for the next person who made the same calculation and arrived here with the same pattern knowledge. He did not explain this third division. She watched him make it and said nothing, which he took as understanding.

She went east. He went north. The city between them absorbed both directions equally, the way cities absorbed individual decisions in the way they always had — one person's choice disappearing into the aggregate of choices that had no visible relationship except in retrospect.

He did not know if he would see Priti again.

He knew she had a boy named Armando who was six and waiting with a neighbor.

He wrote it in the inside cover of the architecture library book: *Priti. Armando, 6. East. Third cache in warehouse behind dock, flattened cardboard.*

---

The radio was in a hardware shop on the corner of Third and Mercer, behind a dropped ceiling tile in the manager's office that he found by noticing the acoustic difference between one section of ceiling and the rest — a skill the manifest job had built without intending to, the habit of noticing discrepancies in surfaces that should have been uniform.

It was a shortwave set, professional grade, the kind a store manager used to monitor weather channels for supply runs. It had a power cable and a small independent battery pack that showed sixty-three percent charge.

He sat on the floor of the manager's office for a while with the radio in his lap before he turned it on.

Sixty-three percent was finite. Whatever it gave him, it gave him once, in some proportion he could not calculate without knowing the hardware's drain rate. After that it was either solar power, which he had no equipment for, or the hand-crank, which worked but charged a smaller battery than this unit held and would take time.

He turned it on.

He cycled through the frequencies he knew from nine days of the hand-crank unit — the dead emergency channels, the locations of the recorded messages, the positions of the ache-frequency that he had filed as *other-language carrier* in his notation. He found the ache-frequency quickly; it was on the same band it had been on since day one, which meant it was not moving and not degrading. He moved past it.

He found a human voice on a frequency he had not reached before — too high for his hand-crank's range, accessible only because this unit could reach it. A man's voice, damaged by static, repeating a location he could not map and instructions for reaching it that assumed landmarks he did not know.

He wrote everything down. The voice ended mid-sentence — either the transmission ended or the unit at the other end had run out of battery or something else had happened to it — and static replaced it.

He moved through the upper bands.

At forty-five minutes into his session he found what the volume had called static and distant screaming and what he would call, in his field notation, the day's second discovery: a frequency occupied not by voices but by a sound that built from white noise into something that had the rhythm of a carrier signal but the texture of breath. In, out. In, out. As if the radio were listening to something breathing from very far away, or to something that breathed on a scale larger than a room.

He noted the frequency. He did not stay on it long.

At fifty-eight minutes he turned the radio off.

He sat in the manager's office in the dark warehouse for a moment before he shouldered his bag.

The frequency with the breathing sound bothered him in a way he could not quite classify. Not fear — fear had a more immediate quality, a specificity about what would happen next. This was more like the feeling he had experienced looking at the fracture on the first day from the window of his building: the sense of a scale that exceeded the category he was trying to fit it into.

He wrote: *Radio found, office at Third/Mercer. Charge 63% remaining. Human voice broadcast found on upper band — location unclear, transmission ended. Third frequency: rhythmic signal, possible biological origin, scale unclear. Will return.*

He put the notebook away.

He had one more stop before dark.

---

The apartment building he had marked on day eight was on the corner of Western — a six-story residential structure with a recessed ground floor that made it less accessible from street level, intact upper floors visible from the outside, and a water main that still showed pressure when he checked the building's laundry room tap on his first pass-through three days ago. The tap had gone dry since, but the main line below it still had pressure differential that meant water was in the pipe even if it wasn't moving.

He was on the third floor doing what he had been doing every other day — walking the perimeter, checking the stairwell access points, sitting with the city map and the list of intersections he had marked — when he heard the sound from below.

Not the wet patience of the changed ones. Not the intermittent rhythm of something injured.

Voices.

Two, possibly three. Low, keeping the same volume discipline he kept, the carefully calibrated quiet of people who had learned that volume was a resource you spent rather than wasted.

He moved to the stairwell and listened.

They were in the lobby. He could not make out words — not because the sound was too quiet but because they were deliberately fragmentary, checking each room with words that were more code than conversation. He identified *clear* and something that sounded like *second floor* and a third sound that might have been a name.

He went down.

Not fast. Not the two-at-a-time pace of emergency — the deliberate pace of someone making a choice about whether to be visible. He stopped on the second floor landing where the stairwell window gave a partial view of the lobby.

Two people. Not the changed ones. Not the look of the changed ones, which by now he could identify from posture alone — the way they held their weight, the way they moved without micro-hesitations, without the constant environmental scanning of people who were afraid and managing their fear.

These two were afraid and managing it.

He stepped to the stairwell railing and said: "I've been using this building for three days. Stairwells are clear. Third floor has a water source — laundry room, but the pressure dropped. Ground floor entry is quiet if you come from the alley."

The two people in the lobby looked up.

A man and a woman. Mid-thirties, both. The woman had a cut on her forehead taped with something that had not been intended for that purpose. The man held a crowbar in a grip that relaxed when he heard Cassian's voice and then tightened again with the specific tension of someone who had learned the hard way that voices were not sufficient evidence.

"How do you know it's clear?" the man asked.

"I checked it this morning."

"Things move."

"Yes," Cassian agreed. "I'll check it again. You can watch me do it."

They watched him check the stairwells and both landing halls. They were watching the way Cassian had watched the grave-walker outside his door on the first night: not for information about the searcher, but for information about what the searcher found.

He found nothing. He said so.

They came up to the third floor and he showed them the laundry room and the adjacent apartment with the least-broken window. He did not offer them anything from his bag. That would come later, if later happened, if the calculation added up. What he offered was the building, which was not his to offer and which he offered anyway because the only math that made sense was the math that included people who were awake and watching in a building he had already been alone in for too long.

Their names were Cole and Desta.

Cole had driven supply trucks. Desta had been finishing a degree in civil engineering before the sky broke. Cole knew three routes east of here that he had scouted in two days of moving before they found this building. Desta knew which buildings' foundations were most likely to hold in conditions that included the kind of ground shifts that had been happening since day one.

Cassian added this to the inside cover of the architecture book: *Cole (supply routes, east). Desta (structural assessment, foundation). Building Third/Western, third floor.*

He went home at dusk with a heavier bag and a lighter feeling that he could not quite name except as something opposite to the sensation he had had on the first night, sitting on the floor with his back against the door, thinking about the length of his contact list.

The shelves were mostly empty.

The city was not.

He ate half a protein bar over the map with his pencil in his hand and marked three more buildings he had not marked before: not for their defensibility, though he was noting that too, but for the evidence of occupation he had seen in them as he passed — a curtain moved from the inside, a methodical light moving through a window on an upper floor, a sound from a sealed room that had the quality of people keeping quiet rather than of people not being there.

He was not alone.

He was not connected.

Those were not the same thing, and the distance between them was not fixed.

He wrote: *Day 10. Not a bad day. Add to that.*

---

He found the shortwave again at the end of the evening, static and one clear burst of that breathing-rhythm sound before the frequency settled into white noise. He did not know what it was. He wrote: *breathing freq again. consistent. location? direction?*

He turned the radio off at thirty percent.

He lay on the floor of his apartment — he had stopped sleeping in the bed, which had the disadvantage of height and the disadvantage of requiring him to move further to reach the door if something required him to move — and looked at the city map tacked to the inside of the closet door where he could see it from the floor.

The circles he had made — twelve of them now, buildings and intersections and one parking lot at Calloway and Third — made a pattern that was not yet a plan.

He looked at it until sleep arrived.

In the morning he would add to it.
