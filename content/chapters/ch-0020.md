## I — Adrian

He had been moving for four days with no one.

Not by choice, exactly — by the mathematics of the route. The twelve kilometers from the interchange to the eastern congregation point had not been twelve kilometers of safe road. They had been twelve kilometers of three detours and one night shelter in a locked building that had the particular combination of features he had learned to identify as *livable for one night*: accessible from above, no compromised walls on the east or south, defensible against the kind of creature activity that preferred not to force entry if it could simply wait. He had spent one night there and moved at dawn.

The congregation point had Selene's trail in it, which was the third time he had found her trail in the past week and arrived after the person had moved on. He had begun to develop a specific relationship with evidence of recent occupation — the warmth of a fire that told him hours, the organization of bedding that told him intention, the direction of departure that told him next.

The school gymnasium had been three days ahead of him. The intake ledger he had found showed a silvery-haired woman running intake — for three days, ending on day sixteen when she had departed east.

He was going east.

He had been going east for so long that east had stopped being a direction and become a practice: the continuous reorientation toward wherever the evidence pointed, the adjustment of each day's route to the best available information. He was not lost and he was not despairing and he was not certain, and the not-certainty had become, over nineteen days of practice, something he could live in.

This was new. He had not been good at uncertainty before the Rapture. He had been good at clarity: this is the person in danger, this is the exit, this is the most direct path. He had always needed the direct path. Selene used to joke that he navigated their relationship the way he navigated city streets — direct route, adequate planning, low tolerance for scenic detours.

He thought about this as he walked and found it, in the current context, funny in the way things were funny when the only alternative was not funny at all.

He was learning the scenic detour.

He was still going east.

At the junction where the eastern road split — north toward the secondary cluster of congregation points, south toward a route he had not yet scouted — he found fresh boot prints in the dried mud at the road's edge.

Small boot. Deliberate stride — not panicked, not wandering. Someone who knew where they were going.

He studied the prints for a moment. Then he looked north.

The north road held more uncertainty and more distance. The south road held evidence of recent human transit.

He went north, because north was where the evidence had been pointing for two days, and the boot prints in the mud were not Selene's size.

He thought: I will find her.

He adjusted his pack and walked into the morning.

## II — Marcus

The choice had been Iris's.

Not entirely — nothing was entirely one person's choice when you were a family — but the shape of it was hers, as the shapes of the important decisions in their shared life had usually been: Marcus provided analysis, Iris provided direction, and between the two they moved through things that would have paralyzed either of them alone.

She had said, on the evening of day eighteen: "We need to be somewhere more permanent."

He had agreed.

He had laid out the options as he understood them: the eastern congregation point, which had resources and population but whose stability had been declining according to the information reaching them; the barn cluster in the outskirts, the *Hale Hold* that had appeared on the shortwave and whose radio broadcasts described a level of organization he found credibly exceptional; or, the option he had been thinking about since day fourteen, the position northeast of the gymnasium where the compass had pointed and where his triangulation suggested a mana source.

"Which one?" Iris asked.

"I want to go northeast," he said.

"Why?"

He thought about how to explain what the compass told him — not in terms of measurement, but in terms of what measurement meant. "The source the compass is pointing at is stationary. Stationary mana sources are — stable. They don't move because they're anchored to something. And if I can find what they're anchored to, I can understand the mana system better, which means I can—"

"Protect Lily," Iris said.

"Yes."

She was quiet.

"And help people," he added, which was also true.

"How far northeast?"

"Three to four kilometers from our current position. Based on the triangulation."

She looked at Lily, who was asleep on the gymnasium cot with her arm curled around her drawing satchel. "What's there?"

"I don't know yet."

"Unsafe unknown or unknown unknown?"

"Unknown unknown. No creature activity indicators from that direction. The compass deviation is consistent — not a fracture signature, something else. Stable."

Iris thought. He watched her think, the same face she had made over the map on every holiday they had ever planned: the face of someone weighing what was known against what was needed.

"We go in the morning," she said.

They packed that night, before Lily woke. They left what they couldn't carry in the gymnasium's communal stock, because leaving things rather than taking things was the practice and Iris had built the practice into their family the way she built everything — quietly, consistently, until it was simply what they did.

In the morning they said goodbye to Jemison, who received the news of their departure with the expression of a man who had stopped being surprised by departures and had started being surprised by the opposite.

"If you find something worth knowing about northeast," he said, "send word."

"First thing," Marcus said.

They went northeast.

Lily walked between them with her satchel on her back and her compass — he had let her keep one of the hiking compasses — in her hand, watching the needle with the focused attention of a child performing a task she had been given and taking it seriously.

"It's pointing northeast," she said.

"I know."

"Are we going toward the thing it points at?"

"We're going toward where I think the thing is."

She was quiet for a moment, navigating a section of damaged sidewalk with the care of a child who had learned to assess surfaces. "What if the thing is dangerous?"

"Then we stop before we reach it."

"How will you know?"

He thought about this. "You'll tell me."

She looked up at him.

"You can see things I can't," he said. "If it looks wrong when we get closer, you tell me and we stop."

She looked down at the compass again. The needle held steady. "Okay," she said.

They walked northeast.

The city gave way to the in-between space at its edge: less building, more empty ground, the gradual transition from urban to not-urban that cities made at their margins. The mana source, if his triangulation was correct, was somewhere in this space — not far, not deep into the wilderness that Nora was mapping from the ridge, but in the intermediate zone where the city had not quite finished being city and the world had not quite begun being something else.

He kept his notebook open.

He was the first person to approach a mana source deliberately, without instruments designed for it, with a six-year-old navigator, and an equipment kit built from salvage and improvisation.

He recorded this fact without ceremony, because ceremony was for situations with more margin.

He walked toward what the compass pointed at and wrote down everything he saw.

## III — Selene

She left the gymnasium on the morning of day nineteen.

Not alone. A group of twelve had been forming since day seventeen around the shared intention of going east — not to the next congregation point, which was known and accounted for, but past it, to the larger cluster that the broadcasts from the north had been describing for four days: a road junction community that had apparently reached the minimum critical mass of resources and organization to begin functioning as something more than temporary shelter.

She had been organizing the group the way she organized everything — not by taking control but by observing what was needed and moving to fill it. The group needed a provisioning list before departure: she made one. The group needed clarity on the route: she sourced it from Ximena's map, which had been shared by Ximena before her departure with Marcus and Iris, and updated the group's copy. The group needed to know what to do if they encountered creature activity: she built the protocol from what she had observed and what the north broadcasts had described, and rehearsed it with the group the way she had rehearsed refugee intake protocols at university, efficiently and without drama.

On the morning of day nineteen, twelve people stood at the gymnasium's east exit with packs organized and route agreed and Selene at the front.

She had Adrian's grey scarf in her jacket pocket. She had had it since the intake ledger entry — not because he had been there, but because Dav had described it, and the woman who ran the intake at the congregation point three days east had produced it from the table drawer and said a brown-haired man had left it for whoever came looking.

Evidence. Still east.

She thought about the year she had spent in Adrian's apartment above the bakery, in the city that was now burning behind her. She thought about the morning coffee, the laptop, the statistics that were personal enemies, the private smile. She thought about his voice saying *count with me, four in* on the floor of the overpass in the dark.

She thought: *I love him. I am going toward him. These are not the same thing as being together yet, but they are the same direction.*

She stepped out of the gymnasium doorway.

Twelve people followed.

The road went east.

Above them, the fractured sky held its wounds with the patience of something that had been waiting for longer than any of them had been waiting and did not intend to stop.

Selene walked.

She organized where organization was needed and observed where observation mattered more, and she kept the grey scarf in her pocket against the chance that east would eventually deliver her to the person who had left it.

She was not afraid of the road.

She was afraid of finding the end of it and discovering that it stopped before she reached him.

She walked anyway.

The first week ended the way it had always been going to end: with people in motion, in separate directions, for separate reasons, all of which made sense and none of which kept anyone together yet.

The three fires Marcus had noted at the end of day zero were smaller now — not gone, but diminished, the city burning itself down to the things that refused to burn. To the north, the barn held its names on the wall. To the south, the gymnasium absorbed new arrivals and distributed what it had. To the northeast, a compass needle pointed at something that had been there before the world ended and would be there after the people walking toward it either found it or turned back.

In the central wilds, a hawk circled above a ridge.

In the east, a man walked toward a rumor that had his name on it.

In the city that was still, barely, a city, a young man lay on the floor of an apartment and planned against a map that was running out of room in its margins for all the things he had written in it.

The first week was over.

The world it had produced was smaller, colder, and more dangerous than the one that had preceded it. It was also — a fact no one had been given time to fully consider yet — the world in which everything that would be built would have to be built, without nostalgia for the one that was gone and without certainty about the one being made.

The sky above held its wounds.

The roads below held their travelers.

The first week ended and the next one began, the way all things began in the new world: without announcement, without permission, and with no one entirely ready.

At dusk, three horizons answered the sky in three different directions.

Adrian saw it first from the ruin belt east — a faint amber wash above the tree line, too steady for fire, too low for aurora. He stopped walking and let the nine people behind him stop with him, and no one spoke because speech would have made it smaller.

Marcus saw it from the western margin an hour later, the same color through a gap in the broken overpass, his compass needle twitching toward it as if the glow had weight. Iris put a hand on his arm. Lily asked what it was. He said, honestly, that he did not know yet, and that they would not go toward it tonight.

Selene saw it last, from the refugee trail, between the gymnasium's last light and the first true dark — a third point on the horizon that did not match any map she had ever studied, and that made the road east feel less like a rumor and more like a corridor with something waiting at the far end.

None of them knew the others were looking at the same wrong light from different bearings.

The first week had taught them separation.

The glow on the horizon suggested the world was larger than separation could account for, and worse, and still worth walking into.

They would not call it a dungeon yet. They would call it a light, a tremor, a mistake of the eye — anything that let them keep moving one more day. But the arithmetic of survival had already shifted, and none of them felt it as clearly as they would later remember feeling it now.
