The maps were wrong.

Not wrong in the way maps became wrong after disasters—roads blocked, bridges down, whole neighborhoods rearranged by flood or fire into something unrecognizable on paper. Those maps could still anchor you. You could look at the empty square where a bridge used to stand and say: here is where it was; here is where I am. The mistake was bounded. It made grief possible because it defined what had been lost.

These maps had a different problem. The roads themselves had moved.

Nora crouched at the edge of what should have been Route 9 heading east and studied the pavement beneath her boots. The road was here. The road had always been here—she had driven it a hundred times, could close her eyes and remember the particular sequence of overpasses, the farm stand with the faded fruit signs, the long straight stretch where the land opened flat and you could see thunderheads building from thirty miles out. She knew this road. It was in her body the way a person knows a path they have walked in the dark.

But it was not where it should be.

The sun's position said north was north. The ridgeline she could see through the tree line, the distinctive silhouette of the long hill she had always called the Shoulder because that was what it resembled from this angle—all of it agreed. North was north. The road had always run east from here. And yet the road now ran slightly wrong—not a dramatic shift, not enough to declare it with certainty to someone else, but enough. Two degrees, maybe three. Enough that by the time you followed it for twenty miles you would arrive somewhere other than where you intended.

She had written three question marks in the margins of her topographical sheet yesterday. Today she had written four.

Whisper turned a slow circle overhead, riding an updraft that came from nowhere she could account for. The hawk was a smudge against the pale sky, wings tilted, surveying. He had been quieter than usual for three days—not the focused quiet of a hawk marking prey, but the edged stillness of an animal that had decided to be careful. Nora knew the difference. She had spent enough time in the field with him, enough predawn mornings and cold camp nights, to read his moods the way other people read faces.

He was not hunting. He was watching something she could not see yet.

"Come down," she said, not loudly.

He banked twice more and then dropped in a long arc, landing on the leather wrap she kept on her left forearm for exactly this purpose. His talons settled with familiar precision and he folded his wings and looked east with an intensity that had no prey shape to explain it.

East, where the light was wrong.

---

Nora had been walking for twenty-one days.

In the first days after Rapture—she had started calling it that privately, the name for the thing that had happened to the sky, because no other word contained enough—she had been moving west with everyone else. Not from fear of the east specifically, but from the animal logic of crowds: people flee toward familiar things, toward cities, toward other people. The refugee streams had pulled her for nearly a week before she realized what she was doing.

She had a talent for seeing patterns at large scale. It was why she had ended up in conservation photography—not portraits of wildlife in controlled settings but the kind of work that required you to stand in one place for four hours waiting for the shot that proved something about an ecosystem, its pressures and responses, its hidden geometries. You had to be willing to be inconvenient. You had to understand that the story was in the space between visible things.

On Day Seven she had turned around.

Not dramatically. Not with ceremony. She had simply woken from a bad dream about her father's kitchen, looked at the stream of people moving past her camp, and understood that the answer was not in that direction. Whatever had broken the sky had come from somewhere. Something had a source. Something could be understood, mapped, documented, and if it could be documented then maybe humanity was not entirely in the dark about what it was walking into.

She had written in her notebook: *Moving wrong. Turn east.*

And she had.

She had not spoken to another person in nine days.

It was not loneliness, exactly. Or it was loneliness of the variety she had always preferred—the kind you chose, the kind that left space for observation. The kind that did not require performance. She ate what she could scavenge and supplement with what she found growing in disturbed ground, drank from streams she treated with tablets from her kit, slept in elevated positions with Whisper on a branch nearby. She was thinner than she had been three weeks ago. Her boots were wearing unevenly on the right side because she had been favoring a blister that was almost healed now. Her camera battery was dead, had been dead for five days, and she was making do with the notebook and pencil that had always been her backup.

She would have liked the camera. There were things she had seen in the last nine days that she could not adequately describe in words—not because they defeated language, but because she had not yet found the right language for them, and an image would have made argument unnecessary.

The architecture that was not architecture, visible through the tree line two days east of here. The stars, on clear nights, arranged in configurations that reminded her of something she could not place—not constellations she knew, not quite wrong enough to call impossible, just slightly shifted, as if the sky above this region were a photograph of the same sky taken from three miles south.

She had written that down too, with multiple question marks.

By midday the sun had burned the worst of the ground mist off and Nora could see the highway stretching ahead more clearly—a long gray ribbon cracked and frost-heaved in the usual patterns of road neglect, but that slight wrongness persisting in the angle of it, the way it curved toward a treeline that should not have been so close.

She walked it anyway, compass out and open, making marks on her topographical sheet at regular intervals. The work calmed her. It always had. Measuring things was a form of respect for them—a way of saying: you exist, I see you, I will record that you are here. It was also, she had come to understand, a way of keeping the panic at bay. Panic wanted shapelessness. Mapping imposed shape. They were enemies.

The road had been busy once. She could tell from the pattern of debris: cars pushed to the shoulders, tires flat, windows smashed in that particular way that meant human force rather than monster damage. People had abandoned these vehicles with intention, had tried to organize their exit. There were sleeping bags, backpacks opened and gone through, a child's shoe in the grass at the roadside that she stepped past without letting herself look at too long. She noted it in the margin—*personal effects, sign of organized departure not panic stop*—and kept moving.

The monsters had come through here too. She found the evidence without looking for it: the deep, irregular claw marks in the asphalt, too wide for any predator she had known before Rapture. A spray of dark staining on a guardrail that was almost certainly blood, but dried old enough to be three days or more. And the silence. The complete, architectural silence of a stretch of land where animals had decided not to be.

Before Rapture, a highway verge like this should have been full of small noise—insects, birds, the rustle of small mammals in the grass. Now there was only wind and the distant sound of her own footsteps.

Whisper shifted on her arm.

She looked at him. His head was turned east, his whole body tight with that particular attention that meant something significant was within perceptual range—something she couldn't sense yet, not from this distance.

"I know," she said. "We're not going all the way there today."

He made a sound that was not quite agreement.

The eastern glow had been visible on clear nights for four days. It was not fire. Fire moved, pulsed, sent up columns of smoke. This was lower on the horizon, steadier, a faint luminescence that reminded her of the light from the fractures but more concentrated—as if whatever was causing the sky to break had a center of gravity somewhere out there, a place where the wound was deepest.

She wanted to go toward it. She wanted to so badly she had to actively restrain herself each morning, because the part of her brain that documented things was desperate to see and the part that survived was not ready. Not yet. She needed better preparation: more supplies, more understanding of what kind of monster territory she'd be moving through, a clearer sense of the terrain.

She had learned patience in long grasses waiting for shots that might not come. She applied it now.

---

She stopped for water and a rest around what she judged to be two in the afternoon, settling at a spot where the road widened briefly into what had once been a rest stop—the concrete pad still visible under the creeping grass of a dry summer, a few weathered picnic tables, the rusted husk of a vending machine that someone had already stripped. She spread her topographical sheet on a table and looked at what she'd marked.

The shift was consistent. Every bearing she had taken over the last two days showed the same slight rotation—roads, ridgelines, even the treelines where she could compare them to her memory of the pre-Rapture landscape. The whole geography had rotated, fractionally but uniformly, as if someone had picked up a very large compass and moved its needle three degrees.

She should have found that terrifying. Instead she felt the specific electricity of being first to document something real. Someone needed to know this. Whoever came after her—and people would come after her, would eventually move through this landscape and try to make sense of it—they would need accurate maps. They would need someone to have stood on these roads and noticed that the numbers were wrong.

She was that person. She had always been that person. The one who noticed.

She was marking a bearing when she saw the mile marker.

It stood at the edge of the rest stop, half-obscured by a clump of unmowed grass. Standard green, standard font, the familiar format she had read thousands of times: a highway number at the top, a mileage figure, and below it the name of the nearest destination with an arrow.

The highway number was right. The mileage was plausible. But the city name below it—

She walked to the post and crouched down and read it twice.

Then she sat back on her heels and looked at it for a long time, the notebook open in her hands and the pencil motionless.

The name on the sign pointed to a city she had never heard of. Not a small town she might have missed, not an abbreviated name she couldn't parse. A full city name, the size of font used for significant destinations, pointing down the road she had been walking as if it had always been there.

Ashford was sixty miles behind her. The next major city in this direction was—had been—another sixty miles ahead. And the name on this sign matched neither, and matched no city she had ever driven through or seen on any paper map or digital navigation she'd ever used.

The city name on the sign was Verath.

She had no idea what that meant.

She wrote it down carefully, noted the mile marker number, triangulated the location as best she could with her compass and the known landmarks behind her. Then she took a sketch—the first sketch she had made with any confidence in days—of the sign itself, the font, the arrow direction, the weeds around the post.

The world was larger than it used to be. She had suspected this for weeks. The sky had broken and something had come through, and what had come through had not only brought monsters and mana and the end of electricity. It had brought geography.

Whisper launched from her arm without warning, climbed fast, and circled once before angling away—north, not east—and called once, sharp and clear.

She packed her notebook.

Whatever was east of here was awake today.

She would map it when she was ready.

---

She made camp three hours later in the upper story of a farmhouse whose roof had collapsed on the east side, leaving the west rooms still sheltered. She boiled water with the last of her fuel tablets, ate from her diminishing supply of preserved food, and wrote in the notebook until the light failed.

The mapping was not just documentation. She understood that now, in a way she hadn't when she first turned around nine days ago. The maps she made might be the only record that any of this had a shape. That the world had changed in ways that could be understood, if someone looked carefully enough. That the wrongness was not chaos—it was a pattern. Patterns could be survived. Patterns could eventually be navigated.

The mile marker with the city name that had never existed on any map: she wrote a long entry about it, turning the name over in her mind. Verath. It sounded like nothing she recognized from any language she knew, though she knew only a few. It sounded old. It sounded like a name that had been waiting for a map to appear on.

She wrote: *Possible conclusions: (1) The road sign is post-Rapture manufacture, unknown origin. (2) The name predates Rapture somehow—pre-Rapture but unknown to contemporary records. (3) The geography shift has moved us into overlap with something that was here before us. None of these conclusions are comfortable.*

Whisper settled on a beam above her, his head tucked but one eye open.

Outside, the eastern horizon gave off that low, steady glow, too far to warm her, too present to ignore.

Nora turned away from it and lay down on her bedroll and looked at the ceiling and tried to sleep.

She was not afraid. She had never quite been afraid of the dark. But she was aware, with the particular awareness that good fieldwork cultivated, that she was standing at the edge of something enormous—that the wrong geometry of roads and the city names that had never existed and the glow that made Whisper press tight against his branch were all part of the same event, the same truth she had not yet found language for.

It was out there. It was patient.

She would find it first.

She closed her eyes and listened to the ruined highway in the dark and felt, beneath the fear she was not admitting to yet, the pulse of the most interesting thing she had ever been close to.

She slept.
