## I — Cassian

The broadcast came at fourteen minutes past six in the evening, on a frequency Cassian had been cycling through every two hours since the first night.

He almost missed it. He had been in the stairwell between the second and third floors, listening to the building, assessing a new sound he had added to his catalogue the night before — something on the west side of the third floor that moved in the twenty-minute intervals he associated with injured things rather than the continuous patrol rhythm of the fully changed ones. He had heard the radio begin its signal in the apartment above and gone up the stairs two at a time because two at a time was faster than one and the stairs were a known quantity now.

The voice was not the recorded voice.

He knew the recorded voice the way he knew the sound of his apartment's pipes — texture and cadence, a specific failure of inflection at the phrase *do not approach* where the recording had been made on equipment that processed *h* sounds poorly. This voice was live. He could hear the live-ness of it in the way live things were different from recordings even when they said the same words: breath at the start of sentences, the micro-pause of a person deciding what came next.

"—Emergency Coordination Authority requests all survivors to—"

Static.

"—to remain in designated shelter areas if—"

More static. Then a sound that was not interference but language, the other-syllables he had been hearing in the background of broadcasts for nine days: layered, ancient, wrong in the specific way of something that had been waiting longer than recorded history to be heard again.

The live voice came back. He could hear it fighting for the frequency.

"—we are unable to maintain contact with regional nodes. All recovery operations are—"

The voice cut to static.

Then silence.

Then, so clearly that Cassian held the radio away from his ear as if distance would make it untrue, one sentence:

*We are alone.*

The broadcast ended.

The radio held static.

Cassian sat on the floor of his apartment with the radio in his lap and looked at the map on his table and thought about the boy on the street who had said *they ate Mr. Chen* and run before Cassian could ask his direction. He thought about the manifests at work. He thought about how he had always known, in the quiet and honest part of himself that he kept behind social performance, that the things he treasured — the map, the ordinary, the unremarkable continuity of a city that moved around him — were provisional. Had always been provisional. He had simply been waiting for the evidence.

This was the evidence.

Outside, the fractured sky pulsed once, and Ashford received the silence without screaming.

## II — Rowan

Rowan heard it on the barn's shortwave at the same moment.

He had been listening since four — an extra shift, self-imposed, after Kai had come to tell him at three-forty that the eastern access track showed movement again and he should know. The movement had resolved into deer: three of them, changed at the edges in the way animals were changing, their eyes holding fracture-light, their movement slightly wrong at the joints. Not hostile, not toward the barn. Just wrong. He had sent Kai back to sleep and stayed at the radio because he was awake and because there was always the chance the next scan would give him something.

Tonight it gave him the live voice.

He straightened on the stool when he recognized the difference — live, not recorded — and increased the volume incrementally, not wanting the sound to carry past the barn door and into the clearing where two of the evening watchers would hear it and draw their own conclusions before he'd had time to draw his.

He heard the other-language underneath. He had been hearing it for days, had filed it as interference, reassessed it after Perkins on the amateur frequency had asked if Rowan was getting the *weird syllables* and Rowan had said yes. It was not interference. He understood that now. It was a carrier frequency that predated their communications infrastructure by longer than their infrastructure had existed, and the broadcast was a human voice trying to speak over it.

The human voice said: *We are unable to maintain contact with regional nodes.*

He had expected this. He had told eleven people in this barn nine days ago, in different words, that this was coming. It still landed differently when it arrived, the way confirmed diagnoses landed differently than suspected ones.

*All recovery operations are—*

Static.

And then the sentence.

*We are alone.*

Rowan set the handset down on the table beside the radio stack and sat with his hands on his knees for a moment. He thought about Captain Duchamp, who had retired south and from whom Rowan had heard nothing since before the fractures. He thought about Adrian — out there somewhere, and Rowan could not know, could only hold the fact of his brother's capability as a comfort that sometimes worked.

He thought about Atlas, asleep on the cot nearest the barn door with the binoculars around his neck because Atlas could not be made to take the binoculars off even while sleeping. He thought about Leon curled against Elena in the sleeping area, one hand closed around the bird feather he had picked up in the access track on day six and been carrying since.

He thought: *This is what I have. Not the city, not the department, not the infrastructure. This.*

He picked up his logbook and wrote, in the entry for Day 9:

*18:14 — broadcast, live signal, unknown origin. No institutional identification. Concluded: "We are alone." Signal terminated. No further contact achieved. Frequency noted for continued monitoring.*

He wrote it the way he wrote everything in the log: factual, contained, stripped of the weight that factual things sometimes carried anyway.

Then he closed the log and went to stand in the doorway and watch the fractured sky and allow himself, for exactly the number of minutes it took the barn to settle into its night sounds, to feel what the sentence meant before it had to become policy.

## III — Adrian

Adrian heard it through a wall.

He was in the maintenance shed on the rail corridor's north side — the same stretch where he had sheltered with Selene on night one, before Selene had moved on toward the refugee congregation point she'd identified from a distance and he had continued east toward Rowan's route, thinking: *if I know where Rowan would go, I can follow the logic of it.* The logic had held for seven days in that it had kept him moving. It had not yet produced Rowan.

The shed on the north side held a radio left by a maintenance crew who had not returned — a larger unit than his handset, mounted to the wall, running off a battery backup that had been partially depleted before he found it. He had been rationing the battery charge the way he rationed food, two hours of active listening per day at the frequencies he knew, which were firefighter frequencies learned from Rowan over years of holiday conversations about the department.

Tonight the battery had twelve percent left, which he estimated at forty more minutes of active use.

He heard the live voice at seven minutes and recognized it as live in the same instant, the same bodily way he had always recognized things that required his full attention — a change in the quality of the air, a settling into readiness in his shoulders that was not quite tension and not quite calm.

Three others were in the shed with him: Preet, who had been a delivery driver and had a broken radius in a splint Adrian had made from a tent pole and gauze on day five; Preet's neighbor whose name was Ximena; and a teenager named Dav who had not spoken in four days and ate when food was placed near him and otherwise sat with his forearms on his knees and watched the middle distance.

All four of them heard the other-language underneath the live voice, and none of them said anything.

The live voice said: *We are alone.*

Preet made a sound. Not words. The sound a person made when they had been carrying a specific weight for a specific number of days and were told the weight was permanent.

Ximena reached over and took Preet's hand.

Adrian turned off the radio with eleven percent remaining — he needed the eleven percent, needed it more than he needed to hear the static that followed — and faced the three people in the shed who were looking at him the way people looked at Adrian when the room needed someone who would not add fear to fear.

He said: "It changes what we wait for. It doesn't change what we do."

Dav looked up from the middle distance for the first time in four days.

"What do we do?" he asked. His voice was rough from disuse.

"Same as yesterday," Adrian said. "Move. Find people. Keep moving."

Dav thought about this. Nodded once with the deliberate weight of a boy who had decided, in the private interior of four days of silence, that some decisions could only be made and not explained. "Okay," he said.

Adrian met Ximena's eyes. She was pressing her lips together in the expression he had learned meant she was performing calm for Preet's benefit.

He was performing it for all four of them.

That was all right. You performed it until it became real. He had learned this from Selene, who had performed stability for him on more than one night in their apartment above the closed bakery, and from Rowan, who had performed confidence on the night Atlas broke his arm falling out of the oak tree in the back yard and Rowan's hands had shaken the entire drive to the hospital and Elena had not mentioned it until Atlas was asleep and safe.

He was his brother's brother.

He performed it.

He believed it would become real.

## IV — Marcus

Marcus Vale heard the broadcast from inside a school gymnasium on the west side of the city where ninety-three survivors had organized, somewhat against their will, into something that functioned as a community because Iris was in it.

He had been in the corner he had designated as workspace — three folding chairs arranged as a table, instruments spread in the order he could reach them fastest, the atmospheric sensor finally producing numbers again now that he had recalibrated for the new baseline — when the improvised PA system crackled to life on the frequency the community's designated radio monitor had been maintaining.

The radio monitor was a retired electrician named Holt, who had found the gymnasium with a working radio and become, by virtue of this equipment, a kind of information broker for the ninety-three. Holt had been broadcasting at six every evening with reports of what frequencies were producing and what they were saying. Ninety-three people listened to Holt in the way people had once listened to news programs: with varying degrees of belief and the shared understanding that the information, however incomplete, was better than none.

Tonight Holt did not editorialize.

He held the microphone up to the radio's speaker and let ninety-three people hear the live voice fight for the frequency and lose, and then the other-language fill the space between, and then the one clear sentence.

*We are alone.*

Ninety-three people in a gymnasium absorbed the sentence.

The silence afterward was not shocked. Marcus had predicted, in the privacy of his field notebook, that the broadcast was coming — had been circling that prediction since day three, had found himself not wanting to write it because writing predictions made them real in the way of things he could be held accountable for. He had written it on day seven when the logic became undeniable. *Emergency coordination infrastructure has failed globally, not regionally. No rescue forthcoming. We are alone.*

He had not shared the prediction with Iris, which she would discover later and consider a kindness and also a failure of partnership, and she would be correct about both.

He looked across the gymnasium to where Iris was sitting with the children's group — twelve children, ages four to eleven, whose parents were variously present, missing, or dead — reading to them from the only chapter book anyone had contributed to the community library, which was a dog-eared fantasy novel about a girl who discovered she could speak to birds. Lily was beside her, drawing in her satchel.

Iris met Marcus's eyes over the children's heads.

She had heard it. Of course she had heard it.

She mouthed something that he read as *it's all right*, which he knew she did not mean as factual information. She meant it as direction.

He went back to his instruments. Wrote: *Day 9. 18:14. Final broadcast. Sentence: "We are alone." Note: lone clear signal before termination. No government, no military, no recovery infrastructure. We begin from what we have.*

He turned a page in the field notebook.

*What we have:* he wrote. *Ninety-three people, gymnasium, two weeks of food at current count. My instruments. Lily's drawings. Iris's lists. This notebook.*

*Begin there.*

## V — Nora

Nora heard it from the ridge.

She had climbed back to the high position after two days of moving northeast through the corridor, documenting the second fracture from multiple distances, filling memory cards with geometry that should not exist and shapes that moved with deliberate purpose through the wounds in the sky. She had come back to the ridge because it was the highest point available to her without entering terrain she had not yet mapped, and the radio signal was cleaner at elevation.

She had made a small camp at the ridge's eastern edge — tent, sleeping mat, the compact cooking setup she used on long photography assignments — and was reviewing the day's frames when the broadcast began.

Whisper was on the tent apex and looked up at the sound of the live voice before Nora registered it consciously, which was how Nora had been using the hawk for nine days: as early-warning for things she should pay attention to.

She set the camera down and listened.

The other-language was louder on the shortwave here. She had been recording it since day two — audio files on her camera's video setting, because the camera could do that and it was the only recording equipment she had. She had four hours of the other-language and no way to analyze it, but four hours was a dataset even without analysis, and datasets were the difference between observing and understanding.

The live voice said what it said.

She listened to the static after.

Whisper turned its golden gaze from the radio to the eastern horizon, toward the direction the fractures had come from, toward the second fracture she had documented at range. In the fracture's glow, nine days into the new world, the ridge grass moved in patterns that did not follow the wind's direction.

Nora wrote in her field notebook: *Day 9. Final broadcast received, ridge position. Sentence: "We are alone." Noted.*

Then she wrote something she had been building toward since frame 91 on day zero, the thing in the fracture that had looked back:

*We are alone in the sense that the institutions we had built are gone. We are not alone in the sense that the thing looking at us from inside the fractures has been here longer than the institutions were. These are separate facts. Both are true.*

She closed the notebook.

The sky above the ridge pulsed once — the slow throb she had come to identify as mana, having read the word in a fragment of a broadcast five days ago and filed it under *label for the phenomenon I have been measuring* — and the pulse moved through the earth as well as the air, as if the world had exhaled.

In the silence after it, the eastern fracture caught the last of the evening light and held it the way a wound held color before it healed.

Nora raised the camera.

She photographed the fracture in the pulse-light. She would review the frames tomorrow. She expected to find the geometry still present, still identical to the pattern she had documented on day zero, still consistent across nine days of independent observation.

The world had changed. The architecture in the fractures had not.

She found that, in the particular temperature-controlled part of her mind she used for field observation, interesting.

She found it, in the part she had not successfully quieted, frightening.

She put the camera down.

*We are alone*, the broadcast had said.

Whisper turned its golden gaze from the fracture to her face.

Nora Winters looked back at the bird whose eyes she had been learning to read for nine days, in the particular look that she was beginning to understand meant *something is coming from that direction, and it is coming toward us*, and she wondered which direction it would come from and what it would want when it arrived.

The ridge held the silence.

The fracture held its light.

The new world said nothing more, because it had already said the important thing, and the important thing was: *begin*.
