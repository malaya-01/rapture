Marcus Vale had been awake since the fractures started because sleep required a quality of inattention he could not, even in his best years, reliably manufacture.

This was not anxiety in the clinical sense—or not only that. It was the particular compulsion of a man whose brain processed the world by recording it, who could not encounter an anomaly without reaching for a notebook. He had filled eleven notebooks by the end of his doctoral work. He had filled another six in the years since, a habit his wife tolerated with the specific patience of someone who understood that the notebooks were how Marcus stayed organized enough to be the person she had married.

He sat at the kitchen table on the morning of Day Four with a fresh notebook open in front of him and wrote down what he knew.

*What he knew:* The sky had fractured on Monday. The fractures were multiplying. Power had been intermittent since Tuesday, out entirely since Wednesday. His last reliable information from a radio with batteries: evacuation order issued by city emergency management, assembly point at the Meridian Sports Complex on the eastern edge of Ashford, convoy departures beginning at 0700. That broadcast had been forty hours ago.

*What he did not know:* Whether the assembly point still existed. Whether the convoy had left. Whether the roads between here and the eastern edge of Ashford remained roads.

*What he had been told by a neighbor with a battery radio:* A second broadcast, sketchy and brief, had come through at midnight. Something about military coordination. Something about aerial surveillance. The neighbor—Dr. Chen from the second floor, physics department, a woman who approached every piece of information as though it were a proof to be checked—had said the word *military* in a way that sounded like comfort and looked like she knew better.

Marcus closed the notebook and went to wake his family.

---

Iris was already awake.

He should have expected this. Iris Vale had a quality that Marcus had once described to a colleague, inadequately, as *operational readiness*—she was never unaware of her environment, never caught without the next practical action already forming. She worked part-time as a teacher's aide and volunteered three afternoons a week with the community center's after-school program, and she had the bone-deep competence of someone who spent her days in rooms full of children: always knowing where the exits were, always knowing who was hungry, always knowing which sounds required immediate response.

She was in the kitchen when he came downstairs. She had packed the go-bags—both of them, his and hers, and a smaller one for Lily—and had laid out bread and peanut butter and two bananas that were at the exact right edge of ripeness.

"Eat," she said. "Both of you eat before we go anywhere."

"Is Lily—"

"Lily's dressing herself. She insisted." The small lift at the corner of Iris's mouth. "She's wearing the blue boots."

He sat down. He ate. He wrote two lines in the notebook while he ate, because not writing felt worse than writing, and Iris watched him with the expression she kept for moments when she had decided to let him be himself rather than redirect him.

"The evacuation point," she said.

"Sports complex on the eastern edge. But the broadcast was forty hours ago."

"Dr. Chen's radio?"

"Nothing since midnight."

She was quiet. She had the quality, which he had not always appreciated and had learned to depend on, of sitting with incomplete information rather than filling the gap with noise.

"We go," she said. "We try for the assembly point. If the assembly point is gone, we look for any organized group. If there's no organized group, we find a building with water access and walls that can hold, and we wait and we document and we figure out what happens next."

"Document," he said.

She looked at him. "It's what you do, Marcus. It's what you're going to do regardless. We may as well call it a strategy."

He thought about arguing and recognized that the argument would not go anywhere he wanted to be.

Lily appeared in the kitchen doorway wearing the blue boots and a yellow rain slicker and a backpack that was almost as large as she was. She was six years old and had the gravity of someone much older in the area of important decisions.

"I packed Mr. Thornton," she announced.

Mr. Thornton was a stuffed bear of indeterminate species who had been with Lily since infancy and had survived being left at a rest stop, washed at high heat by accident, and dropped into a tide pool on vacation. Mr. Thornton had more sentimental mass per ounce than anything else in their household.

"Good," Marcus said. "He can help us navigate."

Lily looked at him with the expression that meant she knew he was making a small joke and she was choosing to let him have it. She was six years old. He did not know where this expression had come from. He suspected she had learned it from watching Iris.

---

The university district in the early morning of Day Four looked like a photograph of itself taken by someone who did not understand what a city was supposed to do.

The faculty housing complex where they lived was six blocks from the main campus. The main campus was six blocks from the eastern arterial that connected to the evacuation route. In normal circumstances it was a twenty-minute walk. In the circumstances of Day Four it was something else—a transit of changed geography, a negotiation with a landscape that had been revised.

Marcus walked with Lily's hand in his left hand and his notebook in his jacket pocket with the pen clipped to the collar. He could not stop himself from looking at everything and he could not stop himself from recording it later, so he might as well make peace with both.

The evidence around him:

A campus building with its ground-floor windows intact but its fifth and sixth floors dark—power cut or evacuated or worse. The campus garden with its irrigation system still running, water falling on plants that would outlast most of what was happening around them. The faculty parking lot with twelve cars remaining, positioned in a pattern that suggested people had left in different directions at different times, not in an organized departure.

A fire on the east edge of campus. Not large—controlled, even—as if someone had lit it deliberately and then left. The smoke had the smell of synthetic materials: furniture, carpet, the particular burn of an office that had gone up before anyone could prioritize it.

Two students—he recognized one as being from his department, a second-year whose name he had learned and then lost—sitting on the steps of the library with their bags on their laps, not going anywhere. He stopped.

"Do you know if the sports complex assembly point is still active?" he asked.

The student he recognized looked up. "There were people going that way this morning. Earlier. I don't know if they made it."

"Are you going?"

The student looked at her companion. The companion was looking at the fire.

"We're waiting," the student said.

Marcus did not know what they were waiting for. He thought about asking and understood that the answer might not be one he could do anything about. He pressed Lily's hand and moved on.

---

The assembly point was still there.

This was the best fact of the morning, and Marcus recorded it with the particular relief of a man who had trained himself to treat evidence as neutral but who was not, in his private interior, neutral about evidence that his family was not going to die today.

The Meridian Sports Complex had been converted with the speed of emergency improvisation: barriers made of vehicles and portable fencing, a registration table manned by someone in a reflective vest who had the distracted competence of someone who had been doing the same task for eighteen hours straight. Behind him, the parking lot held perhaps four hundred people. They had the faces Marcus associated with mass casualty waiting: people who had exhausted the initial adrenaline and found, underneath it, a quieter and more durable fear.

A soldier stood at the vehicle barrier. An actual soldier—army green, body armor, rifle held in the relaxed grip of someone who had been briefed to look reassuring. Behind him, two military vehicles. The presence of them was the most comforting thing Marcus had seen since Monday and the most terrible, because if the military were here in small numbers with no apparent communication infrastructure and the careful expression of people who had not been given adequate information about their own mission, then the military's presence meant less than his first instinct wanted it to mean.

He wrote this down.

Iris registered them. The woman with the reflective vest entered their names in a physical ledger—paper, not electronic—and gave each of them a wristband and a water bottle, and told them the first convoy would depart at noon.

"Direction?" Marcus asked.

"Highway Route 15 north, then junction to the regional processing center at Hartwell."

"What's at Hartwell?"

"Federal emergency management facility. Food, medical, communications."

He wrote this down. He wrote, next to it: *Hartwell—140 miles north. Highway corridor status unknown. Regional processing center—unknown capacity, unknown current operation.*

Iris took his arm. "Marcus. We're registered. We have water. We're in a group."

"I'm just—"

"I know what you're doing." Her hand on his arm. Her voice carrying the specific warmth of the woman who loved him enough to be honest with him. "You can keep doing it. I'm not asking you to stop. I'm asking you to also drink the water."

He drank the water.

Lily found two other children near the registration tent and within ten minutes had organized them into a project involving rocks arranged in patterns on the asphalt. Marcus watched her and felt the specific gratitude of a parent who had not fully understood what children were until he had one—they processed differently, adapted differently, reached for ordinary in the spaces where adults froze. She was six years old and she was arranging rocks while the sky fractured above her and he thought it might be the most human thing he had seen since Monday.

---

The convoy was supposed to depart at noon.

At eleven-thirty a sound came from the north and the soldier at the barrier raised his radio and whatever the radio said made the soldier's expression change in the way the expressions of trained people changed—not to panic but to calculation.

The convoy departed at eleven fifty-two.

Marcus was in the fifth vehicle—a school bus, orange and familiar and surreal in this context—with Iris's hand in his and Lily against his side and the notebook on his knee and forty-seven other people he did not know. The bus moved out of the parking lot and onto the eastern arterial and the convoy began to form, vehicles falling into place with the slow confidence of an organized response.

He thought: *We are going to be all right.*

He thought this for twenty-two minutes. He was counting.

The road broke on the Route 15 interchange.

Not gradually—though the signs had been there for a mile, now that he reviewed his notes: the pavement surface wrong, the guardrail bent in the wrong direction, the trees on the interchange's east embankment tilted at angles they had not started the week at. The break itself was sudden: a section of overpass twenty meters long simply was not there, replaced by a gap through which the street below was visible, and the air above the gap had the silver shimmer he had been learning to associate with places where the fractures had come down to ground level.

The lead vehicle braked.

Everyone braked.

In the bus, forty-seven people went from quiet anxiety to something louder and less organized. The soldier's voice on a bullhorn, directing vehicles to turn around. The military vehicles at the convoy head beginning a three-point turn in a space not designed for three-point turns. A car in the second position pulling hard left and clipping a barrier, spinning against it with the particular metal scream that meant the car was done.

Marcus wrote: *0:23 - Route 15 interchange compromised. Overpass section absent. Fracture at ground level. Convoy attempting reversal.*

Iris said: "Marcus."

He looked up from the notebook. The bus driver was trying to reverse and the vehicle behind them had already started moving and the gap between them was closing and it was going to be fine, it was going to be—

The military vehicles broke east.

Not in an organized retreat. In a break—the two vehicles simply departing the convoy line and accelerating east without the bullhorn, without signaling, without whatever protocol governed the departure of military vehicles from an organized civilian evacuation. They drove until they were out of sight.

The convoy driver stopped using the bullhorn.

For thirty seconds, the convoy sat on the broken overpass approach with a forty-seven-person school bus in position five and no one saying anything over any radio frequency.

Then the first people began to get out of their vehicles.

Not all of them. Some stayed. But the four cars ahead of Marcus's bus all produced people at approximately the same moment, the doors opening in sequence, and those people stood on the broken road and looked at the gap where the overpass had been and began to make individual calculations. Some of them went east, following the direction the military vehicles had taken. Some of them turned back toward the assembly point, which had probably already been abandoned. One man—older, alone, carrying only a small bag—sat down on the guardrail and looked at nothing.

"We're getting off the bus," Iris said.

She was already standing. She had Lily's hand and the go-bag and the small pack. She had made the decision while Marcus was writing and he had not noticed and this was, in the end, the correct division of their marriage: she made the decisions in the field while he recorded what the field contained.

He got off the bus.

---

He could not have said exactly when the bus driver left. At some point—twenty minutes, perhaps thirty—the bus was simply empty and gone, the driver having made the same calculation as everyone else, just later.

They stood in a cluster of fourteen people on the shoulder of the Route 15 approach and discussed what to do with the pragmatic brevity of people who understood that discussion had a cost.

East was where the military had gone and might mean something, or might mean only that the military had gone there.

North was the direction of Hartwell and was blocked.

West was Ashford, which was burning.

South was unknown.

"South," said the man with the small bag who had been sitting on the guardrail. He said it without explanation, as if south were a conclusion he had reached privately and was now sharing as a courtesy.

Marcus looked at Iris. Iris looked at Lily. Lily was looking at the sky, where one of the fracture lines had descended low enough to be visible from the ground—a shimmering line of silver light maybe thirty meters up, running roughly parallel to the road.

"What's that?" Lily asked.

"Something new," Marcus said.

"Is it dangerous?"

He looked at it. He could feel it from this distance—not heat, not cold, something more like pressure, a slight resistance in the air that increased as you faced it directly. He took out the notebook.

*Residual fracture at low altitude. Ground-level manifestation of sky break. Air feels resistive at approximately 20m distance. No visible biological response yet (Lily feels nothing unusual per her report). Possible ambient mana pressure.*

He put the notebook away. He took Lily's other hand.

"We don't know yet," he said. "So we don't go near it until we know more."

She considered this with the gravity she brought to all important information.

"Okay," she said. "Mr. Thornton says the same thing."

They went south.

The city took them in the way cities took the displaced—in side streets, in the spaces between things, in the gaps that had once been transitions and were now destinations in themselves. Marcus wrote in the notebook as he walked, short observations, things he didn't want to lose. He wrote until the light went low and then he wrote by touch, feeling where he was on the page, keeping the pen moving.

Iris never asked him to stop. She had promised herself to his particular kind of mind, and she held to the promise.

The notebook was the only thing, on Day Four, that made him feel like the world could be understood.

He could not know, yet, that understanding was exactly what was going to matter.
