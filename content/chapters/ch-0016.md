She had built the intake system on the second day, out of what was available.

What was available: three folding tables that had been the site of a school lunch program before the gymnasium became a congregation point. A box of chalk and a whiteboard on wheels that rolled badly on the uneven floor. Four people willing to follow organized direction, which was fewer than optimal and more than zero. And the particular energy of a woman who had spent five years organizing refugee policy case studies and had not, until today, understood that the studies were preparation for the actual.

She built it the way she had built everything at university: list the categories, determine the flow, assign people to positions they were suited for, iterate when the flow showed problems. Intake, she knew from theory and from the past fifteen days of direct application, was the difference between a group that knew what it had and a group that didn't, and knowing what you had was the difference between allocating resources and watching them disappear.

Category 1: identity (name, party size, any missing persons still being searched for).
Category 2: medical (injuries, conditions, medications needed).
Category 3: skills (what the person could contribute — no obligations, just information).
Category 4: immediate needs (food, water, warmth, rest — in that order).

She sat at the first table with a chalk ledger and processed each arrival with the kind of warm efficiency she had been told, by people who meant it as a compliment, looked like comfort without the softness. She wrote names down because names mattered. She asked about missing persons because people needed to say the names of the missing out loud, and writing the names in the ledger meant someone was keeping the record, and records were a form of promise.

She did not write Adrian's name.

She kept it in the interior pocket of her own attention, the way she kept things she was managing rather than things she had resolved.

---

The camp at the school gymnasium had three hundred and fourteen people when she arrived on day thirteen, redirected from the congregation point at the southern rail that had become untenable due to water shortage and territorial behavior from a pack of creatures that had claimed the rail cutting adjacent to it. She had come on foot with a group of seven, four of whom had been with her since day nine when she'd organized the rail camp's evacuation.

She had told herself, at the rail camp, that staying and organizing was a way of making herself findable. If she was at the intake table, she was visible. If she was visible, someone looking for her would find her. The logic was correct as logic. As emotional accounting, it was the kind of logic she had been running on for fifteen days, and it was beginning to cost interest.

Adrian was not at the gymnasium.

He had not been at the rail camp, or the pharmacy distribution point south of the overpass, or the secondary congregation in the school yard she had passed through on day eleven. She had asked at each location and received variations of the same uncertain information: someone who looked like him had passed through, possibly. Two days ago, possibly three. Going east, or south, or they were not certain of the direction.

She kept going east.

East was where the larger congregations were, where the infrastructure of survival was accreting — food, medical, communication, the partial radio network that the largest gymnasium had been trying to build since day seven. East was where the next thing was, and the next thing was always where the people who were looking for each other tended to find each other, because people moving toward resources moved toward the same resources.

East was also the direction of a rumor she had heard three days ago: someone matching Adrian's description, brown-haired, substantial, carrying people with him, seen twelve kilometers east. It was the kind of rumor she had trained herself to evaluate rather than consume, because rumors in the first two weeks were more hope than information. It was specific in some ways and vague in others. She had filed it and kept moving.

---

On day fifteen she processed a couple who arrived in the afternoon.

They had been together since before the Rapture — ten years, the woman said, with the particular ease of a number that had been said so many times it had become background. They had been separated in the first days, found each other on day nine at the overpass congregation, and had been moving together since. They were not dramatically expressive about finding each other. They simply occupied the same physical space in the particular way of people who had stopped having to remind themselves the other person was there.

Selene wrote their names in the ledger. She was warm, efficient, present.

She watched them sit together at the allocation table — the woman passing the man half her portion before he asked, the man adjusting his position so she was between him and the draft from the gymnasium door — and she felt the thing she had been monitoring in herself since day five, the thing she had labeled with clinical honesty as *proportionate fear* and had been keeping proportionate by the specific discipline of being useful.

It was not jealousy. She was clear about that. It was a form of grief — not grief for something lost, but grief for something not yet present that had begun to feel, in certain specific moments, like something that might remain not present.

She had always known she was afraid of being left.

Not as pathology — as a specific pattern in the architecture of who she was, traceable to the specific experiences that had built it, documented in the therapy she had done at twenty-one and twenty-three and had told Adrian about in the apartment above the bakery on a Sunday afternoon when she had decided that honesty was better infrastructure than performance. He had listened. He had not minimized it. He had said: *I'll show you by being here.* 

And he had been here. He had been here for four years before the sky broke.

She wrote the next name in the ledger.

---

By late afternoon she had logged eleven reunions.

Not all of them were couples. A mother found a daughter who had been separated at a bridge. Two brothers recognized each other across the allocation line by the way one of them laughed—a sound Selene had not known she was listening for until it happened and the gymnasium seemed, for a moment, less like a warehouse for grief. An elderly man sat with a teenage neighbor he had barely spoken to before the Rapture and held the boy's hand while the boy cried without shame.

Selene processed each reunion the same way she processed everything else: names, medical, skills, needs. She did not linger. Lingering would have meant admitting she was measuring her own absence against their presence, and she had decided long ago that the intake table was not a place for self-pity dressed up as empathy.

But she noticed the pattern anyway.

People found each other when someone stayed visible long enough to be found. They found each other when they kept moving toward the same resources, the same rumors, the same chalked arrows on broken walls. They found each other because love, even when terrified, was still a navigation system.

She was visible. She was moving east in her ledger if not yet in her feet.

The arithmetic said Adrian should have found her by now if the rumor were true and the roads were passable and the world were still obeying the old rules of distance.

The world was not obeying.

She wrote the next name in the ledger.

At nine in the evening the gymnasium's radio operator got a burst of signal on the shortwave: a human voice from somewhere north, cycling on a frequency that suggested they were using the same equipment he was, same battery protocols. The voice gave a location she could not map without better reference points and a resource count that was, by current standards, significant.

She listened from her table.

The voice described its group: seventeen people as of last count, shelter stable, food supply three weeks, medical capability present. The voice gave a name: *Hale Hold*, which was not a name she recognized as a place but which had the quality of a named thing — intentionally named, with the permanence of a decision rather than the accident of geography.

She had heard Hale as a surname twice since the Rapture, in the way you heard surnames in congregations: Adrian's brother was named Rowan Hale, firefighter captain, whose instincts and training she had found herself thinking about in the first week the way survivors thought about people with skills they needed. She did not have enough information to determine if this was the same Hale. The connection might be coincidence.

She wrote *Hale Hold — signal, shortwave, north — 17 persons, stable* in the margin of her ledger.

She told herself she was keeping a record.

She was also keeping hope at one remove, which was how she had always managed the kind of hope that cost something if it turned out to be wrong.

---

The rumor arrived at ten-thirty, carried by a young man who had come in from the eastern approach with a group of five.

He was seventeen, dark hair, thin from the weeks, the particular self-contained quality of a person who had been alone for a significant portion of the previous two weeks and had come to an accommodation with solitude that was not quite peace. He sat at her intake table and gave his information in the order her system requested it: name, party size, medical, skills, needs.

His name was Dav.

Under missing persons, he said: "I'm not looking for anyone. But — a man I was traveling with asked me to pass a message if I found a silver-haired woman organizing things."

Selene stopped writing.

"Brown hair," Dav said. "Broad. Carried people with him even when it slowed him down. Said his name was Adrian."

Her pen was still on the paper.

"He said east. He said he was going east and he wouldn't stop."

She wrote nothing for a long moment.

Then she wrote: *Adrian — east, twelve km, day 12 at latest.* The date was three days ago. East was the direction she was already going.

"Did he seem—" she started, and stopped, because she had not planned to ask and did not know what the end of the sentence was, and Dav was watching her with the specific attention of a person who was trying to determine what kind of answer she needed and whether he had it.

"He seemed like someone who knew what he was doing," Dav said. "He was carrying four of us and he didn't stop for anything that wasn't worth stopping for."

She absorbed this.

"Thank you," she said.

"He said he wouldn't stop looking," Dav said. "I thought that was the part you'd want to know."

She finished Dav's intake form. She allocated him food and water and noted his skills — endurance, basic navigation, willingness to work — in the skills column where Jemison's people would find them. She directed him to the available cot assignments.

She sat at the intake table until the gymnasium quieted, processing the last four arrivals of the evening with the warm efficiency that was not warmth without content but warmth as method, warmth as the deliberate architecture of what she believed about other people's right to feel seen in the worst week of their lives.

Then she went to the section of the gymnasium floor she used for sleeping and sat on her rolled mat and held what Dav had told her against what the rational part of her had been telling her for three days.

*He is looking. He hasn't found you yet. Both of these things are true and neither of them is abandonment.*

She knew this. She had been managing this for fifteen days. Fear of abandonment was a pattern, and patterns were not destiny, and she had fifteen years of accumulated evidence — most of which was waiting for her, she believed, twelve kilometers east — that the pattern was not the truth.

She lay down.

The gymnasium breathed around her. Three hundred and fourteen people plus the day's arrivals, sleeping in the specific layered way of people who were sharing air and warmth and the barely-sufficient comfort of knowing they were not alone.

From across the gymnasium, the intake couple she had processed at five that afternoon slept in adjacent cots, the woman's hand on the man's arm.

Selene looked at the gymnasium ceiling.

She thought: *I am the person at the intake table. I am the person people come to with their names and their missing persons and their three days without eating. I am useful here. I am needed here.*

She thought: *I am also the person waiting for someone to come to my table.*

Both were true.

Tomorrow she would process arrivals and update the ledger and listen for the shortwave and ask the eastern arrivals about brown hair and substantial build and a man who carried people when it slowed him down.

And the day after that she would decide whether to go east herself, or stay visible.

She had not yet decided which was the braver choice.

She fell asleep deciding.

In the morning the gymnasium would smell of chalk dust and boiled grain and the particular human warmth of three hundred people who had not yet learned whether the week they were living was the worst week or only the first of many. She would stand at the intake table again and be the person others came to with their names.

She would also, before noon, walk the eastern perimeter and listen to the road for footsteps that matched a description she had memorized until it hurt.

That was the work hope required when hope could not be passive.

She slept with her boots still on, just in case the road called before dawn and she could not afford the time to lace them.
