The text had been in her drafts for nineteen days.

She had written it on the morning of Day Seven, when the phones still held battery if not signal—when the network was down but the device still worked, when typing felt like sending even when nothing was going anywhere. She had written it in the parking lot of a collapsed gas station while a convoy organized itself around her with the particular chaos of people who had been moving for a week and had not yet learned how to move efficiently.

*Adrian. Still here. Looking for Rowan's shelter route. I have water. I'm on the highway 9 spur. Don't worry about me, just find me.*

She had pressed send six times. Each time the screen showed the spinning circle of transmission and then the gray X of failure. The sixth time she had put the phone in her pocket and told herself she would try again when the signal came back.

The signal had not come back.

She checked the draft every morning anyway. It was still there. It had the particular quality of things that had never been allowed to become memories—still present-tense, still undelivered, still waiting to be made real by the act of arrival. Every morning she looked at it and thought *today, maybe*, and then looked at the network status bar and saw the universal symbol for nothing, the bars all gray, the small icon of connectivity that meant the infrastructure they required no longer existed.

She had stopped thinking of the text as a communication nineteen days ago. It had become something else. A record. A timestamp. *This is what I needed you to know on Day Seven. I was here. I was looking.*

She pressed send again, this morning, standing at the base of the dead phone tower.

The tower had been visible from the trail for an hour before she reached it—its structure unchanged, intact, a monument to the previous world's communication infrastructure. She had walked toward it with the particular kind of hope that was not hope so much as the refusal to stop doing a thing that had consistently not worked, because stopping was its own kind of admission.

The screen showed the gray X.

She put the phone back in her pocket.

---

The eastern refugee trail was not a trail in any formal sense. It was the emergent path of several thousand people who had all made the same general decision in the same general direction over the same two-week period, their footsteps and tire tracks and discarded objects accumulating into a legible route the way water found its way downhill—not by plan, but by the collective weight of individual choices.

Selene had been following it for eight days. East, then slightly north, then east again, tracking the rumor she had been given at the refugee camp on Day Seventeen: that a tall man with dark hair and a calm way of moving had been seen near the highway interchange on Day Fifteen, that he had been moving with a small group, that he had been asking questions about north routes.

She had asked for descriptions at every camp she'd passed through. Height, hair, build, the way he carried himself. Most people gave her something that was at least partially recognizable as Adrian, and most of those descriptions were four to six days old by the time they reached her.

She was following a ghost.

Not a ghost in the frightening sense—in the tracking sense, the sense of being always behind a moving thing, always reading the impressions left by something still alive and still moving, always adjusting her bearing based on what it had done last rather than what it was doing now.

She was good at this. She had not known she was good at it until the world ended and the skill had become necessary, but it turned out that being organized was a form of tracking—that the instinct to maintain lists and systems was the same instinct that read campsites and footprint patterns and the particular way people described strangers when the strangers had been memorable.

Adrian was memorable. He was always memorable. People saw him and they kept him.

She followed him.

She had stopped for water at a camp two hours west of the tower when she heard the rumor that cut wrong.

She had learned to sort rumors by quality—this was a survival skill nobody talked about but everyone developed, the calibration of testimony, the estimation of reliability. Some rumors were information: specific, sourced, with physical evidence attached (*I saw him at the interchange, east side, he had two boys with him and a woman with a medical kit*). Some were noise: generalized, third-hand, shaped by the psychological need to provide useful information rather than the actual possession of it (*someone said someone saw someone matching that description*).

The camp was a good one—organized, with a water rotation and a sentry arrangement that reminded her of the systems she had helped implement at the first camp. She had stopped there for thirty minutes, long enough to drink and exchange what information she had about safe routes to the west.

The couple at the far table, she had not initially paid attention to. They were somewhere in their forties, both of them, the kind of people who blended into camps because they did not need anything and did not cause problems. She had been about to leave when the woman said something that made Selene's hearing sharpen against her will.

*—the alpha who's been moving east with the group—*

She sat back down.

The woman did not notice her attention. She was talking to her partner, the low voice of conversation not meant for the table, and Selene was three seats away and should not have been able to hear her. She was able to hear her.

*—someone at the west camp said he found a woman. That's why he stopped looking.*

Selene held very still.

*—can't blame him, three weeks on the road—*

*—she's not my concern, I'm just saying what they told me. The description matched.*

Her partner said something quiet.

*—no, I don't know. Same height, they said. Dark hair.*

Selene put down her water.

She knew—with the part of her that catalogued and assessed and organized—that this was noise. The description *tall, dark hair, calm* applied to a meaningful fraction of the male population of Ashford, and the refugee trail was full of rumors shaped by what people wanted to believe about the people they were looking for and the people they feared. Rumors about found companions, about stopped searches, about the ways catastrophe reconfigured attachments—these were not information. These were the trail generating narratives around its own uncertainty.

She knew this. She held it carefully in the front of her mind.

The back of her mind was doing something different.

The back of her mind had been doing something different since Day Two, since the apartment and the chaos and the separation, since the moment she had last seen Adrian's back in the crowd and had called his name and he had not been able to hear her over the sound of the city failing. The back of her mind had an inventory that ran parallel to her actual search—an inventory of moments, each one tagged with the question she could not ask aloud: *was I enough? Was I the thing he came back for? When the world is reduced to what you can carry, do I weigh enough to carry?*

She had not allowed this inventory to influence her behavior. She had been rigorous about that—had watched herself for signs of it infecting her decisions, had applied the same organizational discipline to her own psychology that she applied to intake lists and supply rotations. She had seen, at the camps she had passed through, what happened when that kind of fear became the engine rather than the passenger: people who stopped eating because they were afraid their person would not want them when found; people who moved in circles, searching the same routes because certainty was more terrifying than hope; people who started fights at camps because the anger was easier than the waiting.

She was not going to be those people.

She picked up her water cup and drank the rest of it and stood up and thanked the camp coordinator and walked out and continued east.

The rumor was noise. She was certain of this.

She was also certain that her hands were slightly unsteady as she walked, and that the steadiness would return, and that this was acceptable, and that she was not going to think about the woman in the rumor, and that she was going to do this by thinking about something concrete.

---

The concrete thing was a conversation she'd had at the previous camp, before the tower, which she had filed away to think about later.

A young couple, traveling with a toddler who had been asleep through most of the conversation. They had been moving west, away from Ashford—not because they were running from something specific, but because they had heard something, and the something had been enough to change their direction.

"There's an omega in Ashford," the young woman had said, with the particular tone of something verified through multiple sources. "In the elevated district. Makes things vanish."

Selene had been only partially paying attention.

"Vanish how?" she'd asked.

The woman had shrugged. "I don't know what they do exactly. Just that they—move things. Without touching them. My friend's group saw it happen. Someone's pack, just appeared somewhere else. And the omega did something that—" She made a gesture that meant nothing specifically. "It was impossible. Calm about it. That's what my friend kept saying. Not scared. Just—did the thing and kept moving."

Selene had made a noncommittal sound and moved on.

But the detail had lodged. *Calm about it.* Not the power—the calmness. Because that was unusual. Calmness under the specific pressure of manifesting something impossible was unusual in a way that ordinary survival skills were not. Most people who did something they couldn't explain panicked afterward, or performed calm over actual terror, or announced what they'd done because they needed someone else to confirm it had happened.

Calm about it, and kept moving.

She filed this. She did not know why she was filing it—the omega in Ashford was not relevant to her search, not connected to anything she was tracking. But the filing was automatic, the same system that had kept her functional in a world without systems, and she did not override it.

Omega. Elevated district. Makes things vanish. Calm.

A person like that would be useful to know. The kind of useful that rewrote calculations—not weapons-useful, but *changes what's possible* useful. If you were trying to build something after the world ended, you would want to know about people like that.

She stored the information and let it rest.

---

She found him three hours after the tower.

Or she thought she had found him—for sixty seconds, she was certain.

The jacket first. The particular dark blue of it, the cut of the shoulders, the way it hung on a frame that was the right size. She saw it from forty meters on the trail—saw it and stopped breathing and started moving without conscious decision, not running because running drew attention and because she was not certain yet and because some part of her was afraid of what certainty felt like.

Thirty meters. The walk of the person in the jacket—right height, right build, the set of the head she had been following in memory for twenty-six days.

Twenty meters. She called his name.

The person turned.

She stopped.

He was about Adrian's height and about Adrian's build and his hair was almost right and his face was the face of a stranger.

He looked at her with the polite alarm of someone who had been called by the wrong name in a public place. She looked at him. She looked at the jacket—it was Adrian's color but not Adrian's jacket, similar cut but different buttons, worn differently, longer at the hem.

"Sorry," she said. Her voice was level. She was extremely proud of the level.

"You looking for someone?" the man said.

"Yes," she said. "Not you. I apologize."

He nodded—the nod of someone who understood this kind of thing happened and did not need it explained—and continued down the trail.

Selene stood where she had stopped for a moment.

She held the feeling still, because moving through it too quickly would drive it underground and underground was where it would do the most damage. She let herself be in it: the sixty seconds of certainty, the twenty meters of hope, the fact that her body had moved before her mind had caught up, the particular grief of stopping at twenty meters and being wrong.

She had felt replaceable before the world ended. Not because of anything Adrian had done—he had never given her reason. But because she had felt it in smaller ways for longer, the low-grade awareness of a person who had grown up one arrangement of circumstances away from being left and had learned to read the distance between herself and the edge. Adrian had not made it worse. He had made it better, significantly better, for three years.

But the world had ended and the distance to the edge was shorter now.

She breathed in the way he had taught her, which was the way that worked—the one where you counted inward and held and then exhaled to a number that was higher than the rest. She did it until her hands were steady.

Then she looked at the trail ahead.

East. Still east. He was east, somewhere, in the direction the rumor had always pointed, and she was going to find him.

The rumor about the woman was noise. She had assessed it and found it lacking in specificity and reliability. She was not changing direction based on noise.

She walked.

The eastern horizon did what it had been doing for three days—gave off the low, steady glow of something she could not name, something that was neither fire nor fracture-light but had characteristics of both, something that was becoming more visible as she moved toward it.

She was moving toward it.

Not because of the glow—because of Adrian, because the trail led east, because east was where the rumor of him had been most consistent and most recent. The glow was incidental.

She did not think about the omega who made things vanish.

She did not think about the woman in the rumor.

She thought about Adrian's face when she was talking too fast about models and footnotes, the particular look of someone who was genuinely paying attention but was also a little in love with the fact that she was excited, and she held that face very carefully in the front of her mind and used it as a compass bearing.

East.

She was going to find him.

The trail continued. The horizon glowed.

She walked, and the unsent message stayed in her pocket, and the draft said *don't worry about me, just find me*, and somewhere out there in the direction she was walking, she believed—she continued to believe—that he was looking for her with the same urgency she was looking for him.

She had to believe that.

The alternative was a reckoning she was not ready for.

She walked until the light failed and the east glowed brighter than the west, and she did not stop walking until she found a safe place to sleep.

In her dreams, she saw his jacket again, forty meters ahead, and she called his name.

And this time he turned, and it was him.

She woke before she could reach him.
