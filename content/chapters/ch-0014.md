The problem with the instruments was that they had not been designed for the phenomenon.

Marcus had been working around this since day zero — since the atmospheric sensor had gone flat against a ceiling it was not calibrated to reach, since the mana density readings had exceeded every scale he had brought into the field and he had spent six days improvising from the gymnasium's salvage supplies. He had a physics background that was now, in the specific way that backgrounds became useful in inverse proportion to how impractical they had seemed at the time of acquisition, the most relevant thing about him. He understood what mana was doing without yet understanding what mana was, and the distinction between those two states of knowledge was the work.

His corner of the gymnasium held, by day thirteen:

A repurposed data logger he had rebuilt from a circuit board salvaged from a wall-mounted thermostat system that Jemison — the logistics man who had been managing the gymnasium's resources with impressive pragmatism — had permitted him to dismantle once Marcus explained what he needed it for. The logger could measure electromagnetic flux in a range that, while not designed for mana, produced readings that correlated with the bone-note frequency he had been tracking since day zero. Correlation was not identity. It was a start.

A compass that did not work in the normal sense — it had been behaving wrongly since day five, the needle drawn northeast rather than north with a consistency that suggested a source rather than interference. Marcus had been tracking the direction daily and the deviation was steady: fourteen degrees northeast of magnetic north, a constant he had noted and not yet explained.

A notebook that was now the third he had filled since day zero, the first two archived in the waterproof sleeve he kept in the interior of his bag because the notebooks were the instrument that none of the others could replace.

And, on the gym floor beside his makeshift table, Lily.

---

She had spent the morning drawing while he worked, which was a pattern they had established by day three and which suited both of them: she drew what she observed, he noted what he measured, and at irregular intervals one of them asked the other a question that the other tried to answer honestly. Lily at age six was constitutionally opposed to the comfortable answer; Marcus had spent most of the previous decade learning to answer her honestly before she noticed when he was softening the truth, which she always did.

"Dad," Lily said, without looking up from her drawing.

"Yes."

"The compass is pointing at the fracture."

He stopped writing. Looked at her.

She was still drawing — the cracked-sky series she had been maintaining since day zero, each entry with a date in the corner in the round block letters she had been practicing since January. "Fourteen degrees northeast. That's where the big fracture is. Over the harbor."

"How do you know it's fourteen degrees?"

She held up her drawing — two pages, side by side. The left page was her drawing of the compass from three days ago. The right page was today's. The needle angles were marked with a precise line in both, and she had drawn the comparison correctly. She had been doing this with her drawings for months, Marcus realized, and he had not been paying adequate attention. "I have all of them," she said. "It hasn't moved."

He looked at his own notes on the compass deviation. Hers were better.

"You're right," he said.

"It means the fracture is still there."

"It does."

"Can we measure it?" she asked. "Like, the distance?"

"Not with what I have."

"What would you need?"

"Two instruments in different positions. If I have two readings from two different positions, I can triangulate the source." He paused. "I'd need someone who could take one of the instruments to a location I specify and read the deviation at that location."

Lily considered this. "Ximena has a map."

"Yes."

"If you gave her the other compass—"

"I only have this compass."

"There are compasses in the hiking gear in the supply room," Lily said. "Three of them. I counted them when I was in there getting the gauze for Bree's knee."

Marcus looked at his daughter.

She looked back with the expression she reserved for situations where the answer was obvious and she was waiting for him to catch up.

He went to talk to Jemison about the hiking compasses.

---

He had the second reading by mid-afternoon, courtesy of Ximena, who had taken the second compass four blocks north to the position Marcus had calculated would produce a useful parallax angle, stood there for five minutes, and returned with a reading noted in the margin of her map with a precision that impressed him. She had been a civil engineer before the Rapture. Of course her measurements were precise.

Fourteen degrees northeast from position one. Eleven degrees northeast from position two.

He triangulated.

The source of the compass deviation was not the harbor fracture.

The harbor fracture was visible from the gymnasium roof — still present, still open, smaller in angular size than on day zero but present. It was due east from the gymnasium. The compass was pointing fourteen degrees northeast. The triangulation placed the source three kilometers northeast, which put it over the industrial waterfront district north of the harbor, a location that had not been the primary fracture site on day zero.

He sat with this for a time.

The compass deviation had been consistent for nine days, and the harbor fracture had been the most visible event. He had assumed correlation without adequately checking the assumption. The compass was not pointing at the harbor fracture. It was pointing at something else northeast, something that the compass identified as a magnetic-equivalent source, which in the new taxonomy of the world meant: a concentrated mana source, possibly a fracture too small or too obscured to be visible from his position, possibly something else.

He wrote: *Compass deviation 14° NE. Triangulated source: approximately 3 km northeast, industrial waterfront district. Not harbor fracture. Unknown source type. Sustained since day 5 at minimum (possibly earlier, first reading taken day 5). Stable direction suggests stationary source, not moving phenomenon. Investigation required when safe to approach.*

He underlined *stable* twice.

This was the kind of data point he had been building toward since day zero: not the dramatic, the visible, the already-documented end of the world, but the quiet anomaly that required instruments to find, the kind that suggested not chaos but *structure*. Things did not remain stable by accident. Things remained stable because something was holding them stable, and things that were held stable had characteristics, and characteristics had rules.

Rules could be learned.

---

Lily brought him lunch without being asked, which meant Iris had sent her, which meant Iris knew he had forgotten to eat. He ate the rice and dried protein mix that constituted the gymnasium's midday allocation without tasting it and did not stop working.

Lily sat beside him and ate her own allocation and looked at his notebook.

"What does mana do?" she asked.

"We don't know yet."

"We know it does something. The people whose eyes are different — they have it in them. I can see it."

Marcus looked up. "What do you mean, you can see it?"

"In the light from the fractures. Some people have a little bit of it — they glow a tiny bit in the fracture light when they're near a window. The changed ones have a lot, that's why their eyes are bright." She said this with the matter-of-fact quality she brought to observations that had seemed obvious to her for days. "You don't glow very much. Ximena doesn't. Mrs. Holt has a little. That man Jemison has none at all."

Marcus was very still. "When did you notice this?"

"Day two. In the morning light." She pointed to the gymnasium's high windows, where the fracture-light still supplemented the insufficient interior lighting. "Not in normal light. Only in that light."

He pulled his notebook toward him. Wrote: *Lily — apparent visual perception of mana presence in fracture light. Not confirmed, but consistent with her observational track record. Age 6. Framing: glow differential between individuals. Note: investigate. Possible secondary magic affinity? File for later.*

"Can you tell which direction the glow comes from strongest?" he asked.

She pointed northeast.

Exactly fourteen degrees northeast of straight north.

He wrote: *Lily's directional perception consistent with compass deviation. Independent confirmation of northeastern source.*

He sat back and looked at his daughter.

Lily finished her lunch. She picked up her drawing tools and began the day's new page.

"Dad," she said.

"Yes."

"The glowing people — can they do things with it? With the glow?"

"I don't know yet."

"Elena — the doctor lady in your friend's group who Ximena knows about — she helped that man with the hurt arm, right? And he didn't get better?"

"No."

"But someone in here — the woman with the shoulder, Priya — her shoulder is better than it was yesterday. I looked at it this morning when Priya wasn't watching."

Marcus frowned. "What made you look at Priya's shoulder?"

"She glows. A little more than most people. And her shoulder was wrong yesterday and less wrong today." Lily drew a careful line. "Maybe the glow helps with fixing things. The body things. Maybe that's what mana does to some people."

He thought about Elena Hale, who was three kilometers northwest of them in the outskirts barn, treating an infection that had killed a man today — he did not know this, would not know it for weeks, but he thought about healers as a category and about the relationship between the thing Lily was describing and the category that had existed in human imagination across cultures and across history, always called something different, always pointing at the same underlying phenomenon.

He wrote: *Healing magic: possible. Investigative priority after mana source location confirmed. Cross-reference: Elena Hale (medic, Rowan Hale's group) — anecdotal.*

"What are you writing?" Lily asked.

"What you told me."

She paused drawing. "Is it important?"

"It might be. I don't know yet."

"That's okay," she said. "You always figure it out eventually."

He was not certain that was true anymore. The world had grown faster than his instruments could follow, and he suspected this was a permanent condition rather than a temporary one, and he was still deciding whether that condition was frightening or freeing. Both, probably. The way most honest things were both.

---

At dusk he found a sample.

Not the kind of sample he had been collecting since the field — soil, atmospheric, water quality, the kind of sample that required a full lab to analyze and that he had been collecting anyway because the habit of building the record mattered even when the analysis was deferred. A living sample: a piece of vegetation growing in the gymnasium's cracked concrete floor beside the east wall, in the fracture-light that came through the high window.

The plant was not one he recognized.

This was, given his field, mildly alarming. He had a botany adjacent to his physical sciences work — the kind of cross-disciplinary familiarity that came from studying atmospheric phenomena in ecosystems. He knew the common plants of the Ashford region. He did not know this plant, which had emerged in four days from a crack in a gymnasium floor.

He photographed it with the camera Lily had been using for her field documentation. He took three samples with the sterile swab kit that Felicity — the nurse who had arrived at the barn, according to Jemison's secondhand account — had apparently left instructions about before leaving. He labeled the samples.

At nine in the evening, after lights-down, he lit the small reading light he had been rationing and reviewed the plant sample under the loupe he had brought from his university office along with the notebooks, because loopers were the kind of thing that survived personal crisis when properly packed.

The sample was fine. Not unusual. Normal plant cells, normal structure.

He turned off the reading light and sat in the dark.

Then he turned it back on.

He took the sample again, in the fracture light — not the reading light, the silver fracture glow from the high window.

The cells glowed.

Not all of them. The ones at the growing edge — the cells actively dividing, adding to the plant's structure in real time — emitted light in the fracture glow that the mature cells did not. As if the fracture energy were feeding the division process, contributing to the formation of new cell walls in a way that the plant's standard biochemistry did not account for.

He looked at this for a long time.

He wrote: *Day 13, 21:14. Unidentified plant sample from gymnasium floor fracture. Growing cells luminescent in fracture light. Mature cells: dark. Interpretation: mana absorption during cell division. Plant utilizing mana in growth mechanism. Implication: mana is a resource for biological processes. Not exclusively destructive. Not exclusively alien.*

He paused.

*Maybe integrated.*

He closed his notebook.

Iris appeared in the medical alcove doorway with a second cup of the gymnasium's coffee substitute, which was made from roasted grain and tasted like a memory of coffee rather than coffee itself. She set it beside his loupe and sat cross-legged on the floor the way she sat on all floors in difficult conversations.

"Lily told me," she said.

"What did she tell you?"

"About the glowing."

He had told Iris about the mana observations on day four, the numbers, the preliminary findings. He had not told her about Lily's independent confirmation of the directional source. "She's more observant than either of us."

"She is." Iris looked at the plant sample. "What does it mean?"

"I don't know yet."

"Approximately."

He thought about how to frame it. Iris's strength was not physics; her strength was people and logistics and the architecture of community. The framing that would be most useful to her was not equations. "It means the fractures aren't just damaging us. They're also — feeding. Something is happening to the biology of living things, and some of it is destructive and some of it is constructive, and I don't know yet which factors determine which outcome."

Iris looked at the plant.

"It might mean," Marcus continued, "that the people who survive this, and adapt to it, will be — different. Than we were. Not in a way I can predict yet."

She was quiet.

"That's frightening," she said.

"Yes."

"And interesting."

"Yes."

She picked up the cup she had brought and handed it to him. "Drink this. Then sleep. Tomorrow you figure out what the compass is pointing at."

"I think I know what it's pointing at."

"Then tomorrow you confirm it."

He drank the coffee substitute and did not argue, which was the correct response to Iris making a decision. He went to the cot they shared and looked at Lily sleeping beside them with her drawing satchel tucked under her arm and her newest page visible in the fracture-light glow from the high window.

The page showed the gymnasium floor, the crack, the plant. In Lily's drawing, the plant glowed.

She had drawn it the way it looked to her, which was how it actually looked, which she had known and he had not.

He closed his eyes and thought about what it meant that a six-year-old could perceive, without instruments, what his instruments were only beginning to approach with calibrated uncertainty.

He would write it down in the morning.

For now: sleep, and the sound of ninety people breathing in a city that was learning what it was made of, one observation at a time.
