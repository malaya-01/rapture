## i. Cassian

The water had been off since Monday.

He had known this was coming—had filled every container in the apartment on the first night, had moved the bathtub stopper and let the tub fill, had arranged the bottles in a line on the counter in descending size, a row of priorities wearing plastic labels that no longer meant anything. On Day Three he counted them. It was the same thing he did at the office with shipping manifests: numbers in, numbers out, discrepancies flagged for someone more important to fix.

There was no one more important to flag this to. He was the only person in the building.

He had learned this by listening. The couple on the third floor had left the second night—he had heard them on the stairs, the low urgency of two people moving fast and not speaking, and he had pressed his ear to the door and heard them go and done nothing because there was nothing to do. The woman in 4B whose dog barked every morning had either left or stopped opening her windows. The barking had stopped on Day Two.

He did not know if the silence was better or worse.

Cassian Reed ate half a granola bar and drank a measured cup of water and made a list.

He made lists the way other people made coffee—automatically, before anything else, as a form of preparation that was also a form of courage. Water: six liters if he was careful, three days at survival minimum. Food: two weeks if he stretched it, one if he didn't feel like it. Candles: enough. The battery lantern had most of its charge. He had his phone even though the phone had no signal, because the phone had a flashlight and a compass and seventeen percent battery that he was not touching.

Outside, the city made noise.

Not the noise of a city that was functioning. The noise of a city that was something else—a different kind of living, lower and less organized and occasionally very sudden. He had heard two car crashes in the night that were not car crashes, that had the particular weight of something large moving through a space built for things that were not large. He had heard sounds that he categorized as human and sounds that he did not categorize at all.

He sat at the table by the window and looked at the map. The same city map, folded soft at the creases. He had grown up with this map and he was revising it, block by block, based on what he could see and hear from the window.

The four blocks south of his building: multiple fires, now mostly burned out, smoke but no active flame as of this morning. Passable if he hugged the west side of the street.

The bodega on the corner of Fifth: still standing. Front window intact as of last evening. Possibly still stocked, possibly already stripped by whoever had been on the street before him.

Fourth Street, where he had seen the thing that had been a dog: blocked. Not by the thing—he hadn't seen it since, and absence was not the same as absence—but by two overturned vehicles and the spreading stain on the asphalt that he had looked at for one second and looked away from.

He traced the route with his finger. He needed water more than food. There was a corner store two blocks east with a refrigeration section that had probably been warm for two days, which meant the water was fine and the meat was not, and he did not need to think about the meat. Two blocks east. He had his apartment key in one hand and a fire iron from the nail salon below in the other, which he had taken the first night when he went down to check what was available.

He had never held a weapon. He did not think the fire iron counted as one, but it was metal and it was heavy and it had a hook on one end that he preferred not to imagine using.

He listened at his door for three full minutes. He had counted. Then he opened it.

---

The stairwell smelled like the building had been holding its breath.

Layers: old cooking smells from apartments now empty, something chemical and sweet from the floor below, the faint copper of water that had sat too long in pipes that were dry now. He had learned the stairwell the way he had learned his childhood neighborhood—not by looking but by trusting his feet to remember the patterns, the way the second landing always echoed, the way the lobby door hung slightly against its frame.

The lobby was empty. Bright, because the glass front let in the fractured daylight, and wrong, because that light came from a sky that had not been right in three days.

He pushed through into the street and stopped.

The block was quieter than it should have been. The silence had a quality he recognized from the hours after snowstorms: the particular absence of the city's usual frequencies, replaced by something lower and more attentive. He moved fast, keeping close to the buildings, watching both ends of the street.

On Second Avenue he passed the first dead.

She was against the wall of a laundromat. He could tell almost nothing about her except that she had been wearing a red coat and that the coat was no longer red in most places. He did not stop. He did not look away fast enough, either—that was the problem with looking away fast, it meant you had seen enough to know, and now he knew, and he carried the knowledge with him for the rest of the block, the rest of the day, perhaps the rest of the time he would spend in this city.

The corner store was unlocked. Someone had broken the glass door rather than the window, which suggested either panic or efficiency, and the inside smelled of warm meat and turned dairy and the flat sweetness of spilled soda. He breathed through his mouth and moved to the back.

The water was still there. Most of it—the individual bottles had been taken but three cases of twenty-four were on the bottom shelf, untouched, too heavy for someone in a hurry. He loaded his bag. He took two cans of beans from a shelf that had been mostly cleared of anything good and one bag of rice that someone had knocked to the floor and not retrieved.

He was at the door when he heard them.

Shuffling. More than one. Not in the store—outside, on the cross street, moving in the particular rhythm he had watched from his window and had not been able to name. Slow and purposeful and wrong, the gait of things that had once been people and had kept only the direction, not the reasoning behind it.

He pressed himself against the wall beside the door and waited.

They passed.

Three of them—he counted by sound, three separate rhythms, three separate variations on the dragging-weight shuffle that made his teeth ache in a way he could not explain. They moved south and did not turn and he watched through the broken door as the last one cleared his sightline. Grey-green skin the color of old concrete. Torn office clothes. One was missing a shoe, and the bare foot left a print on the asphalt that Cassian could not think about.

He went back the way he had come.

He was two blocks from his building when the sky pulsed.

Not the fracture-light—something different, a single deep thrum that rolled through the air like a second heartbeat, felt in the chest rather than heard by the ear. On the horizon above the harbor district, a column of amber-gold flame rose to a height that made the buildings beneath it look like toys, then dispersed, slowly, into the fractured clouds.

Ashford was burning from the east.

He ran the last two blocks. He got inside. He locked the door and sat on the floor with his back against it and waited for his hands to stop shaking.

They stopped. Eventually.

He moved the water to the counter. He counted what he had. He made a new list.

The math was better than it had been this morning. That would have to be enough.

## ii. Rowan

The rescue barn had seventeen people in it on Day Three and Rowan Hale had promised himself he would not count them again.

He counted them anyway. Habit. You ran accountability on your people the same way you ran it on equipment—not because you distrusted them but because the information mattered and you were the one responsible for having it. Elena. Atlas. Leon. The volunteer squad couple—Tom and Risa—who had been at the barn when they arrived and had the good sense to give Rowan the generator without argument. The family of four who had appeared at dawn on Day Two from the Miller's Cutoff direction, the mother carrying an infant that had not cried since they arrived, the father with a bite on his left forearm that Elena had cleaned and wrapped and was watching.

The father's name was Paul.

Paul was thirty-four, slightly built, the kind of man who chose careful words and offered both hands when he shook yours. He had told Rowan his family's route in detail—the same routes Rowan had taken, the same roads that had gone wrong in the same ways—and then he had sat down and helped Atlas sort the medical supply inventory because helping was the only thing Paul knew how to do with fear.

Rowan watched the arm.

Elena said nothing about it in front of Paul. She said things to Rowan at night, quiet and specific, medical and terrible. She had cleaned and debrided and dressed and she was watching. That was the honest version. The honest version was the only one Rowan had ever been able to work with, even when he wanted a different one.

He ran his radio frequencies at six, at ten, at noon. Results: static, static, a voice on the emergency management band that repeated the same twelve-second message in a loop before breaking mid-sentence into something that sounded like language and was not.

At two in the afternoon the something came back to the tree line.

Not the same creature Rowan had faced the first night—or if it was the same creature, it had brought company. He counted shapes through the barn's high window: four, possibly five, moving between the trees with the stop-and-start pattern of things that were navigating by sound or smell rather than sight. They had not come closer since he had reinforced the barn door. They seemed to understand the concept of a barrier, which was more than he would have guessed at the beginning of the week.

"Atlas," he said.

Atlas appeared at his elbow. Nine years old and already had his father's habit of arriving without being heard.

"Stay from the windows," Rowan said. "Wake your mother. Tell her quiet."

"She's already awake," Atlas said. "She's been awake."

Of course she had. Rowan crossed to Elena, who was rewrapping Paul's arm with the careful movements of someone doing math in her head while her hands worked.

"Five minutes," he said. "Interior room."

She looked up. The interior room was the supply closet—twelve feet square, two walls of solid cinderblock, a shelf of water and food and the emergency bag she had packed without being asked. She had prepared it on arrival. She always prepared the exit before she needed it.

"Leon's asleep," she said.

"Atlas will carry him."

"Atlas is nine."

"Leon's seven. Atlas can carry Leon." He made himself look at Paul. "Mr. Harmon. I need you to move your family to the back room."

Paul looked at his arm. He had the eyes of a man who had been having an honest conversation with himself and did not like what it was saying.

"The bite," Paul said.

"We don't know yet," Rowan said. This was true.

"I feel cold," Paul said. "I've felt cold since last night."

This was also true, and Elena's hands had stilled for one second when Paul said it, and in that second Rowan understood everything that was going to happen in the next twelve hours and chose not to say so. He put his hand on Paul's shoulder instead.

"Back room," he said. "Your family first."

Paul moved. He moved with the dignity of a man who understood his situation and was choosing to protect his children from it for as long as that choice remained available. Rowan watched him go and felt the particular weight that firefighters knew and did not talk about—the weight of standing in the doorway between the living and what came next, knowing the difference was sometimes made by forces that had nothing to do with effort or worth.

The things in the tree line began to move closer.

Rowan picked up the fire axe.

---

The next two hours required everything he had.

He was not a soldier. He was a firefighter—which was different in specific ways that mattered enormously in a burning building and mattered not at all when the thing coming through the barn's side door had the shape of a person and the eyes of something that had stopped being a person before the word *person* had time to complete itself in Rowan's understanding.

He killed it with the axe.

He did not think too carefully about the word *killed*. It was accurate. The thing had stopped moving after the third blow and had not resumed. He checked. He was thorough.

There were two more outside.

Tom took one with a length of rebar and a competence that suggested he had been more than a volunteer rescue squad member before the week started, and Rowan filed that information away for later, for the moment when later existed.

The third one Rowan took.

After, he stood in the doorway and looked at the tree line and at the sky beyond it. The sky was wrong in three directions. To the east, where Ashford sat, the fracture glow pulsed with amber-gold light that meant fire—real fire, structure fire, the kind his entire career had been oriented toward. To the north, a different glow, colder and higher, one of the fracture lines having opened into something he didn't have a classification for. To the west: smoke. Black and thick and rising in columns that spoke of a different kind of burning, slow and petroleum-smelling.

No center held.

He went inside and washed his hands in water he couldn't afford to spare and told Elena the tree line was clear.

She looked at his hands. At his face.

"Paul?" he asked.

She shook her head once.

He nodded. He went to tell the family.

## iii. Adrian

The south route had been wrong since Day Two.

Adrian Hale stood at a corner he had memorized from the city map and looked at what the map had not prepared him for: an intersection where the road surface itself had buckled along a seam, not earthquake-buckled but something smoother, as if the ground had been lifted from beneath and set back down slightly wrong. The seam ran from the storm drain on the east side to the wall of the pharmacy on the west, and glittered along its edges with something that was not quartz.

Selene had her back to the wall of the bodega on the corner, watching the street they had come from.

"Around?" she asked.

"Through the parking structure. Two blocks north and back south."

"The lot on Clement?"

"You know the lot on Clement."

"I came through it last winter with four hundred pages of translation contract and a broken umbrella and a very strong opinion about its lighting." She said it matter-of-factly, the kind of shorthand people developed after enough shared time—not a joke exactly, just a reference, a way of saying *I trust your direction because I know this city through your eyes and mine.* "Lead."

They moved.

The city on Day Three was not the city of Day One. Day One had been chaos in the active sense—visible running, audible screaming, the particular frenzy of systems failing in real time. Day Three was chaos in its settled form: the stillness that followed the first collapse, a landscape of abandoned choices. Cars at angles. Doors open. A stroller upright on the sidewalk with nothing in it. A pharmacy with its front window intact and its shelves visible from the street, half-stocked, unlooted—either no one had thought of it yet or everyone had decided something was guarding it.

He checked as he passed. Nothing visible inside.

He filed the pharmacy.

They were two blocks from the parking structure when they heard the voices.

A group—he counted at least eight by sound—barricaded in the print shop on the north side of the street. He could see them through the plate glass: adults, at least two children, a man standing in the window watching the street with his hands flat against the glass like a message.

Selene had already stopped.

"Adrian."

"I see them."

"We can't leave them there."

"We don't know their situation."

"Their situation is that they're alive and they don't know where to go." She said this without heat, which was worse than if she'd said it with heat. This was her reasonable voice, the one she used when she had already decided and was explaining the conclusion to him as a courtesy.

He thought about Rowan waiting. About the south route. About the list of things he did not know, which was getting longer.

He crossed to the print shop door and knocked three times.

---

What followed was messy and took forty minutes and used resources he had been rationing with great care, but at the end of it the group from the print shop had a direction—the same rescue barn Rowan had mentioned, far enough south that Rowan's radio silence did not necessarily mean what it might mean—and the woman who had been bandaging children's knees with her own shirt had the medical kit from Adrian's bag, which left him one antiseptic wipe and three bandages and the knowledge that Selene would not say anything about this because she had handed over her own kit first.

He caught her eye as they left the print shop with the group.

She looked at him with the expression that had been building since they packed this morning—not the soft expression, not the brave expression, but the third one, the one that meant *I love you and you are exhausting and I would not change either of those facts.*

He looked away first. He did not trust himself to acknowledge it correctly with eight strangers watching.

They moved south.

---

By evening the city was burning from three directions.

He stood at the edge of the elevated section of Route 9, where the overpass had not yet collapsed and you could see most of Ashford's center, and he looked at it. Selene stood beside him. The group they had picked up was somewhere behind them, moving at the pace of the slowest child, and in twenty minutes he would go back and help carry that child, but for now he had twenty minutes of looking at the city that had been his for six years.

Smoke columns, east and west and north. The fracture glow pulsing in the sky. The amber-gold fire light from the harbor district where the dragon had passed. And moving in the streets below—things he could see only as shapes from this height, wrong shapes, too many wrong shapes, the visible population of a city that was no longer primarily human.

He thought of Rowan. Of the last text.

He thought of Selene's hand in his for the last three hours, not romantically—practically, the way two people held on in water, using each other as buoyancy.

He took out the map he had been carrying since the apartment and looked at it for thirty seconds and then put it away.

"We're going to make it south," he said.

"I know," Selene said.

She said it the way she said things she was not entirely certain of but had decided to believe. He recognized the decision in her voice. He loved her for it—the ordinary, complicated love of someone who had shared enough time with another person to know the difference between their comfort and their courage.

Behind them, Ashford finished burning what it had been.

Whatever it was becoming had only just begun.
