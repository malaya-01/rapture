Lily had been asking the same question in five different ways for eleven days.

The first version, on Day Thirteen, had been direct: *Why did the sky break?* Marcus had been in the middle of his third field observation of the morning, crouched over a sample of soil that had begun exhibiting the luminescent property he'd first noted in the blood sample nine days prior, and the question had arrived so cleanly and without preamble that he'd answered it before he could think about whether he should.

"I don't know yet," he'd said. Which was true, and which was also the most honest answer he had given anyone in two weeks—including himself, including Iris, including the pages of his notebook where he had been conducting a kind of running argument with the evidence he was accumulating.

Lily had processed this. She was seven. Seven was the age, Marcus had observed across many years of watching the children of his colleagues at the university, where the distinction between *no one knows* and *you specifically don't know* became important. She had decided, apparently, that he was the second category, and had returned to the question every two days with a slightly different angle: *What made the cracks? Why did the monsters come through? Does the sky still hurt? What happens if it breaks all the way?*

He had answered each one honestly, which meant he had answered each one incompletely, which was—he was slowly understanding—not the same as answering badly.

---

They were moving. They had been moving for most of ten days now, the family unit of three plus the two quiet survivors who had attached themselves to Marcus's vehicle on Day Seventeen and had not yet found a reason to leave—a woman named Opal who had been a veterinarian and who had taken over Lily's health monitoring with an efficiency that Marcus found enormously relieving, and her partner Dex, a contractor who thought in load-bearing terms and had immediately and without being asked begun assessing the structures they sheltered in for stability.

Marcus was grateful for them. They had arrived at a moment when he was beginning to notice, with the precise awareness of a man who documented things, that his emotional resources were running low and that running them to zero while his family depended on him would be a specific failure he could not recover from.

The vehicle—a modified camping trailer they had taken from an abandoned sporting goods lot on Day Twelve, the truck ahead of it one they'd similarly acquired—carried everything. Marcus's field lab: such as it was, which was to say a collection of instruments rated for outdoor research that were surviving the apocalypse better than their manufacturers had ever anticipated. Iris's supplies: organized with a precision that put Marcus's own organizational habits to shame, the food and medical stores catalogued and rotated on a schedule that had kept them fed and healthy for twenty-four days in conditions that should have made both impossible. Lily's things: two stuffed animals she had chosen deliberately from their apartment on Day Two, a box of colored pencils, and the growing collection of objects she had been accumulating in the way seven-year-olds accumulated things—every interesting stone, every feather, every scrap of material that had an unusual texture or color or weight.

The stone from yesterday was in that collection now. Marcus was still thinking about it.

---

They stopped at midday in a place that Dex had assessed and Marcus had agreed offered reasonable shelter: a roadside rest stop that had been recently abandoned, showing evidence of organized departure rather than panic. There was a structure still standing—a concrete block building with restrooms and a covered picnic area—and they pulled in and stopped the engine and let the silence of the open road settle around them.

The silence of the road was different from the silence of Ashford. Marcus had grown up in cities, had done his research at urban universities, had taken field trips to rural sites with the particular disorientation of someone who had never quite learned to read a landscape that didn't have buildings in it. The road silence included wind, insects—fewer than before, but returning in patches that he'd been noting with increasing interest—and the very distant, very faint sound that he had finally identified as coming from the east.

Not a sound, exactly. A frequency. Something below the threshold of language but above the threshold of awareness, the kind of thing that manifested as unease rather than sound, that made Lily put her hand on the side of the truck and say *I feel that* and made Iris press her lips together.

Marcus had been measuring it for four days. The readings were consistent. The direction was consistent. He had a hypothesis he had not yet shared with Iris, because sharing it would require him to commit to it, and committing to it required him to be ready for the conversation about what it meant.

Lily was at the table with her colored pencils when she asked the fifth version.

"If something wanted to break the sky," she said, not looking up from what she was drawing, "how would it do that?"

Marcus looked up from the sample he was studying. Iris, across the table, caught his eye.

"That's a very different question," Marcus said, carefully.

"I know. The other ones weren't right." She made a mark with the yellow pencil, dissatisfied, switched to orange. "The sky didn't just break. Something happened to it. Millie says things don't happen by themselves."

Millie was the stuffed bear who had accompanied Lily since she was three and who had become, in the past twenty-four days, a kind of philosophical interlocutor. Marcus had learned to take Millie's arguments seriously as indicators of where Lily was in her thinking.

"Millie is correct," Marcus said. "Things don't happen without causes."

"So what caused it?"

"I don't know yet." He paused. "But I have some ideas."

Lily put down the orange pencil and looked at him. This was the attention she gave when she had decided something was worth the full version. He had learned to recognize it. "Tell me."

Iris said, "Lily—"

"No," Lily said, still looking at Marcus. "He always explains better when I ask." She said this without accusation, as pure observation. It was accurate.

Marcus folded his hands on the table, the way he had learned to do when he was preparing to say something carefully. "The sky didn't break randomly. The fractures follow patterns—there's a geometry to where they appear and how they spread. Geometry means mathematics. Mathematics means underlying rules."

"So something knows the rules."

"Something obeys them. Whether it knows them is a different question."

She thought about this. "Is it like gravity?"

"It might be related to something like that. We've been calling it mana—that's a placeholder name, a word from old mythology that we're borrowing because we don't have a better one yet. It's a form of energy. It exists in the ground, in living things, in the air. Before Rapture it was too faint to measure with ordinary instruments. Now—" He glanced at his instruments. "Now it's measurable by things I built in a camping trailer."

"Where did it come from?"

"From somewhere beyond where we could see." He chose his words. "There are things in physics—real physics, the kind I studied at university—that describe structures like what we're seeing. Dimensions. Layers of reality that exist alongside the one we know but that ordinarily we can't reach. The fractures might be—doors."

Lily absorbed this with the calm of a child who had not yet learned to dismiss the impossible. "Doors to where the monsters come from?"

"Doors to where things come from. Monsters, yes. But also—" He thought of the reading he'd taken last night, the particular signature that was distinct from monster-activity, that was structured in a way that monsters didn't produce. "Also other things."

"What other things?"

"That's the question I'm trying to answer."

She looked at him for a long moment, seven years old and the most serious thing in the room. "You're scared of the answer."

It wasn't a question. Marcus looked at Iris, who was watching him with the expression he'd learned to read as *she's right and you know it*.

"Yes," he said. "A little. But I'm more scared of not knowing than of knowing."

Lily picked up the orange pencil again. "That's like me with the dark," she said. "When I don't know what's in the dark I'm more scared than when I know it's empty."

"Exactly like that."

She drew for a moment. Then: "Is it empty? The dark?"

He looked at his instruments.

"Not entirely," he said.

---

Iris waited until Lily was asleep in the back of the trailer before she said what she had been waiting to say since noon.

They sat outside in the late afternoon light, the two of them with cups of reconstituted coffee that was not good and was better than nothing, and Iris said: "We need to stop moving."

Marcus did not pretend to misunderstand. "I know."

"I mean actually stop. For more than two days."

"I know."

"Lily is—" She paused. "She's fine. She's adapting better than I expected. Better than me. But she needs consistency. A routine that lasts more than a week. Something that looks like a fixed point."

Marcus turned his cup in his hands. The coffee was at exactly the temperature that made warming your hands on the cup feel worthwhile but made drinking it an act of optimism. "I've been watching the frequency readings."

"I know you have."

"There's something to the east. Not just the fractures—something more structured. A specific signature that repeats on a consistent bearing. It started weak four days ago and it's getting stronger." He set the cup down. "I want to understand what it is before we commit to a position."

"And if it takes another week to understand?"

"Then we move sooner than that." He looked at her. "I won't sacrifice the family for data. I promise."

She looked at him for a long moment, doing the calculation he knew she was doing—the calculation of whether she believed him, whether the version of him that existed now under pressure and deprivation and fear was the same version who had made that promise or a different one.

"I believe you," she said. "But Lily comes first. You understand that."

"She always has."

"Say it again."

"She always has, Iris." He met her eyes. "Whatever I find in these readings—Lily first."

She took his hand. Her fingers were cold from the cup and warm beneath that from the person she still was under three weeks of catastrophe, and Marcus felt, with a precision that belonged to observation rather than sentiment, that the distance between catastrophe and love was exactly the width of a held hand.

They sat like that until the light changed.

---

Lily found it when they were breaking camp the next morning.

She had been in the habit of ranging slightly from the group during packing—not far, and always visible, a habit Opal had established on Day Eighteen by walking the perimeter with her and teaching her what to look for in the way of exit routes and structural risk. Lily had taken the lesson in the way she took all lessons, with the absolute seriousness of a child for whom the world had recently proven that adults telling her things were important was not performance.

She was on the far edge of the rest stop's concrete pad when she stopped.

Marcus saw her from across the space. Saw her crouch. Saw her put both hands flat on something at ground level with the careful posture she used for things she wasn't sure about.

He was beside her in thirty seconds.

"Don't pick it up yet," he said.

"I wasn't going to." She looked up at him. "It's warm."

He crouched beside her.

The stone was roughly the size of his fist. Slate-grey, smooth, the color and texture of a beach stone that had spent centuries in water and come out polished. It lay half-embedded in the crack between two concrete slabs in a way that might have been natural—except that the crack was recent, and the stone was older than the concrete by an order of magnitude that Marcus could estimate but not calculate precisely from sight alone.

It was warm.

Not heated by the morning sun, which had been up for forty minutes and was not yet strong enough to warm concrete, let alone a rock in shadow. Warm from the inside. Not uncomfortably warm—the warmth of a hand that had been closed for a few minutes, something just above body temperature.

And on its surface, on the upper face that had been visible and on the sides he could see once he tilted his head, markings.

Carved, not printed. Deliberate in a way that erosion could not produce. The lines were precise—not primitive, not accidental—but they did not match any writing system Marcus knew from seven years of academic study that had ranged widely enough to include most known ancient scripts. These were not Sumerian. Not proto-Sinaitic. Not Linear A or B or any of the undeciphered scripts he'd spent time with in his third year when he was still trying to decide whether his vocation was language or materials.

He took out his field notebook and sketched them.

His hands were steady, which surprised him. He had been expecting, at the first encounter with genuine archaeological anomaly in the wild, to feel the particular tremor of scholarly excitement that he associated with archive discovery. What he felt instead was something cooler and more careful, the feeling of a man who had been measuring an approaching frequency for four days and was now looking at a possible answer and was not sure he wanted to be right.

"What does it say?" Lily asked.

"I don't know yet."

"But it says something."

He looked at the markings. Symmetrical in structure but not rigidly so—there was a flow to the composition, a directionality, a sense that one end was beginning and another was end or that the text wrapped in some way he could not parse without a key. "Yes. I think it says something."

"Is it from the dark?" she asked, meaning the dark he had told her was not entirely empty.

He turned the sketch page and wrote his preliminary assessment in the careful small hand he used for things he might need to read in bad light: *Unknown script. Pre-Rapture age unknown. Thermal anomaly. Consistent bearing with eastern frequency. Carved, not stamped. Intentional. First physical artifact of—*

He stopped writing.

First physical artifact of what. He did not know what category this belonged to yet. He had measured a frequency. He had documented a stone. He had told his daughter the dark was not entirely empty.

He was building a picture, and the picture was taking a shape he didn't have language for, and the shape was—

"Lily," he said, "don't touch the stone yet. I need to take some readings first."

She sat back on her heels with the patience of a child who had learned that scientific process was not negotiation. "Okay."

He took out his instruments.

The readings came back immediately and consistently, with a clarity that the previous four days had not given him—not the background frequency of the atmosphere but a specific, structured signal, a tight signature that his best current instrument was barely capable of capturing but was definitely capturing.

It was the same frequency as the eastern bearing.

The stone was pointing east. Not physically pointing—it was a stone. But its signal matched the bearing he'd been tracking, the direction he had been unable to explain and had been moving toward anyway, the frequency that Lily felt through the side of the truck.

He wrote: *The stone and the signal are the same event.*

Then: *This changes what I'm looking for.*

Then, in the margin, in Lily's handwriting—she had borrowed his pen while he was measuring and written in the deliberate block letters of a child doing something careful—she had written: *WHAT IS IT.*

Not a question mark. She had decided the question marks were for things you doubted. This was something she was certain needed an answer.

Marcus looked at the stone, still warm, carved with language from nowhere he recognized, and for the first time in twenty-four days felt not afraid of not knowing but entirely, urgently, alive in not knowing—the feeling that had made him a scholar in the first place, that had nothing to do with careers or credentials and everything to do with the particular electricity of standing at the edge of a thing too large to see whole.

He was going to understand this. He did not know how long it would take. He did not know what it would cost.

But the stone was warm in the morning, and the markings were old, and the signal pointed east, and Marcus Vale—scholar, husband, father, man who had chosen notebooks over weapons on evacuation day and had been proven right by the world's refusal to be simply survived—felt the unmistakable sensation of being in exactly the right place for exactly the right work.

"Pack the stone carefully," he told Lily. "Wrap it in cloth. Keep it separate from your other things."

She looked at him. "Is it dangerous?"

"I don't know."

"You're going to figure it out?"

"Yes."

She wrapped it in the sleeve she pulled from a piece of clothing she had already designated for parts—a practical child's practical decision—and placed it with the deliberate care of someone who had been told this was important. Then she carried it to the trailer and climbed inside, and Marcus watched her go, and Iris appeared at his shoulder and said, "What is it?"

"I don't know yet," he said, for the fifth time in eleven days.

But it was the first time he said it and felt something opening rather than closing.
