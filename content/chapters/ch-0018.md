She had been awake for thirty-one hours when the boy's temperature reached forty-two.

She knew the number because she had been tracking it in half-hour intervals since his mother carried him in at three the previous morning — not in the barn's medical alcove, which was occupied, but in the corner of the main space she had cleared with two cots and a folding screen salvaged from the gymnasium run. She had the thermometer from the clinic stock Felicity had brought, which was functional and which she had calibrated against the clinic's own records; she trusted the number.

Forty-two degrees Celsius in an eight-year-old body was the number at which the triage calculus changed. Not from crisis to acceptable — from manageable crisis to diminishing-returns crisis, the threshold where the interventions she had available were less effective than the interventions she did not have and could not obtain.

She had been managing the interventions she had.

Ice packs: the clinic's cold packs, which she had been rotating in four-minute intervals across the neck and axilla and groin, the standard approach for high-fever reduction before the world had ended and with it the hospital's cooling blankets and IV antipyretics and the pediatric intensivist she had been calling in her head since midnight the way she sometimes called in other people when she was at the edge of what she could provide alone.

Fluids: oral, carefully administered, a specific quantity per hour calibrated to his weight and the fever's contribution to his fluid loss, because dehydration compound on top of fever was the second problem that became the reason the first problem won.

Medication: the last of the barn's pediatric ibuprofen, which Felicity had confirmed was the correct dose and which was doing something — the temperature had held at forty-one-point-seven for four hours before climbing to forty-two — but not enough.

She kept working.

His mother sat at the cot's edge across from Elena. Her name was Priya and she was the engineer's wife who had arrived at the barn with Theo on day five, and Elena had come to understand her in seventeen days as a person whose stillness was not passivity but attention — who processed quietly and acted when she had finished processing and who was, right now, sitting very still and very close to her son and watching Elena's hands because she was finished processing and had decided that watching Elena's hands was the action available to her.

Elena did not tell her to go sleep. She had told people to go sleep when the situation allowed it, and this was not a situation that allowed it, and Priya would know if Elena pretended otherwise.

---

At three in the morning, Rowan appeared.

She had told him she was fine when she had last seen him, which had been nine hours ago, and she had been fine in the sense that she was functional and the child was still alive. Rowan's sense of *fine* when applied to Elena meant something different than her sense of fine when applied to herself, and he had learned, over years, to read the difference in the set of her shoulders rather than the words she used.

He brought her tea and a protein bar and sat on the stool at the edge of the screening without coming inside, which was correct — she needed the space around the cot clear and he knew that.

"Report," he said.

She gave it: temperature, interventions, current status, projection.

He listened the way he listened in emergencies — without expression, taking in the information without performing a response to it, because performed responses cost time that information-processing needed. Then: "What do you need?"

"What I can't have." She turned another cold pack. "Pediatric critical care. IV antibiotic — I have adult-dose oral which is imprecise at his weight."

"Felicity?"

"She's as experienced as I am and has the same supplies I do."

He was quiet.

"There's nothing more you can do on logistics," she said. "You've gotten me what there is. This is what there is."

"Elena—"

"Go check the watch rotation. Come back in two hours. If there's a change I'll call you."

He went.

She knew he stood outside the folding screen for a few minutes before she heard his footsteps moving away, which was also correct — he needed to stand there for a few minutes, and she let him, and he went when he was ready.

She turned back to the boy.

His name was Soren. He was eight years old and had come into the barn with the body of a child who ate well and played outside and did not have a file yet with a pediatrician who knew his history. He had a fever because he had a wound on his lower leg, received on the transit to the barn, that had been cleaned and dressed and that had developed an infection despite cleaning and dressing and twice-daily monitoring by Elena herself. The infection was bacterial and it was winning.

She had the antibiotic. It was the wrong form and the wrong dose and she had been calculating and recalculating the adjusted dose for six hours with increasing desperation at the math, and what the math said was that she was at the edge of what she could do for this child with what she had.

She knew what being at the edge of what she could do felt like. She had been at that edge many times. It felt like a surface that had no further, like a door that was simply a wall with hinges.

She worked the cold pack rotation. She monitored the fluid intake. She sat beside Soren and watched his chest rise and fall with the focused attention of a person whose only meaningful work was maintaining the conditions under which he might improve.

At five in the morning, his temperature reached forty-two-point-four.

She made the decision she had been approaching for three hours: a second dose of the adult antibiotic at the lower bound of the adjusted calculation, accepting the imprecision, accepting that imprecision was the risk she had to take against the certainty of the alternative.

She administered it. She documented the dose, the time, the rationale.

She sat back.

She held Soren's hand because his mother was asleep — had finally slept, slumping sideways on the cot, twenty minutes ago — and because no one should have a high fever in the dark and be alone in it.

She held his hand and she did the thing she had been doing since day three when she had not had enough and had never had enough and would never have enough: she stayed.

---

He died at six forty-two in the morning.

The temperature did not spike further. It dropped slightly, in the specific way temperatures dropped when the body gave up the fight, and she had seen this enough times to know the difference between the drop that meant improvement and the drop that meant something else entirely.

She checked the pulse. Waited. Checked again.

Priya woke at the change — mothers woke at the change, always — and looked at Elena's face, and Elena met her eyes, and whatever was in Elena's face was true enough that Priya did not need words for it. She made the sound that a mother made. Elena came around the cot and put her arms around the woman and held on.

This was not clinical. This was not protocol. She had been told, in training, to maintain professional distance in patient deaths, and she had spent fifteen years finding the useful parts of that guidance and the harmful parts and discarding the harmful parts. Priya needed to be held, and Elena could hold her, and that was what she did.

---

She told Rowan at seven, when he came back.

He stood in the barn doorway and received the information the way he received all hard information: without expression, adding it to the load. Then he came inside and found Elena sitting on the barn floor with her back against the wall below the medical cabinet and sat beside her without speaking.

She had sent Priya to the back of the barn, where Felicity and Weaver were, where people who needed tending were tended without ceremony. She had cleaned the corner. She had stripped the cot. She had written in her medical notes: *Soren — patient died 6:42 a.m., Day 17. Fever, bacterial infection, inadequate pharmaceutical resources. All available interventions applied. Outcome: terminal.*

All available interventions. Applied.

She was very tired.

"How long since you slept?" Rowan asked.

"I'll sleep when the morning watch changes."

"Elena."

"Rowan." She turned her head to look at him. "I know what you're going to say."

"You need—"

"I need to check the other three patients in the alcove and document Soren and tell Atlas that the things Leon is asking about death are good questions and he should keep asking them and also keep him away from the medical area this morning." She paused. "And then I'll sleep."

He looked at her for a long moment. Then he took her hand. His hand was scraped and calloused and warm and exactly the right size for hers, which she had thought on the first night they held hands and had thought every time since.

"One hour," he said. "Then I want you in the cot."

"Two."

"One and a half."

"Done."

She checked the three patients in the alcove. She documented Soren. She found Atlas, who was on watch at the barn door with the binoculars and who did not need to be told to give his brother whatever room he needed; Atlas had been reading the barn's emotional weather with a accuracy that impressed her since day three. She found Leon, who was at the back of the barn with a look on his face that she recognized as processing, and crouched beside him.

"It's okay to feel this," she said.

He looked at her with the eyes that caught mana light, which were showing gold at the rim even in the barn's interior dimness, which was something she had been monitoring with the part of her medical attention that tracked anomalies without yet having a category for them. "Can you always save people?"

"No," she said.

"Not with enough medicine?"

"Not always." She sat on the floor beside him. "Some things move faster than medicine. Some things are too advanced when we find them."

"Was there something else to do?"

She thought about the question the way she thought about all questions he asked, which was: correctly. "There might have been, with different resources. I did everything I could with what I had."

"Is that enough?"

She was quiet for a moment. "It has to be. Because I'm what there is."

Leon considered this. Then he leaned against her side — not seeking comfort exactly, more like acknowledging proximity — and they sat together in the morning light that came through the barn's east-facing gap.

"I'm going to write his name down," Leon said.

"Yes," she said. "Please."

---

She went to the cot at nine and slept.

Rowan sat at the farm table twelve feet away because she slept better knowing he was close, and she had stopped pretending otherwise after the third night in the barn when she had asked him not to go to the watch rotation early and he had stayed and the sleep had been better. She would tell him he had been right about the one hour. She would tell him later, when she had the energy to grant him a victory without it feeling like a concession.

She woke at ten-twenty.

She lay still and assessed, the way she always assessed on waking: the quality of the light, the sounds of the barn, her own body — whether the fatigue was in the superficial layer that sleep replenished or in the deeper layer that accumulated past replenishment.

Superficial layer. She had gotten something.

She put her hand over her eyes.

She would not have noticed it if she had not been looking for the light differential — Marcus Vale's instrument logic had filtered through Ximena's conversations and Felicity's observations into the general knowledge of the barn in the past week, the knowledge that mana was visible in fracture light, that some people held it and some didn't, that holding it apparently did something to those people. She had been curious in the clinical way, noting the phenomenon without a category for it.

Her hands, in the fracture light coming through the east gap of the barn, glowed.

Not brightly. Not continuously. A warmth — in the palms specifically, a diffuse light that appeared when she consciously thought about the dead boy and the cold pack rotation and the thirty-one hours and the sound Priya had made.

It lasted three seconds.

When it faded, her hands were ordinary hands again.

She stared at them.

She pulled them back from the light, back into the shadow of her own body, and held them against her chest and felt the warmth dissipating and felt, with the part of her that was not clinician or wife or mother but simply the woman who had been trained to understand the body as a system with laws, something that she did not yet have a name for.

Not fear, exactly.

Something adjacent to fear, in the way that wonder was adjacent to fear: the response to a door opening in a wall you had been told was solid.

She lay still until the warmth was gone.

Then she sat up and called across the barn: "Rowan."

He turned from the table.

"I need to tell you something," she said. "Later. After the morning inventory."

He looked at her face. He was very good at her face. "You slept."

"One hour twenty minutes."

"I'll take it." He came and sat on the edge of the cot. "You all right?"

"Something happened," she said. "I don't know what it was. I'm not—" She stopped. The warmth was gone from her palms. She was looking at perfectly ordinary hands. "I'm not frightened. I want you to know that. But something happened."

He waited.

She would tell him. She would document it — in her medical notes, carefully, with the same precision she applied to dose calculations and infection tracking. She would ask Felicity if she had seen this in other survivors. She would ask Leon, when the time was right, whether her hands looked different to him in the fracture light.

She would not use the warmth yet. She was not ready to use something she did not understand, not on patients, not on the people she was responsible for keeping alive with instruments she trusted and limits she knew.

But she would note it.

She would keep the record.

She was a healer and the world had handed her something impossible and her job was to understand it before she used it, because using things she didn't understand was how people died in the care of doctors who meant well.

She looked at her hands in the ordinary shadow.

They were warm.

She chose to leave it there, for now — in the warmth, in the record, in the category labeled *observe further.*

That was enough for today.
